Year 7 Biology – Cells

B1.4 Movement of Substances

Understanding diffusion in cells, plants and everyday life

Use this sheet with your textbook page on “Movement of substances”.

Instructions: Read the textbook page carefully. Answer each question in full sentences using scientific words. When you have finished a question, you can click on Show answer to check your work or get help if you are stuck.

Section A – Quick Recall

Use the information to remind yourself of the key facts.

1
Name two substances that move into body cells from the blood.
Show answer

Glucose (from digested food) and oxygen both move into body cells from the blood.

2
Name one gas that moves out of body cells into the blood.
Show answer

Carbon dioxide moves out of body cells into the blood.

3
What is diffusion? Use the words particles, high concentration and low concentration in your answer.
Show answer

Diffusion is the movement of particles from an area where they are at a high concentration to an area where they are at a low concentration.

4
What does the word concentration mean?
Show answer

Concentration is the number of particles of a substance in a certain volume or space.

5
Which part of a plant cell fills with water to make the cell firm and rigid?
Show answer

The vacuole fills with water and makes the cell firm and rigid.

Section B – Using the Text

Think carefully about what is happening in each example.

6
In the burnt toast example, where are the toast-smell particles at a high concentration to begin with, and where do they spread to?
Show answer

At first, the toast-smell particles are at a high concentration in the kitchen. They spread along the hallway and then into the living room until they are spread out more evenly.

7
Why might you only be able to smell the burnt toast in the kitchen at first, but later you can smell it in the living room?
Show answer

At first most smell particles are still in the kitchen, so the concentration is highest there. Over time the particles diffuse through the air to other rooms, so you can smell the toast in the living room later.

8
Describe how oxygen gets from the air into your body cells and how carbon dioxide leaves your body.
Show answer

Oxygen enters the lungs when we breathe in and diffuses into the blood. The blood carries oxygen to body cells, where it diffuses into the cells. Carbon dioxide made in respiration diffuses out of cells into the blood, is carried back to the lungs, and then diffuses into the air and is breathed out.

9
Explain how oxygen and carbon dioxide move in and out of a leaf during gas exchange for photosynthesis and respiration.
Show answer

Oxygen from the air diffuses into the leaf through tiny holes on the underside and then into the plant’s cells for respiration. Carbon dioxide produced in respiration diffuses back out through the same holes. During photosynthesis, carbon dioxide diffuses into the leaf and oxygen made in photosynthesis diffuses out.

10
Plants need a constant supply of water. Describe how water gets from the soil into the plant using the root hair cells.
Show answer

Water molecules move by diffusion from the soil, where there is a high concentration of water, into the root hair cells, where the concentration of water is lower. From the root hair cells the water moves to other cells in the plant.

Section C – Thinking Questions

Use your understanding to explain what is happening in more detail.

11a
A room spray is used in one corner of a classroom. After a few minutes the whole room smells of the spray. Use the idea of diffusion to explain what has happened to the spray particles.
Show answer

The spray particles start in one corner at a high concentration. They diffuse through the air, spreading out from high concentration to low concentration, until the particles are spread all around the classroom.

11b
How is this similar to the burnt toast example in the textbook?
Show answer

Both examples show smell particles spreading out from where they are made (the toast or the spray) to the rest of the room or house by diffusion.

12
A student says: “Diffusion only happens in gases.” Explain why this statement is not correct, using an example from plants.
Show answer

The statement is not correct because diffusion also happens in liquids. For example, water diffuses from the soil into the root hair cells of a plant.

13a
Look at the pictures of healthy and wilted plants. What has happened to the water inside the cells of the wilted plant?
Show answer

Much of the water has left the cells of the wilted plant, so there is less water inside the cells.

13b
How does this affect the vacuole and the cell wall?
Show answer

The vacuole becomes smaller and does not push out strongly on the cell wall, so the cell is no longer firm.

13c
Why does this make the plant droop or wilt?
Show answer

Because the cells are no longer firm, the plant’s stems and leaves become floppy. The plant cannot hold itself upright, so it droops and wilts.

Section D – Stink-Bomb Alert!

Practise explaining science ideas clearly in writing.

14
The textbook asks you to write a reply to a toy shop that wants to understand how stink bombs work. In 3–4 sentences, explain simply how stink bombs work, using the key word diffusion.
Show answer

When a stink bomb breaks, smelly gas particles are released into the air at a high concentration near the bomb. The particles spread out by diffusion from this area of high concentration to areas of lower concentration. As the particles reach people’s noses around the room, they smell the stink. So stink bombs work because the smell particles diffuse through the air.
