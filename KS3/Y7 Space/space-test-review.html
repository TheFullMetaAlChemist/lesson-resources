Question 1 5 marks Solar System basics

Planets and the Sun

This question checks whether you know the order of the planets, how planets differ, and the force involved in forming the Sun.

1a. Label the planets in the correct order from the Sun.

Model answer

Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune

How the marks worked

  • 3 marks for all 8 planets in the correct order.
  • 2 marks for one mistake.
  • 1 mark for two mistakes.

Where marks are often lost

  • Mixing up Uranus and Neptune.
  • Missing out a planet.
  • Putting the planets in an order based on size instead of distance from the Sun.

1b. Give one other way the planets are different from each other.

Model answer

The planets are different sizes.

Other acceptable ideas included temperature, type of planet, number of moons, speed of orbit, or whether they have rings.

Examiner thinking

You only needed one valid difference. A simple, correct science fact was enough for the mark.

1c. Name the force that pulled dust and gas together to form the Sun.

Model answer

Gravity

Common mistake

Answers such as magnetism or pressure do not get the mark here. The key force is gravity.

Question 2 2 marks Apparent movement of the Sun

Why the Sun seems to move

This question is about what we observe in the sky and the real reason behind it.

2a. Which direction does the Sun appear to move?

Model answer

The arrow should go from East to West.

2b. Explain why the Sun appears to move across the sky.

Model answer

The Sun appears to move because the Earth is spinning on its axis.

What the answer needed

You needed the idea that the Earth rotates. The Sun is not actually moving across the sky around Earth each day.

Common mistake

Saying that the Sun goes around the Earth is not accepted.

Question 3 1 mark Seasons

Summer in the UK

You had to identify the point in Earth’s orbit where the UK is tilted towards the Sun.

Model answer

The correct position was the left-hand Earth on the diagram.

What this shows

The UK has summer when the Northern Hemisphere is tilted towards the Sun, so it gets more direct sunlight and longer days.

Question 4 2 marks Moon phases

Torch, globe and Moon model

This question used a practical model to test understanding of Moon phases.

4a. What does the torch represent?

Model answer

The Sun

4b. Which phase is shown when the ball is directly between the globe and the torch?

Model answer

New moon

Explanation

When the Moon is between Earth and the Sun, the lit side faces away from Earth, so it looks dark to us. That is a new moon.

Question 5 4 marks Solar eclipses

Total and partial solar eclipses

This was a longer answer. You had to use the diagram and explain what happens in the umbra and penumbra.

Model answer

A solar eclipse happens when the Moon moves between the Sun and the Earth and blocks the Sun’s light. The umbra is the darkest part of the shadow, where the Sun is completely blocked, so people there see a total solar eclipse. The penumbra is the lighter outer shadow, where only some of the Sun’s light is blocked, so people there see a partial solar eclipse.

On the diagram, the umbra should be the narrow central shadow behind the Moon, and the penumbra should be the wider, lighter shadow around it.

What the mark scheme was looking for

Moon between the Sun and Earth.
Umbra correctly identified or labelled.
No sunlight reaches the umbra, so this gives a total eclipse.
Penumbra correctly identified or labelled.
Only some sunlight reaches the penumbra, so this gives a partial eclipse.

How marks were lost

  • Only describing the eclipse without saying what the umbra and penumbra are.
  • Mixing up total and partial eclipse.
  • Not making clear that the Moon blocks the Sun’s light.
Question 6 2 marks Planet data

Comparing planet sizes

This question used data from a table and then asked you to think about Pluto.

6a. How many times bigger is Jupiter than Earth?

Model answer

About 11 times

135 800 ÷ 12 700 ≈ 10.7, which rounds to about 11.

6b. Suggest why Pluto is not a planet.

Model answer

Its diameter is too small.

Important note

This follows the wording of the test mark scheme. In more detail, Pluto is classed as a dwarf planet rather than a full planet.

Question 7 6 marks Seasons and sunlight

Why summer is hotter than winter

This question linked an experiment with the real reason for the seasons.

7a. State one thing Mark must keep the same.

Model answer

The amount of sand in each tray should be the same.

Other acceptable answers included keeping the sand level, keeping the distance from the lamp the same, or keeping the thermometer depth the same.

7b. Explain why Tray A gets hotter than Tray B.

Model answer

In Tray A the light is concentrated on a smaller area. In Tray B the light is spread out over a larger area, so the sand does not heat up as much.

7c. Why does the Sun hit the UK at an angle in winter?

Model answer

Because the Earth is tilted.

7d. Give another reason it is hotter in summer than in winter.

Model answer

The Sun is in the sky for longer in summer, so there are more hours of daylight and more time for the ground to warm up.

7e. Explain why people near the Equator do not have four seasons like the UK.

Model answer

Near the Equator, the Sun stays at a similar angle in the sky through the year, so the sunlight is quite direct most of the time. The length of the day and the temperature do not change very much, so places near the Equator do not usually have four clearly different seasons like the UK.

A good diagram would show Earth with a labelled equator and the Sun’s rays hitting near the Equator at a similar angle.

To get both marks

  • Include the idea that the Sun’s angle does not change much near the Equator.
  • Include the idea that temperature or daylight hours do not change much.
Question 8 1 mark Seeing objects in space

Why some objects can be seen with the naked eye

This question was about light.

Model answer

Objects that we can see with the naked eye emit enough light, or reflect enough light, to be seen from Earth. More distant objects may not emit or reflect enough light for our eyes to detect.

Common mistake

Only saying that something is “too far away” is not quite enough unless you link it to the amount of light reaching Earth.

Question 9 5 marks Graphs and patterns

Distance from the Sun and orbit time

This question combined graph skills, pattern spotting and scale model maths.

9a. Plot the last three planets and draw a smooth curve.

What a full-mark answer needed

  • Correctly plot Saturn: (1400, 29)
  • Correctly plot Uranus: (2900, 84)
  • Correctly plot Neptune: (4500, 165)
  • Draw a smooth curve through the pattern

How marks were lost

One mark could still be awarded for two correct points, but missing the curve or plotting inaccurate coordinates would lose marks.

9b. Describe the pattern shown on the graph.

Model answer

The further a planet is from the Sun, the longer it takes to orbit the Sun.

9c. Suggest an explanation for this pattern.

Model answer

Planets that are further away have a bigger orbit, so they have further to travel. Also, the Sun’s gravity is weaker further away.

What was accepted

You only needed one of those ideas for the mark.

9d. If Earth is 15 cm from the Sun in the model, how far away should Neptune be?

Model answer

450 cm

Earth is 150 million km from the Sun and Neptune is 4500 million km away. Neptune is 30 times farther than Earth, so 15 × 30 = 450 cm.
