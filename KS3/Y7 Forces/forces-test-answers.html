Question 1 — Forces on a swimmer

Marks: 7 Key ideas: contact/non-contact, balanced forces, drag

(a) Label the forces on the swimmer (3)

Model answer
• Upwards arrow: upthrust
• Downwards arrow: weight (or gravity)
• Backwards arrow: drag (water resistance / friction)
Explanation
Weight pulls down due to gravity. Upthrust is the upward push from the water. Drag acts opposite the swimmer’s motion because moving through water causes resistance.

(b) Which force is non-contact? (1)

Model answer
Weight
Explanation
Weight is caused by gravity, which acts at a distance (no touching needed).

(c) Circle the correct words (2)

(i) Balanced or unbalanced?
Unbalanced
(ii) Motion
Getting faster
Explanation
Unbalanced forces mean there is a resultant force, so the motion changes. Here, the swimmer speeds up (accelerates).

(d) Streamlined suits reduce which force? (1)

Model answer
Drag (water resistance / friction)
Explanation
A more streamlined shape cuts resistance from the water, reducing drag so the swimmer can move more efficiently.
Common mistakes (optional)
  • Labelling the backwards force as “upthrust” (upthrust is always upwards).
  • Saying drag is non-contact (it’s contact: particles collide/push on the object).
  • Choosing “balanced” but also “getting faster” (those contradict).

Question 2 — Investigating friction

Marks: 3 Key ideas: fair test, lubrication, uses of low friction

(a) Name one thing to keep the same (1)

Model answer (any one)
• Same block (mass/size/shape/material) or
• Same starting angle or
• Keep surfaces dry or
• Lift the slope at the same speed
Explanation
It must be a fair test: only the slope material should change, so you can compare friction properly.

(b) How to make friction even smaller (1)

Model answer
Add a lubricant (e.g. water or oil) to the slope / bottom of the block.
Explanation
A lubricant creates a thin layer that helps surfaces slide more easily, reducing friction.

(c) Another example where we want as little friction as possible (1)

Model answer (any sensible example)
• Skis on snow • Hockey puck on ice • Wheel bearings • Aeroplanes moving through air
Explanation
In these situations, low friction/resistance means less energy wasted and smoother or faster movement.
Quick check: what NOT to write

Don’t talk about decreasing air resistance here — the practical is about friction between the block and the slope.

Question 3 — Hooke’s Law

Marks: 6 Key ideas: extension, newton meter, proportionality

(a) What name do we give to how much the spring stretches? (1)

Model answer
Extension
Explanation
Extension means how much longer the spring gets compared with its original length.

(b) Equipment to measure weight of masses (1)

Model answer
Newton meter (force meter)
Explanation
Weight is a force, so you measure it in newtons with a force meter/newton meter.

(c) Results and Hooke’s Law

(i) If you double the force, you will ____ the extension (1)

Model answer
Double
Explanation
Hooke’s Law: extension is proportional to force (so doubling force doubles extension) until the spring stops behaving elastically.

(ii) Do the results agree with Hooke’s Law? How can you tell? (2)

Model answer
Yes. When the force doubles, the extension doubles (e.g. 1 N → 0.8 cm and 2 N → 1.6 cm).
Explanation
The table shows a consistent proportional pattern at first (1–4 N). That supports Hooke’s Law.
Notice at 5 N the extension is 4.8 cm, which is larger than the simple pattern would predict (it would be 4.0 cm). That suggests it may be starting to go beyond perfect proportionality at higher force.

(d) One safety precaution (1)

Model answer (any one)
• Wear goggles or
• Don’t drop masses on your feet or
• Stand back from hanging masses or
• Use a landing pad under masses
Explanation
Hanging masses can swing or fall, so you reduce risk of injury or broken equipment.

Question 4 — Drag forces and upthrust

Marks: 3 Key ideas: contact forces, direction of forces, particle model

(a) One similarity and one difference (2)

Model answer
Similarity: Both are contact forces and can act in fluids (air/water).

Difference: Upthrust always acts upwards, but drag acts opposite to motion (and only matters when moving).
Explanation
Upthrust is due to pressure differences in a fluid. Drag is due to collisions/friction with fluid particles when an object moves.

(b) Use the particle model to explain drag (1)

Model answer
As the object moves, it pushes air/water particles out of the way and they push back on the object, creating a force that opposes motion.
Explanation
More collisions (e.g. higher speed or denser fluid) means a larger drag force.

Question 5 — NASA’s Curiosity Rover on Mars

Marks: 12 Key ideas: W = m×g, rearranging, drag and particles

(a) Complete the word equation for weight (2)

Model answer
Weight (N) = mass (kg) × gravitational field strength (N/kg)
Explanation
Mass stays the same wherever you are, but weight changes because gravitational field strength changes.

(b) Calculate the gravitational field strength on Mars (3)

Model method + answer
g = W / m g = 3326 / 899 g = 3.7 N/kg
Answer: 3.7 N/kg
Explanation
You rearrange W = m × g to get g = W ÷ m, then substitute the Mars weight and the mass.

(c) Parachute and forces

(i) What happened to the two forces when the parachute opened, and why? (4)

Model answer
• The weight stayed the same because the rover’s mass did not change (and g didn’t suddenly change).
• The drag force increased because the parachute increased the surface area.
Explanation
Bigger area → more collisions with air particles → larger drag force → rover slows down.

(ii) Mars’ atmosphere is thinner. Is drag bigger, smaller or the same as on Earth? Explain using particles. (3)

Model answer
Smaller. There are fewer particles in Mars’ atmosphere, so there are fewer collisions/pushes on the rover, giving a smaller drag force.
Explanation
Drag comes from the rover pushing particles out of the way and particles pushing back. Fewer particles means less pushing back overall.
Mini “exam tip”

When calculating g, always write the rearranged equation first (g = W ÷ m) and then substitute numbers — it helps avoid mixing up mass and weight.
