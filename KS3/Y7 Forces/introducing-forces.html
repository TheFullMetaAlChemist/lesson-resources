Introducing Forces

1. What Is a Force?

Concept: A force is a push or a pull that acts on an object. Forces can be caused by contact (like pushing a swing) or from a distance (like gravity pulling you down). In space, a rocket lifts off because its engines exert a huge force pushing it upward, overcoming gravity.

Forces can cause objects to start moving, stop moving, speed up, slow down, or change direction. They can also change an object’s shape.

3. Describing Forces

We often describe forces by their size (measured in newtons, N) and the direction in which they act. For example, a 5 N force acting upwards on a book can balance a 5 N weight pulling down.

Sometimes we use arrows to show forces: the length of the arrow can represent how big the force is, and the arrow points in the direction of the force.

Remember: If forces are equal in size and opposite in direction, they can balance each other out.

5. Different Types of Forces

We can group forces into different types:

  • Contact forces: friction, air resistance, tension, normal contact force (the push from a surface), etc.
  • Non-contact forces: gravity, magnetism, and electrostatic forces, which act at a distance.

For instance, gravity is always pulling you down, while the floor pushes you up with a contact force. A magnet can pull a piece of metal without touching it—that’s a non-contact force.

7. Interaction Pairs

Forces come in pairs (Newton’s Third Law): when one object exerts a force on another, the second object exerts an equal and opposite force back.

For example, if you push against the wall with 10 N, the wall pushes back on you with 10 N in the opposite direction. These forces act on different objects but are equal in size.

In a playground, if two students hang from each other’s arms, each student exerts a force on the other—and each student experiences an equal and opposite pull in return.
