Task 1: Match Up Beginning

In this task, you need to match each chemistry term with its correct definition. This helps you understand the key vocabulary related to metals and water reactions.

Hint

Think about what we learned in class about the reactivity series and what happens when certain metals react with water.

When metals react with water, they typically produce two things: a metal compound and a gas. Can you remember which gas bubbles up during these reactions?

Real-World Example

If you've ever seen a fizzy antacid tablet dropped in water, you're actually witnessing a chemical reaction that produces gas bubbles - similar to what happens when reactive metals are placed in water!

Another example is the emergency flares that some car kits include - these often contain reactive metals like magnesium that can burn even when wet.

Did You Know?

The alkali metals (like potassium and sodium) are so reactive with water that they have to be stored under oil to prevent them from reacting with moisture in the air!

When sodium reacts with water, the hydrogen gas produced can actually ignite due to the heat of the reaction - creating a small flame on the water's surface!

Key Terms:

  • Reactivity series - This is like a league table that ranks metals from most reactive to least reactive. It helps us predict how metals will behave in different situations.
  • Metal hydroxide - This is a compound that forms when a metal reacts with water. It has the metal plus OH (hydroxide). For example, sodium + water produces sodium hydroxide (NaOH).
  • Hydrogen - This is a gas that's produced when metals react with water. It's the lightest element and highly flammable.
  • Potassium - This is a very reactive metal from Group 1 of the periodic table. It reacts vigorously with water and can even catch fire during the reaction.

Task 2: Fill in the Blanks Working Towards

This task asks you to complete sentences using the correct scientific terms about metal reactions with water. This helps check your understanding of key concepts.

Hint

Look carefully at the words in the box. Think about which word makes the most sense in each blank space.

Remember what we observed in the demonstrations of metals reacting with water - which ones reacted quickly (vigorously) and which ones didn't react at all?

Real-World Example

Did you know that the Statue of Liberty is covered in copper? Despite being exposed to rain for over 130 years, it hasn't dissolved or reacted with the water. This is because copper is very unreactive with water, making it perfect for water pipes and outdoor structures.

On the other hand, if a piece of sodium fell into the ocean, it would react violently, producing hydrogen gas and sodium hydroxide (which is used in drain cleaners!).

Did You Know?

The reactivity of metals with water was discovered partly by accident! In 1807, Sir Humphry Davy isolated potassium by using electricity to separate it from its compounds. When he accidentally dropped some in water, he was surprised by the violent reaction - the metal caught fire and skated across the water's surface!

Metal + Water Reaction:

  • Hydrogen - A gas produced when metals react with water. It's colorless and highly flammable.
  • Hydroxide - Part of the compound formed when metals react with water (e.g., sodium hydroxide). Has the chemical formula OH-.
  • Steam - Water in its gas state, created by heating water above 100°C.
  • Unreactive - Describes a substance that does not react or reacts very slowly with other substances.
  • Vigorously - Describes a reaction that happens quickly and with a lot of energy, often producing bubbles, heat, or light.
  • Reactivity series - A list showing how reactive different metals are, arranged from most to least reactive.
  • Bubbles - These form when a gas is produced in a reaction. In metal-water reactions, the bubbles contain hydrogen gas.

Task 3: Word Equations Working Towards

In this task, you need to write word equations for the reactions of different metals with water. This helps you understand the pattern of chemical reactions.

Hint

All these reactions follow a similar pattern:

Metal + Water → Metal Hydroxide + Hydrogen

Just replace "Metal" with the specific metal name in each equation (potassium, calcium, etc.).

Real-World Example

Scientists use the reaction between aluminum and water to propel some underwater vehicles! Aluminum reacts with water to produce hydrogen gas, which can be used as fuel.

Another real-world application: hydrogen produced from metal-water reactions can be used in fuel cells to generate electricity with zero pollution - only water is produced as a by-product!

Did You Know?

The hydrogen gas produced when metals react with water is the same element that makes up about 75% of the universe! It's the fuel that powers our sun through nuclear fusion.

The extremely reactive metal caesium reacts so violently with water that it can cause an explosion! Scientists have to handle it with extreme care.

The pattern for the reactions in this task is:

  1. For reactions with liquid water: Metal + Water → Metal Hydroxide + Hydrogen
    • Example: Potassium + Water → Potassium Hydroxide + Hydrogen
    • Example: Calcium + Water → Calcium Hydroxide + Hydrogen
  2. For reactions with steam (water vapor): Metal + Steam → Metal Oxide + Hydrogen
    • Example: Magnesium + Steam → Magnesium Oxide + Hydrogen
    • Example: Iron + Steam → Iron Oxide + Hydrogen

Notice that with steam, we get a metal oxide (not a hydroxide). This is because at high temperatures, metal hydroxides break down to form metal oxides.

Task 4: Ordering Reactivity Expected

Here you need to place metals in order from most reactive with water to least reactive. This helps you understand the reactivity series concept.

Hint

Think about which metals react with cold water and which need steam to react.

The more reactive a metal is, the more vigorously it reacts with water.

Group 1 metals (alkali metals like potassium and sodium) are the most reactive with water.

Real-World Example

Engineers use the reactivity series when designing water pipes. They choose metals like copper that are low on the reactivity series because they won't react with the water flowing through them.

In contrast, in naval engineering, sacrificial anodes made of more reactive metals like zinc are attached to ship hulls to protect them from corrosion in seawater - the zinc corrodes instead of the hull!

Did You Know?

Gold is so unreactive that gold artifacts from ancient civilizations (over 3000 years old) still look shiny today! This is why gold is used for jewelry and currency.

If you look at the periodic table, metals generally become more reactive as you move down Group 1 and Group 2. This is because the outer electrons are farther from the nucleus and more easily given away in reactions.

Metal Reactivity with Water:

  • Potassium - Reacts violently with cold water, may catch fire, floats and moves around rapidly
  • Sodium - Reacts vigorously with cold water, melts into a silvery ball that moves around
  • Calcium - Reacts readily with cold water, producing bubbles of hydrogen
  • Iron - Does not react with cold water, but reacts slowly with steam to form iron oxide
  • Copper - Does not react with water or steam at all

For this task, you need to decide if statements about metal reactions with water are true or false. This tests your deeper understanding of the concepts.

Hint

Think about what we've learned about the reactivity series and which metals react with water.

Remember that some metals only react with steam (hot water vapor), not with cold water.

If a statement is false, make sure you can explain why it's incorrect and what the correct statement would be.

Real-World Example

The fact that some metals react with water and others don't is important in everyday life. Water taps are often made of brass (a copper alloy) because it doesn't react with water. If they were made of sodium, they would burst into flames when you turned them on!

Ancient shipwrecks made of iron or steel rust away underwater over time because these metals can react with water, while gold coins found in the same shipwrecks remain intact for centuries.

Did You Know?

The Titanic is being slowly "eaten" at the bottom of the Atlantic Ocean because the iron in its hull reacts with dissolved oxygen in the water, forming rust. Scientists estimate it will completely disappear within 100-200 years!

During the Hindenburg disaster in 1937, it was actually the reaction of aluminum components with the atmosphere (after the initial hydrogen fire) that caused much of the destruction - a demonstration of metal reactivity with oxygen.

Key Facts About Metals and Water:

  • NOT all metals react with cold water. Only the most reactive metals (like potassium, sodium, and calcium) react with cold water.
  • Copper does NOT react with steam. It's too unreactive to react with water in any form.
  • Sodium DOES produce hydrogen gas when it reacts with water. The reaction produces sodium hydroxide and hydrogen gas.
  • The reactivity series is the same for reactions with water and oxygen. Metals that are reactive with one tend to be reactive with the other.
  • Magnesium does NOT react vigorously with cold water. It only reacts with steam (water vapor at high temperature).

Example: Prediction for Calcium in Water

Prediction: The calcium will sink to the bottom of the water and start producing bubbles of hydrogen gas. The reaction will be slower than sodium but still clearly visible.

Explanation: Calcium is less reactive than sodium but still reactive enough to react with cold water. The reaction produces calcium hydroxide and hydrogen gas. The bubbles are the hydrogen gas being released. The reaction is exothermic but not enough to make the metal melt or catch fire.

Thinking Process for Your Predictions:

  1. Identify where the metal is in the reactivity series
  2. Determine if it reacts with cold water, only with steam, or not at all
  3. Think about specific observations that would be seen (bubbles, movement, heat, etc.)
  4. Consider the products formed (metal hydroxide/oxide and hydrogen)
  5. Explain why these reactions occur based on the metal's properties

Task 8: Experimental Design Exceeding

This task asks you to design an experiment to determine where nickel belongs in the reactivity series. This tests your scientific thinking and practical knowledge.

Hint

To design your experiment, think about:

  • What equipment will you need to safely test reactions with both cold water and steam?
  • What safety precautions are essential when working with reactive metals and steam?
  • What observations will help you determine nickel's reactivity compared to other metals?
  • How will you make your experiment fair and reliable?

Real-World Example

The experimental process you're designing is similar to how scientists at NASA test materials for space missions. Before sending materials into space, they need to know exactly how they'll react to different conditions.

Similarly, when archaeologists discover ancient metal artifacts, they often conduct reactivity tests to identify the metals without damaging the artifacts - just like you're designing tests for nickel.

Did You Know?

Nickel is actually used in many common coins, including the U.S. nickel and the UK 5p and 10p coins!

Nickel was once considered a "bad luck" metal by miners because its ore looked like copper ore but didn't produce copper when processed - they didn't realize it was a valuable metal in its own right!

Experimental Design Components:

  1. Equipment:
    • Test tubes and test tube rack
    • Beakers for water
    • Bunsen burner or heating device for producing steam
    • Tongs for handling metals
    • Nickel samples and samples of metals with known reactivity
    • Safety equipment: goggles, heat-resistant gloves, lab coat
  2. Procedure:
    • Test 1: Place nickel in cold water and observe for several minutes
    • Test 2: Heat water to produce steam and expose nickel to the steam
    • Test 3: Compare nickel's reactions to those of other metals (e.g., magnesium, iron)
  3. Observations: Look for bubbles, color changes, speed of reaction
  4. Data collection: Create a table to record observations systematically
  5. Safety: Wear eye protection, handle hot equipment with care, ensure proper ventilation
  6. Analysis: Compare nickel's behavior to metals of known reactivity to place it appropriately in the series

Task 9: Problem Solving Excelling

In this advanced task, you need to analyze data about metal reactivity to place tin in the reactivity series. This tests your analytical thinking and ability to interpret data.

Hint

Look carefully at the pattern of reactions in the table.

Compare tin's reaction with dilute acid to the reactions of other metals.

Consider how a metal's reaction with acid relates to its position in the reactivity series.

Remember that more reactive metals react more vigorously with acids.

Real-World Example

Materials scientists use reactivity data like this when developing protective coatings for metals. Tin is often used as a coating on steel food cans because it's less reactive than iron and protects the can from rusting.

Archaeologists use their knowledge of the reactivity series to help preserve ancient metal artifacts. By understanding how different metals react with air and water, they can create the right conditions to prevent deterioration.

Did You Know?

Tin has been used by humans for over 5,000 years! Bronze, one of the first metal alloys made by humans, is a mixture of copper and tin.

Tin undergoes a strange transformation at very cold temperatures (below 13.2°C) called "tin pest" - it slowly crumbles into a gray powder! This is thought to have contributed to the failure of Napoleon's Russian campaign when the tin buttons on soldiers' uniforms crumbled in the winter cold.

Strategy for Analysis:

  1. Look at the data for how each metal reacts with acid, water, and steam.
  2. Arrange the metals in order of reactivity based on their reactions with acid:
    • Magnesium (bubbles vigorously)
    • Zinc (bubbles steadily)
    • Iron (bubbles slowly)
    • Tin (bubbles slowly)
    • Lead (very slow reaction)
    • Copper (no reaction)
  3. Note that tin bubbles slowly in acid - similar to iron but faster than lead.
  4. Based on this, tin would likely be placed between iron and lead in the reactivity series.
  5. Check if this makes sense based on the periodic table - tin (Sn) is below lead (Pb) in Group 14, which generally supports this placement.

Remember: In the reactivity series, the general order is:

Magnesium > Zinc > Iron > Tin > Lead > Copper

But always use the specific evidence provided in your task to make your final decision.

Did You Know?

The Nobel Prize medal is made of gold because gold is so unreactive it won't tarnish over time - ensuring the medal stays shiny for generations!

The reactivity of aluminum was once considered so valuable that aluminum was more expensive than gold in the 1800s! Napoleon III even had a set of aluminum cutlery that was only used for the most honored guests, while everyone else had to use gold cutlery.
