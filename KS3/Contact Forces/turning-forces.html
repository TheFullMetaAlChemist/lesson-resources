Turning Forces

1. Introduction to Turning Forces

Key Idea: A turning force, or moment, occurs when a force acts at a distance from a pivot (also called a fulcrum). Think of a seesaw or a door hinge: the pivot is where it rotates, and the force acts at some distance away.

Real-world examples include:

  • Seesaw: Children on opposite ends applying forces around the pivot in the center.
  • Door handle: You push or pull the handle at the edge, turning the door on its hinges.
  • Spanner/wrench: You apply force at the end to turn a nut or bolt.

2. Understanding the Pivot and the Moment

The pivot is the point about which something turns. The moment (turning effect) depends on:

  • The size of the force.
  • The perpendicular distance from the pivot to where the force is applied.

Mathematically: Moment = Force × Distance from Pivot

3. The Moment Equation – Explanation & Examples

The equation for a moment is:

Moment (Nm) = Force (N) × Perpendicular distance from pivot (m)

Example 1: If you apply a 10 N force at a distance of 0.5 m from the pivot, the moment is: 10 N × 0.5 m = 5 Nm.

Example 2: Using a spanner with a 20 N force applied 0.25 m from the nut: 20 N × 0.25 m = 5 Nm.

5. The Law of Moments

The law of moments states that for an object in equilibrium (not turning):

Sum of clockwise moments = Sum of anticlockwise moments

This means if you have a seesaw balanced, the total turning effect on one side (clockwise) is equal to the total turning effect on the other side (anticlockwise).

Example: If a 30 N child sits 2 m from the pivot on one side (moment = 60 Nm), you can balance them by placing a 20 N child on the other side at 3 m from the pivot (moment = 60 Nm). Both sides are 60 Nm, so the seesaw is balanced.



6. Practice – Applying the Law of Moments

Law of Moments (Equilibrium): Clockwise Moment = Anticlockwise Moment

Worked Example:
A 10 N weight is 3 m to the left of the pivot (30 Nm clockwise). To balance, you need 30 Nm anticlockwise on the right. If the distance on the right is 2 m, the force needed is 30 ÷ 2 = 15 N.

7. Centre of Gravity / Centre of Mass

The centre of gravity (or centre of mass) of an object is the point where we can consider the whole weight of the object to act. For a uniform object (like a regular shape with mass evenly distributed), this is at its geometric center.

Real-world example: If you balance a ruler on your finger at its midpoint, it is because the centre of gravity is at the midpoint, so moments on each side are equal.
