Stress on Solids (N/m²)

1. Introduction to Stress on Solids

Key Idea: When a force acts on an area of a solid surface, we say the object experiences stress. Stress tells us how concentrated the force is over that area.

Real-world examples include:

  • Standing on soft ground: Your weight spreads over the area of your feet. Standing on tiptoes means a smaller area, leading to higher stress.
  • Snowshoes: They increase the area so the stress on the snow is lower, preventing you from sinking.
  • Nails and screws: A sharp tip has a tiny area, so stress is very high, helping them pierce materials.

2. What is Stress?

Definition: Stress is the force applied per unit area.

Symbolically: Stress = Force / Area

Units of stress: N/m2

3. How Stress is Calculated

The equation for stress is:

Stress (N/m2) = Force (N) ÷ Area (m2)

Example 1: If a 600 N person stands on one foot of area 0.03 m2, the stress is 600 ÷ 0.03 = 20,000 N/m2.



4. Practice – Calculating Stress

Equation: Stress = Force ÷ Area

5. How Area Affects Stress

Key Concept: For the same force, reducing the contact area increases stress; increasing the contact area decreases stress.

Examples:

  • High heels vs. flat shoes: High heels have a small area in contact with the ground, creating a very high stress.
  • Tank tracks: Tanks have wide tracks to spread their massive weight over a large area, reducing stress on the ground.


6. Practice – The Effect of Area

Remember: Stress = Force ÷ Area.

If the force stays the same, a smaller area means a larger stress.

Question 1: You have a 500 N force. Which area gives the higher stress, 0.01 m2 or 0.05 m2?

Question 2: If you double the area and keep the same force, what happens to the stress?

Question 3: A 600 N load on 0.03 m2 gives 20,000 N/m2. If the same 600 N acts on 0.06 m2, what is the new stress?




7. Rearranging the Equation to Find Force

You can also rearrange Stress = Force ÷ Area to find Force or Area:

  • Force = Stress × Area
  • Area = Force ÷ Stress

Example: If the stress on a metal beam is 15,000 N/m2 and the cross-sectional area is 0.2 m2, the force is 15,000 × 0.2 = 3,000 N.

9. Practical Activity: Stress from Your Footprint

Objective: Measure the area of your foot (or shoe) and use your weight to calculate the stress you exert on the floor.

  1. Trace around your shoe (or foot) on graph paper.
  2. Count the squares to estimate the area in cm2. Convert to m2 (1 m2 = 10,000 cm2).
  3. Use your weight in newtons (weight in kg × 9.8 to approximate in N).
  4. Calculate the stress when standing on one foot, then on two feet.
  5. Repeat for standing on tiptoes to see how the smaller contact area changes the stress.
