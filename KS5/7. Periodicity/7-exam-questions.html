Medium Q1 ‚Äì Period 2 First Ionisation Energies & p-Block
Total: 12 marks

State and explain the general trend in first ionisation energy across Period 2 of the Periodic Table.

Across Period 2, the first ionisation energy generally increases from Li to Ne.

  • The nuclear charge increases as the number of protons increases.
  • Electrons are added to the same principal energy level (n = 2), so shielding remains similar.
  • The atomic radius decreases, so outer electrons are closer to the nucleus.
  • Therefore, the electrostatic attraction between the nucleus and the outer electrons increases, so more energy is required to remove an electron.

Boron and one other Period 2 element deviate from this trend. Identify this element and explain why both deviate.

The other element is oxygen.

  • Boron: Its outer electron is in a 2p sub-shell, whereas beryllium‚Äôs outer electrons are in the 2s sub-shell. The 2p sub-shell is higher in energy and slightly more shielded than 2s, so the 2p electron is easier to remove and boron‚Äôs first ionisation energy is lower than expected.
  • Oxygen: Nitrogen has a half-filled 2p sub-shell with three unpaired 2p electrons. Oxygen has four 2p electrons, so one orbital contains a pair of electrons. The extra electron‚Äìelectron repulsion in the paired orbital makes one electron easier to remove, so oxygen‚Äôs first ionisation energy is lower than expected compared with nitrogen.

Its full electron configuration is: 1s¬≤ 2s¬≤ 2p¬≥.

Identify the Period 3 element that has the lowest melting point and explain why, with reference to bonding and structure.

The Period 3 element with the lowest melting point is argon.

  • Argon exists as monoatomic Ar atoms with only very weak London (dispersion) forces between them.
  • These weak forces require very little energy to overcome.

Medium Q4 ‚Äì Graphite vs Diamond & Comparison with Metals
Total: 16 marks

Describe the structure and bonding of graphite and explain why it has such a high melting point.

  • Graphite has a giant covalent structure made of layers of carbon atoms.
  • Each carbon is covalently bonded to three other carbons in hexagonal sheets.
  • The fourth valence electron is delocalised and can move between the layers.
  • There are many strong covalent bonds within each layer that must be broken to melt graphite.
  • Breaking these bonds requires a very large amount of energy, so graphite has a very high melting point.

Graphite conducts electricity but diamond does not. Explain this difference.

  • In graphite each carbon uses three outer electrons for bonding, leaving one delocalised electron per atom that can move freely through the structure, so graphite can conduct electricity.
  • In diamond each carbon uses all four outer electrons to form covalent bonds, so there are no delocalised electrons or mobile ions.
  • Therefore, diamond cannot conduct electricity.

Give one other difference in the properties of graphite and diamond.

Graphite is soft and slippery, whereas diamond is extremely hard.

Graphite is soft and used as a lubricant, whereas diamond is hard and used in cutting tools. Explain this difference with reference to structure and bonding.

  • Graphite: Carbon atoms form layers held together by weak London forces. These weak forces allow the layers to slide over each other, so graphite is soft and can act as a lubricant.
  • Diamond: Each carbon atom forms four strong covalent bonds in a 3D tetrahedral network.
  • This creates a rigid, tightly bonded lattice, making diamond very hard.
  • A large force is needed to break these covalent bonds, so diamond can cut other materials.

Both graphite and lithium are good conductors of electricity and have high boiling points. Explain these similarities in terms of bonding and structure.

  • Electrical conductivity:
    • In lithium, delocalised electrons move through a giant metallic lattice of Li‚Å∫ ions.
    • In graphite, delocalised electrons move along the carbon layers.
    • In both, these delocalised electrons can carry charge, so both conduct electricity.
  • High boiling points:
    • Lithium has strong metallic bonds between Li‚Å∫ ions and delocalised electrons.
    • Graphite has strong covalent bonds within its carbon layers.
    • Breaking these strong bonds requires a large amount of energy, giving high boiling points for both substances.

The table shows the properties of Li, C (graphite) and O‚ÇÇ (boiling points and conductivity). Explain these properties in terms of bonding and structure.

Lithium (Li):

  • Structure & bonding: Lithium has a giant metallic structure of Li‚Å∫ ions in a lattice surrounded by a sea of delocalised electrons.
