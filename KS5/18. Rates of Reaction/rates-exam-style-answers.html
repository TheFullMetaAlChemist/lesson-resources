Q1. Orders & k from a rate law

(a) Orders: X = 2, Y = 1, Z = 0.
(b) Overall order = 3.
(c) k = 18.6 dm⁶ mol⁻² s⁻¹ (from rate ÷ ( [X]²[Y] ) at the given point).
(d) Double [X] (others fixed) → rate ×4.
Why

Z not in the rate law → zero order. Doubling X gives ×4 change (order 2); doubling Y gives ×2 (order 1). Overall order is the sum (3). Units for order 3 give dm⁶ mol⁻² s⁻¹. Rate ∝ [X]², so doubling X → 2² = 4.

Tips

Units pattern (overall order n): dm³⁽ⁿ⁻¹⁾ mol¹⁻ⁿ s⁻¹. For “what if we double…”, raise the factor to the order.

Q2. Bromate(V)/bromide in acid (orders & rate law)

(a) Order in H⁺: 2.
(b) If first order in Br⁻, [Br⁻] vs t is a decaying exponential (constant half-life).
(c) Overall order 4 with first order in Br⁻ and H⁺² → order in BrO₃⁻ is 1; rate = k[Br⁻][BrO₃⁻][H⁺]².
(d) k = rate ÷ ( [Br⁻][BrO₃⁻][H⁺]² ); units = dm⁹ mol⁻³ s⁻¹.
Why

Rate–[H⁺] curve steepens → second order. To total 4: 1 (Br⁻) + 1 (BrO₃⁻) + 2 (H⁺) = 4. Units: (mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹) ÷ (mol dm⁻³)⁴ → dm⁹ mol⁻³ s⁻¹.

Tips

Straight line through origin in rate–[X] → first order. Curving upward → second order.

Q3. Decomposition of H₂O₂ with MnO₂

(a) Half-life: time for a reactant’s concentration to fall to half its current value.
(b) First-order in H₂O₂; rate = k[H₂O₂].
(c) Rate at 100 s: magnitude of tangent slope at t=100 s. Then k = rate / [H₂O₂] at that instant, or k = ln2 / t½.
(d) Halving initial [H₂O₂] does not change t½ (constant for first-order).
Why

Equal half-life steps (c₀ → c₀/2 → c₀/4 …) diagnose first order. For first order, ln[H₂O₂] vs t is a straight line with slope −k. t½ = ln2/k is independent of concentration.

Tips

Use k = ln2/t½ to avoid tangent errors when possible.

Q4. Initial rates; mechanism & monitoring

(a) Initial rate = instantaneous rate at t=0 (tangent at t=0).
(b) Overall order 4 implies multi-step (one-step 4-body collision is improbable).
(c) Proposed RDS 2Br⁻+BrO₃⁻+2H⁺ → … would be 5th order; inconsistent with observed 4th order → not correct.
(d) Measure rate by colorimetry (follow Br₂ colour increase) and take initial slope.
Why

High molecularity steps are extremely unlikely. RDS orders mirror coefficients of that step, not the overall equation. Colorimetry tracks [Br₂] rise in real time for an initial slope.

Tips

Initial-rate methods avoid reverse reactions and significant concentration changes.

Q5. Arrhenius skills

(a) A: pre-exponential (units of k). Eₐ: activation energy (J mol⁻¹). T: K.
(b) A = k × e^(-Eₐ/RT).
(c) y=ln k, x=1/T, slope m=−Eₐ/R, intercept c=ln A.
(d) Gradient negative; Eₐ = (−m)R → tens to low hundreds of kJ mol⁻¹.
Why

ln k = ln A − Eₐ(1/RT) → straight line in ln k vs 1/T with slope −Eₐ/R and intercept ln A.

Tips

Keep Eₐ in J while calculating (R = 8.314 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹); convert to kJ at the end.

Q6. Deduce orders from an initial-rates table; rate law; units of k

(a) Order in A = 1; order in B = 2 (C is zero order if stated).
(b) rate = k[A][B]².
(c) Units of k (overall order 3): dm⁶ mol⁻² s⁻¹.
Why

Rate doubles when [A] doubles → first order. Rate ×4 when [B] doubles → second order. Units from (mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹) ÷ (mol dm⁻³)³.

Tips

Pick pairs where only one species changes; otherwise combine ratios carefully.

Q7. Use rate law to find k; predict new rate

(a) k = rate ÷ ([A][B]²) using any row; units from Q6(c).
(b) New rate = old rate × (f_A)¹ × (f_B)² or compute directly with k.
Why

Orders give the exponents for proportional changes; using a consistent k from any row validates the deduced orders.

Tips

Always write a units line when you compute k to catch slips.

Q8. Concentration–time graph: identify order & determine k

(a) First order (constant half-life).
(b) k = ln2/t½ (half-life method) or k = −(slope of ln[reactant] vs t).
Why

Equal t½ intervals diagnose first order; linear ln[reactant] vs t with negative slope equals −k.

Tips

Zero order → [A] vs t is a straight line down; second order → 1/[A] vs t is linear up.

Q9. Arrhenius from two temperatures

(a) Eₐ = R × { ln(k₁/k₂) ÷ (1/T₂ − 1/T₁) } (J mol⁻¹).
(b) Find A from one (k,T) pair then use ln k = ln A − Eₐ/(RT), or use the two-point form again to get k at the new T.
Why

Start from ln(k₂/k₁) = −Eₐ/R × (1/T₂ − 1/T₁). Rearranging gives Eₐ; then use Arrhenius to get new k.

Tips

Keep temperatures in K and R = 8.314 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹; convert the final Eₐ to kJ mol⁻¹ if requested.

Q10. Mechanism & RDS reasoning

(a) Not a single step: observed overall order (≥3) implies an improbably high-molecularity collision.
(b) A consistent scheme for rate = k[A][B]² is: fast A + B <-> I; slow I + B -> products (so rate ∝ [A][B]²).
Why

Elementary RDS orders equal stoichiometric coefficients of that step; pre-equilibria can make intermediates proportional to reactant concentrations.

Tips

Match the observed orders by choosing the slow step’s molecularity and any preceding equilibria appropriately.

Q1. Chlorination of propanone in acid (orders, k, pH change, temperature)

(a) Orders: propanone = 1, H⁺ = 1, Cl₂ = 0.
(b) rate = k[propanone][H⁺]; k ≈ 4.8×10⁻⁵ dm³ mol⁻¹ s⁻¹.
(c) pH 2 → 1 makes [H⁺] ×10; first order in H⁺ → rate ×10.
(d) Lower T → k decreases (Arrhenius) → rate decreases.
Why

Initial-rates comparisons show first order in propanone and H⁺, zero in Cl₂. Arrhenius: k = A e^(−Eₐ/RT); decreasing T reduces k, hence rate.

Tips

Every unit drop in pH multiplies [H⁺] by 10; apply the order exponent.

Q2. Clock method with I⁻/H₂O₂/H⁺ and thiosulfate–starch

(a) Assumption: the amount of product at the colour change is the same in each run → 1/t ∝ initial rate.
(b) S₂O₃²⁻ removes I₂ as it forms; when it’s used up, a fixed excess of I₂ complexes with starch → sudden colour; measure the time.
(c) Show first order in I⁻ by varying [I⁻] at constant [S₂O₈²⁻]; first order in S₂O₈²⁻ by varying [S₂O₈²⁻] at constant [I⁻]; in each case 1/t scales linearly with the varied concentration.
Why

The clock holds I₂ near zero until thiosulfate is consumed, giving a fixed-amount endpoint so time is inversely proportional to initial rate.

Tips

Choose thiosulfate so times are in tens of seconds for better precision.

Q3. Mixed kinetics: k vs T; mechanism choice; ethanal dimerisation

(a) k increases with T exponentially (Arrhenius). Changing concentration does not change k.
(b) Choose the step whose molecularity matches the observed orders; for rate ∝ [X][Y], the RDS must be bimolecular in X and Y (or produced via a pre-equilibrium giving [intermediate] ∝ [X] then slow with Y).
(c)(i) For CH₃CHO dimerisation at 298 K with base: k = 7.20×10⁻² dm³ mol⁻¹ s⁻¹ (from rate ÷ ([CH₃CHO][OH⁻])).
(c)(ii) RDS is the nucleophilic addition step consistent with rate ∝ [CH₃CHO][OH⁻]; acid–base steps are fast equilibria.
Why

Arrhenius explains T-dependence of k. The observed orders must arise from the slow step’s stoichiometry and any pre-equilibria. The given numbers yield k = 2.7×10⁻⁴ ÷ (0.15×0.025) = 7.20×10⁻².

Tips

Whenever asked “does changing [A] change k?” → No; only T (and catalyst) affects k.

Q4. O₃ + C₂H₄ -> 2CH₂O + ½O₂ (rate change)

First order in O₃ and in C₂H₄. If initial CH₂O rate is 2.0×10⁻¹¹ and we double [O₃] and triple [C₂H₄], new CH₂O rate = 2.0×10⁻¹¹ × (2×3) = 1.2×10⁻¹⁰. O₂ forms at half that rate by stoichiometry → 6.0×10⁻¹¹ mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹.

Why

rate ∝ [O₃]¹[C₂H₄]¹. Compare product formation rates using their stoichiometric coefficients (CH₂O:O₂ = 2:½ = 4:1).

Tips

When the question asks for a different species’ rate, scale by the stoichiometric ratio.

Q5. Methanoic acid + bromine kinetics; I₂–propanone orders

(a) Monitor [Br₂] by colorimetry (absorbance vs time). Alternative: quench & titrate with thiosulfate, or conductivity. Excess HCOOH keeps [HCOOH] ~constant → pseudo-zero order in HCOOH.
(b) From [Br₂] (or A) vs t: initial rate = tangent at t=0. Then k = rate ÷ (product of concentrations to their orders); if ln[Br₂] vs t is straight, slope = −k directly.
(c) I₂ + propanone in HCl: orders — I₂ = 0, propanone = 1, HCl = 1.
Why

In a clock/indicator setup, measuring initial slope gives initial rate. Large excess of one reactant makes its concentration effectively constant, so the measured rate appears independent of it. For I₂–propanone, rate is independent of [I₂] (flat line), and proportional to [propanone] and [H⁺].

Tips

State instrument + observable (e.g., “colorimeter, absorbance of Br₂”). For orders, combine table ratios with rate–concentration plots.
