A Level OCR Chemistry • 5.1 Rates, Orders & Arrhenius

Structured Question Answers & Explanations

A checked and improved answer guide for the exam-style questions. The layout matches the multiple-choice answer guide, with Easy, Medium and Hard questions organised into tabs.

Systematic Approach

Use before marking

1. Orders first

Read the rate equation or compare initial-rate experiments. Species missing from the rate equation are zero order.

2. Then calculate k

Substitute into the rate equation only after the orders are known. Units come from rate divided by the concentration terms.

3. Check Arrhenius units

Use R in J K-1 mol-1, so Ea is calculated in J mol-1. Convert to kJ mol-1 at the end.

These questions focus on core rate equation skills, half-life, graph interpretation and basic Arrhenius plotting.

Easy Q19 marksRate law basics

Orders, overall order and rate constant

(a) X is second order, Y is first order and Z is zero order.
(b) Overall order = 2 + 1 + 0 = 3.
(c) rate = k[X]2[Y]k = 3.72 × 10-5 ÷ ((0.010)2 × 0.020) = 18.6Units: dm6 mol-2 s-1.
(d) Doubling [X] makes the rate four times faster, because X is second order and 22 = 4. New rate would be 1.49 × 10-4 mol dm-3 s-1.
Exam tip

Do not include Z in the rate equation calculation. If a species is absent from the rate equation, its order is zero and its concentration has no effect on the rate.

Easy Q26 marksGraph orders

Bromide and bromate(V) ions in acid

(a) The rate–[H+] graph curves upwards, so the reaction is second order with respect to H+.
(b) The [Br-]–time sketch should be a decreasing curve: steep at first, then gradually flattening. This is the concentration–time shape for a first-order reactant.
(c) Overall order is 4. Br- is first order and H+ is second order, so BrO3- must be first order. rate = k[Br-][BrO3-][H+]2
(d) k = rate ÷ ([Br-][BrO3-][H+]2)Overall order = 4, so units of k are dm9 mol-3 s-1.
Why this works

For rate–concentration graphs: horizontal line = zero order, straight line through origin = first order, upward curve = second order.

Easy Q39 marksGraph value approximate

Decomposition of hydrogen peroxide

(a) Half-life is the time taken for the concentration of a reactant to fall to half of its current value.
(b) The graph shows approximately constant half-lives: 2.00 to 1.00 mol dm-3, then 1.00 to 0.50 mol dm-3, then 0.50 to 0.25 mol dm-3 take similar times. This shows first-order behaviour. rate = k[H2O2]
(c)(i) Draw a tangent at 100 s. A reasonable tangent gives a rate of about 5.5–6.5 × 10-3 mol dm-3 s-1.
(c)(ii) At 100 s, [H2O2] is about 0.40–0.45 mol dm-3. k = rate ÷ [H2O2] ≈ 1.4–1.6 × 10-2 s-1A half-life method gives a similar value: k = ln 2 ÷ t1/2.
(d) The half-life would be unchanged. Half-life is independent of initial concentration for a first-order reaction.
Marking note

Because the rate is read from a drawn tangent, allow a range of values. The method is more important than one exact number.

Easy Q511 marksArrhenius unit check

Arrhenius equation and graph

(a) A = Arrhenius constant / pre-exponential factor, with the same units as k. Ea = activation energy, usually J mol-1 in calculations. T = absolute temperature in K.
(b) k = Ae-Ea/RTA = k ÷ e-Ea/RT = keEa/RT
(c) For y = mx + c: y = ln k, x = 1/T, m = -Ea/R and c = ln A.
(d) From the graph, a suitable gradient is approximately -9.3 × 103 K. Ea = -gradient × R ≈ 9.3 × 103 × 8.314 ≈ 7.7 × 104 J mol-1So Ea is about 77 kJ mol-1.
Correction note

The old HTML had the sign wrong when rearranging for A. Dividing by e-Ea/RT is the same as multiplying by eEa/RT.

Medium Q58 marksGraph values approximate

Methanoic acid/bromine and iodine/propanone orders

(a)(i) Monitor bromine concentration using a colorimeter, following the decrease in orange/brown colour over time.
(a)(ii) A different method would be to remove samples at timed intervals, quench the reaction, and titrate the remaining bromine with sodium thiosulfate. Alternatively, monitor CO2 volume if gas collection is suitable.
(a)(iii) If HCOOH is in large excess, its concentration changes by only a tiny fraction, so it is effectively constant. Its effect is absorbed into k, making the observed order with respect to HCOOH effectively zero.
(b) From the bromine graph, initial rate from the tangent at t = 0 is about 3.3–3.7 × 10-5 mol dm-3 s-1. With [Br2]0 ≈ 0.010 mol dm-3, k ≈ initial rate ÷ [Br2] ≈ 3.3–3.7 × 10-3 s-1
(c) The iodine/propanone results give: I2 is zero order because the rate–concentration graph is horizontal; propanone is first order because doubling propanone doubles the rate; HCl is first order because increasing [HCl] by ×2.5 increases rate by ×2.5.

57 marks

These are the more demanding synthesis questions, especially mechanism design and Arrhenius graph calculations.

Hard Q111 marksChlorination of propanone

Orders, k, pH and temperature

(a) Chlorine is zero order because the [Cl2]–time graph is a straight line. H+ is first order because doubling [H+] doubles the rate. Propanone is first order because, after allowing for the ×3 change in [H+] from experiment 1 to 2, the remaining ×2 increase in rate is due to propanone doubling.
(b) rate = k[CH3COCH3][H+]k = 0.18 × 10-5 ÷ (0.75 × 0.05) = 4.8 × 10-5 dm3 mol-1 s-1
(c) pH 2 to pH 1 means [H+] increases by ×10. Since the reaction is first order in H+, the rate also increases by ×10: 2.6 × 10-5 mol dm-3 s-1.
(d) At lower temperature, the rate decreases and the rate constant k decreases. Concentrations and orders do not change, but fewer particles have enough energy to react.
Hard Q215 marksClock reaction

Hydrogen peroxide and iodide clock reaction

(a) The assumption is that the same fixed amount of iodine/product is produced at the visual endpoint each time, so initial rate is proportional to 1/t.
(b) Thiosulfate removes iodine as it forms, so no starch–iodine colour appears at first. Once the thiosulfate is used up, iodine remains in solution and gives a sudden blue-black colour with starch.
(c) Completed 1/t values: 259 s → 0.00386 s-1; 125 s → 0.00800 s-1; 78 s → 0.0128 s-1; 59 s → 0.0169 s-1; 48 s → 0.0208 s-1. A plot of 1/t against [H2O2] should be approximately a straight line through the origin, showing first order in H2O2.
(d) The graph against [I-] is a straight line through the origin, so the reaction is first order in I-. The graph against [H+] is horizontal, so the reaction is zero order in H+. rate = k[H2O2][I-]
(e) The reaction must take place in more than one step because the rate equation does not match the stoichiometry of the overall equation. H+ appears in the overall equation but not in the rate equation.
(f) One possible mechanism is: Step 1 slow: H2O2 + I- → IO- + H2OStep 2 fast: IO- + H+ → HIOStep 3 fast: HIO + I- + H+ → I2 + H2OAdding the steps gives the overall equation and the slow step gives rate = k[H2O2][I-].
Hard Q310 marksCorrected product-rate scaling

Nitrogen monoxide, ozone and product formation rates

(a) Tripling [NO] makes rate ×9 because NO is second order. Halving [O2] makes rate ×0.5. Overall effect = ×4.5, so the rate becomes 4.5 times faster.
(b)(i) Adding the lower-atmosphere equations gives: 1.5O2(g) → O3(g)
(b)(ii) NO acts as a catalyst because it is used in one step and regenerated in another, so it is not used up overall.
(c)(i) The mechanism must contain more than one step because NO appears in the rate equation but not in the overall equation. It acts as a catalyst/intermediate participant in the mechanism.
(c)(ii) Possible mechanism: Step 1 slow: NO + O3 → NO2 + O2Step 2 fast: NO2 + O → NO + O2
(d) Doubling ozone and tripling ethene increases the methanal formation rate by ×6: 2.0 × 10-11 × 6 = 1.2 × 10-10The equation makes 2CH2O for every 0.5O2, so oxygen forms at one-quarter of the methanal formation rate: 3.0 × 10-11 mol dm-3 s-1.
Correction note

The old HTML gave 6.0 × 10-11. The stoichiometric ratio CH2O : O2 is 2 : 0.5, so oxygen formation is one-quarter of methanal formation.

Reaction between F and G
