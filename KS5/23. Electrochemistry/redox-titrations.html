Potassium Manganate(VII) (KMnO4) Titrations

1 ▸ Fundamental Theory

In strongly acidic medium the deep-purple manganate(VII) ion (MnO4−) is a powerful oxidising agent. During a titration it accepts electrons from a reducing agent (such as Fe2+ or C2O42−) and is reduced to the almost colourless Mn2+ ion. Because the titrant itself is intensely coloured, no external indicator is required; the first permanent pale-pink coloration indicates the end-point.

Key half-equation (OCR spec 5.1.3):

MnO4− + 8 H+ + 5 e− → Mn2+ + 4 H2O

2 ▸ Six-Step Practical Method

  1. Rinse and fill the burette with standardised KMnO4(aq); ensure the jet is full and bubble-free.
  2. Make up a 250cm3 iron solutionv in a volumetric flask. Transfer exactly 25.0 cm3 of the unknown reducing-agent solution to a clean conical flask using a volumetric pipette.
  3. Add about 10 cm3 of 1 mol dm−3 H2SO4. (Avoid HCl – would produce Cl2; avoid HNO3 – itself an oxidiser.)
  4. Titrate with gentle swirling, adding dropwise near the end-point until a faint pink colour persists for 30 s.
  5. Repeat until you obtain concordant titres (two agreeing within 0.10 cm3).
  6. Process the data: convert volumes to moles, apply the 1 : 5 stoichiometry, and scale to the required volume.

3 ▸ Worked Example – Finding the Concentration of Iron(II)

Experimental data

  • Total volume of Fe²⁺ solution = 250.0 cm³
  • Volume of Fe²⁺ solution pipetted = 25.0 cm³
  • Average (concordant) titre of KMnO₄(aq) = 23.60 cm³
  • Concentration of KMnO₄(aq) in burette = 0.0200 mol dm−3

Step-by-step calculation

  1. Convert the average titre to dm³: 23.60 cm³ → 0.02360 dm³.
  2. Calculate the amount (mol) of MnO₄− delivered:
    n(MnO₄−) = 0.0200 mol dm−3 × 0.02360 dm³ = 4.72 × 10−4 mol
  3. From the ionic equation, 1 mol MnO₄− reacts with 5 mol Fe²⁺. Therefore n(Fe²⁺ in 25 cm³) = 5 × 4.72 × 10−4 = 2.36 × 10−3 mol
  4. Convert the pipetted volume to dm³: 0.0250 dm³.
  5. Concentration of Fe²⁺ = 2.36 × 10−3 mol ÷ 0.0250 dm³ = 0.0944 mol dm−3.

4 ▸ Examiner-Friendly Reminders

  • Show the ionic equation before using mole ratios.
  • Use cm3 → dm3 conversions every time to avoid factor-1000 slips.
  • Quote final answers to 3 s.f. unless instructed otherwise.
  • Don't forget to scale up to the original sample after finding the number of moles in the conical flask.

Sodium Thiosulfate (Na₂S₂O₃) / Iodometric Titrations

1 ▸ Fundamental Theory

Iodometric titrations involve two sequenced redox steps:

  1. An oxidising agent (e.g. chlorate(I) in bleach, Cu2+ from brass) reacts with excess iodide ions in acid, liberating iodine (I2), which colours the solution brown.
  2. The liberated I2 is titrated with standard sodium thiosulfate until the brown fades to pale straw. A few drops of starch are added, giving a blue-black complex, and titration continues until the blue disappears – the end-point.

Titration equation:

I2 + 2 S2O32− → 2 I− + S4O62−

2 ▸ Operational Tips

  • Add starch indicator only when the solution is pale yellow – earlier addition can trap iodine and inflate the titre.
  • Keep iodide/iodine solutions in amber glass; I2 is volatile and decomposes in strong light.
  • Standardise Na₂S₂O₃ periodically against primary-standard KIO₃.

3 ▸ Worked Example – Determining the Concentration of an Iodine Solution

Experimental data

  • Volume of iodine solution pipetted = 25.0 cm³
  • Average titre of Na₂S₂O₃(aq) = 18.40 cm³
  • [Na₂S₂O₃(aq)] = 0.100 mol dm−3

Step-by-step calculation

  1. n(S₂O₃2−) = 0.100 mol dm−3 × 0.01840 dm³ = 1.84 × 10−3 mol
  2. Stoichiometry: 2 mol thiosulfate react with 1 mol I₂ → n(I₂) = 1.84 × 10−3 ÷ 2 = 9.20 × 10−4 mol
  3. [I₂] = 9.20 × 10−4 mol ÷ 0.0250 dm³ = 0.0368 mol dm−3

4 ▸ Relating I2 Back to the Oxidiser

After finding the total moles of I2 present in the volumetric flask, use the balanced equation for the liberation step to deduce the amount of oxidiser originally in the sample. For example, bleach analysis uses:

ClO− + 2 I− + 2 H+ → I2 + Cl− + H2O

Here 1 mol ClO− gives 1 mol I2.

KMnO₄ – Exam-Style Practice Questions

Question 1 – Iron Tablets (Structured)

An iron supplement tablet was dissolved in dilute sulfuric acid and the solution was transferred to a 250 cm3 volumetric flask. After dilution to the mark, a 25.0 cm3 portion of this solution was titrated with 0.0200 mol dm−3 acidified potassium manganate(VII). The average titre was 24.80 cm3.

  1. Write the ionic equation for the reaction between MnO₄− and Fe2+ in acidic conditions.
  2. Calculate the concentration of Fe2+ ions in the aliquot, in mol dm−3.
  3. Determine the mass of iron present in one tablet.

Step-by-step solution

  • Ionic equation: MnO₄− + 8 H+ + 5 Fe2+ → Mn2+ + 5 Fe3+ + 4 H2O
  • n(MnO₄−) = 0.0200 × 0.02480 = 4.96 × 10−4 mol
  • n(Fe2+, 25 cm3) = 5 × 4.96 × 10−4 = 2.48 × 10−3 mol
  • [Fe2+] = 2.48 × 10−3 ÷ 0.0250 = 0.0992 mol dm−3
  • Total n(Fe2+) in 250 cm3 = 2.48 × 10−3 × 10 = 2.48 × 10−2 mol
  • m(Fe) = 2.48 × 10−2 × 55.85 = 1.39 g per tablet

Question 2 – Percentage Purity of Iron in an Ore (Unstructured)

A 1.80 g sample of iron ore was dissolved in excess dilute sulfuric acid. The solution was filtered into a 250 cm3 volumetric flask and diluted to the mark. Twenty-five cubic centimetre portions were titrated with 0.0200 mol dm−3 KMnO₄(aq); the mean titre was 22.40 cm3.

Calculate the percentage by mass of iron in the ore sample.

  • n(MnO₄−) = 0.0200 × 0.02240 = 4.48 × 10−4 mol
  • n(Fe2+, 25 cm3) = 5 × 4.48 × 10−4 = 2.24 × 10−3 mol
  • Total n(Fe2+) in 250 cm3 = 2.24 × 10−3 × 10 = 2.24 × 10−2 mol
  • m(Fe) = 2.24 × 10−2 × 55.85 = 1.25 g
  • % Fe = 1.25 g ÷ 1.80 g × 100 = 69.4 %

Na₂S₂O₃ – Exam-Style Practice Questions

Question 1 – Determining the Chlorine Content of Bleach (Structured)

Exactly 10.00 cm3 of household bleach (density 1.08 g cm−3) was transferred to a 250 cm3 volumetric flask. Excess potassium iodide solution and dilute sulfuric acid were added, and the mixture was diluted to the mark. After five minutes in the dark, 25.0 cm3 portions of the brown solution were titrated with 0.100 mol dm−3 sodium thiosulfate. The mean titre was 21.40 cm3.

Calculate the percentage by mass of chlorine present in the bleach, assuming all the chlorine is present as chlorate(I), ClO−.

  1. n(S₂O₃2−) = 0.100 × 0.02140 = 2.14 × 10−3 mol
  2. n(I₂, 25 cm3) = 2.14 × 10−3 ÷ 2 = 1.07 × 10−3 mol
  3. Total n(I₂) = 1.07 × 10−3 × 10 = 1.07 × 10−2 mol
  4. ClO− : I₂ = 1 : 1 → n(ClO−) = 1.07 × 10−2 mol
  5. Mr(ClO−) = 35.45 + 16.00 = 51.45
  6. m(ClO−) = 1.07 × 10−2 × 51.45 = 0.551 g
  7. m(bleach sample) = 10.00 cm3 × 1.08 g cm−3 = 10.8 g
  8. % Cl (as ClO−) = 0.551 g ÷ 10.8 g × 100 = 5.10 %

Question 2 – Percentage Copper in Brass (Unstructured)

A 1.50 g sample of brass was dissolved in concentrated nitric acid, converting all copper into Cu2+ ions. The solution was neutralised, and excess potassium iodide was added to precipitate CuI and liberate iodine according to:

2 Cu2+ + 4 I− → 2 CuI + I2

The mixture was transferred to a 250 cm3 volumetric flask and diluted to the mark. Twenty-five cubic centimetre aliquots of the brown solution were titrated with 0.100 mol dm−3 sodium thiosulfate; the mean titre was 23.60 cm3.

Calculate the percentage by mass of copper in the brass sample.

  1. n(S₂O₃2−) = 0.100 × 0.02360 = 2.36 × 10−3 mol
  2. n(I₂, 25 cm3) = 2.36 × 10−3 ÷ 2 = 1.18 × 10−3 mol
  3. Total n(I₂) = 1.18 × 10−3 × 10 = 1.18 × 10−2 mol
  4. 2 Cu2+ produce 1 I₂ → n(Cu2+) = 2 × 1.18 × 10−2 = 2.36 × 10−2 mol
  5. m(Cu) = 2.36 × 10−2 × 63.55 = 1.50 g
  6. % Cu = 1.50 g ÷ 1.50 g × 100 = 100 % (theoretical – practical values are lower)
