Model Answer:

Electrochemical cells are devices that convert chemical energy into electrical energy (galvanic/voltaic cells) or use electrical energy to drive non-spontaneous chemical reactions (electrolytic cells). They consist of two half-cells connected externally by a wire (allowing electron flow) and internally by a salt bridge or porous barrier (allowing ion flow).

Each half-cell contains an electrode (usually a metal or conductive material) immersed in an electrolyte solution containing ions related to the electrode.

Electrode potential (specifically standard electrode potential, E°) is a measure of the tendency of a chemical species in a half-cell to gain electrons and be reduced, relative to the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE) which is assigned a potential of 0.00 V under standard conditions (298K, 1M concentration, 1 atm pressure for gases). It quantifies the potential difference between the electrode and its solution. A more positive E° indicates a greater tendency for reduction.

Model Answer:

Oxidation is the process where a chemical species *loses* electrons, resulting in an *increase* in its oxidation state. In a galvanic cell, oxidation occurs at the *anode* (the negative electrode).

Reduction is the process where a chemical species *gains* electrons, resulting in a *decrease* in its oxidation state. In a galvanic cell, reduction occurs at the *cathode* (the positive electrode).

These two processes always occur together in a redox reaction. The acronym "OIL RIG" (Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain of electrons) is a helpful mnemonic.

Model Answer:

The cell potential (E°cell) is a direct measure of the tendency for the overall redox reaction to occur and is related to the strengths of the oxidizing and reducing agents involved.

  • Strong oxidizing agents (species that readily gain electrons, e.g., Cu²⁺, Ag⁺) have more positive standard electrode potentials (E°).
  • Strong reducing agents (species that readily lose electrons, e.g., Zn, Mg) have more negative standard electrode potentials (E°).

When two half-cells are connected:

  • The half-cell with the more positive E° value will undergo reduction (its oxidized form acts as the oxidizing agent at the cathode).
  • The half-cell with the more negative E° value will undergo oxidation (its reduced form acts as the reducing agent at the anode).

The overall cell potential is calculated as: E°cell = E°(cathode/reduction) - E°(anode/oxidation). A larger positive E°cell value indicates a greater difference in potential between the half-cells, a stronger driving force for the reaction, and thus a greater difference in strength between the oxidizing agent in the cathode half-cell and the reducing agent in the anode half-cell. A positive E°cell signifies a spontaneous reaction under standard conditions.

Model Answer:

Standard electrode potentials (E°) are determined experimentally under standard conditions: 25°C (298 K), 1.0 mol dm⁻³ concentration for all aqueous ions, and 1 atm (100 kPa or 101.3 kPa) pressure for any gases involved.

The process involves:

  1. Setting up the half-cell of interest with its electrode and 1.0 M electrolyte solution.
  2. Setting up a Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE), which consists of a platinum electrode coated in platinum black, immersed in a 1.0 M H⁺(aq) solution, with hydrogen gas bubbled over it at 1 atm pressure. The SHE is assigned E° = 0.00 V by definition.
  3. Connecting the two half-cells using a salt bridge (e.g., filter paper soaked in saturated KNO₃ solution) to allow ion flow and complete the circuit.
  4. Connecting the electrodes externally via a high-impedance voltmeter to measure the potential difference (voltage) between the half-cell of interest and the SHE, without drawing significant current.
  5. The measured voltage is the standard electrode potential (E°) of the half-cell relative to the SHE. The sign (+ or -) depends on whether the half-cell is positive (undergoes reduction) or negative (undergoes oxidation) relative to the SHE.
  6. In practice, secondary reference electrodes (e.g., Ag/AgCl or calomel) with accurately known potentials relative to the SHE are often used for convenience, and the measured potential is adjusted accordingly.

Model Answer:

  2. Clean the surface of each metal electrode strip (e.g., Cu, Zn, Fe) using sandpaper or emery paper until shiny. Rinse with distilled water and dry with a paper towel.

  12. Rinse the electrodes thoroughly with distilled water and dry them.
  13. Repeat steps 2-12 for each planned combination of half-cells (e.g., Fe/Cu, Mg/Cu etc.), using fresh solutions or ensuring no contamination, and always using a fresh salt bridge.
  14. For improved reliability, repeat the measurement for each cell combination two more times (total of 3 trials) and calculate the average voltage.

Model Answer:

Potential sources of error include:

  • Systematic Errors:
    • Inaccurate concentration of electrolyte solutions (not exactly 1.0 M).
    • Temperature differing significantly from standard 25°C (affecting E values).
    • Calibration error in the voltmeter.
    • Consistent presence of an oxide layer if cleaning is inadequate for all electrodes.
    • Junction potentials at the salt bridge interfaces (small potentials generated where dissimilar solutions meet).
  • Random Errors:
    • Fluctuations in room temperature during the experiment.
    • Inconsistent cleaning of electrode surfaces between trials.
