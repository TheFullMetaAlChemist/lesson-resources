Model Answer:

"Rate of reaction is a measure of how quickly reactants are converted into products. It can be calculated by measuring the change in concentration of reactants or products per unit time. In this experiment, it can be determined by measuring either the mass of carbon dioxide produced per unit time (mass loss method) or the volume of carbon dioxide produced per unit time (gas collection method)."

Explain collision theory

Explain collision theory and how it relates to rate of reaction.

Hints:

  • What two conditions must be met for particles to react successfully?
  • How does the concentration of reactants affect the frequency of collisions?
  • Why would the reaction rate change as the reaction proceeds?
  • How could factors like temperature and surface area be explained by collision theory?

Model Answer:

"Collision theory states that for a reaction to occur, particles must collide with sufficient energy (equal to or greater than the activation energy) and with the correct orientation. The rate of reaction depends on the frequency of successful collisions between reactant particles.

In this experiment, as the reaction proceeds, the concentration of reactants decreases, leading to fewer collisions between hydrochloric acid and calcium carbonate particles. This results in a decrease in the reaction rate over time. Temperature affects the kinetic energy of particles, while surface area affects the number of exposed particles available for collision."

Hypothesis

Your hypothesis

  • Consider how the surface area of calcium carbonate might change during the reaction
  • Apply collision theory to explain your prediction about the reaction rate

Model Answer:

"I predict that the rate of reaction will be highest at the start of the reaction and will gradually decrease over time until the reaction eventually stops. This is because initially, the concentration of hydrochloric acid is at its maximum and there is a large surface area of calcium carbonate available for reaction. As the reaction proceeds, both the concentration of acid and the surface area of calcium carbonate decrease, resulting in fewer successful collisions between particles per unit time. According to collision theory, this reduction in successful collisions will cause the reaction rate to decrease over time."

Procedure

Method A - Measuring mass loss

Provide clear, numbered step-by-step methods for measuring mass loss.

Hints:

  • How can you track the mass change precisely over time?
  • What's the best way to add the marble chips to start the reaction?
  • How will you time the measurements consistently?
  • What's a good time interval for taking measurements?
  • How will you know when to stop collecting data?

Model Answer:

  1. Set up the electronic balance and zero it.
  2. Weigh a clean, dry conical flask.
  3. Measure 50mL of 1M hydrochloric acid using a measuring cylinder and add it to the flask.
  4. Weigh approximately 2g of marble chips on filter paper.
  5. Start the stopwatch as you add the marble chips to the acid in the flask.
  6. Place the flask on the balance and record the initial mass.
  7. Record the mass every 30 seconds for 10 minutes or until the reaction appears to stop.
  8. Calculate the mass loss at each time interval by subtracting from the initial mass.

Method B - Measuring gas volume

Provide clear, numbered step-by-step methods for measuring gas volume.

Hints:

  • How can you ensure the gas collection apparatus is sealed properly?
  • What's the best way to connect the reaction flask to the gas collection system?
  • How will you accurately read the volume of gas collected?
  • What might happen if the reaction is very vigorous at the start?
  • How could you minimize gas dissolution in water if using the water displacement method?

Model Answer:

  1. Set up a gas collection apparatus with a conical flask connected via delivery tube to a gas syringe or an inverted measuring cylinder in a water bath.
  2. Measure 50mL of 1M hydrochloric acid and add it to the conical flask.
  3. Weigh approximately 2g of marble chips on filter paper.
  4. Insert the bung with delivery tube into the flask, ensuring the apparatus is airtight.
  5. Start the stopwatch as you add the marble chips to the acid in the flask.
  6. Record the volume of gas collected every 30 seconds for 10 minutes or until gas production ceases.
  7. Calculate the volume of gas produced at each time interval.

Analysis

Data Analysis

Describe how you will calculate and compare the rate of reaction from your collected data, include how you will interpret your results graphically.

Hints:

  • What formula will you use to calculate rate from your data?
  • What two main graphs should you plot?
  • Which graph will show the progress of the reaction?
  • Which graph will directly show how the rate changes?
  • How will the shape of your graphs illustrate your hypothesis?
  • What features on your graphs will represent the initial rate?
  • How can you compare the two methods graphically?

Model Answer:

"To calculate the rate of reaction:

  • For the mass loss method: I will calculate the change in mass for each time interval and divide by the time period (Δmass/Δtime) to determine the rate in g/s.
  • For the gas collection method: I will calculate the volume of gas produced for each time interval and divide by the time period (Δvolume/Δtime) to determine the rate in mL/s.

I will plot the following graphs:

  1. Mass loss or gas volume vs. time - This will show a curve that gradually levels off, indicating how the reaction slows down.
  2. Rate of reaction vs. time - This will show a decreasing curve, demonstrating how the reaction rate decreases over time.

From these graphs, I can determine:

  • Initial rate of reaction (from the steepest part of the curve)
  • How quickly the rate decreases
  • When the reaction effectively completes (when the curve plateaus)

I will also compare the accuracy and reliability of both methods by analyzing their consistency and any discrepancies between them."
