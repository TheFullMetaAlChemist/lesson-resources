Question 1 — Catalytic Converters and Enthalpy Profiles

Focus: exothermic enthalpy profiles, catalysts, heterogeneous catalysis and activation energy.

The catalytic converter speeds up the reaction between carbon monoxide and nitrogen monoxide: 2NO(g) + 2CO(g) → 2CO₂(g) + N₂(g), ΔH = −746 kJ mol⁻¹.

1a(i) Draw an enthalpy profile and label ΔH

2 marks
Model answerThe diagram should show reactants at a higher energy level than products. The curve should rise to a peak and then fall to the products. A downward arrow from reactants to products should be labelled ΔH.
Why this gets the marks
  • 1 mark: products shown below reactants because the reaction is exothermic.
  • 1 mark: ΔH shown as a downward arrow from reactants to products.
Key explanationΔH is negative, so energy is released to the surroundings. The products have less enthalpy than the reactants.
Common mistakesDrawing products above reactants, drawing ΔH upwards, or missing the activation energy peak.
Exam tipCheck the sign of ΔH before drawing. Negative ΔH means products lower than reactants.

1a(ii) Add a profile for no catalyst

1 mark
Model answerDraw a second curve with a higher activation energy peak. It should start at the same reactant level and end at the same product level. Label it “no catalyst”.
Why this gets the mark
The uncatalysed pathway has a higher activation energy, but ΔH is unchanged.
Key explanationA catalyst provides an alternative pathway with lower activation energy. It does not change the overall energy difference between reactants and products.
Common mistakesChanging the product energy level or changing the size/direction of ΔH.
Exam tipCatalysts change the pathway, not the overall enthalpy change.

1a(iii) Homogeneous or heterogeneous catalysis?

2 marks
Model answerThis is heterogeneous catalysis because the catalyst is in a different phase from the reactants. The catalyst is a solid metal, while the reactants are gases.
Why this gets the marks
  • 1 mark: identifies heterogeneous catalysis.
  • 1 mark: explains that catalyst and reactants are in different phases/states.
Key explanationHomogeneous catalysis means catalyst and reactants are in the same phase. Heterogeneous catalysis means they are in different phases.
Common mistakesSaying homogeneous because the gases pass over or mix with the catalyst.
Exam tipUse state symbols or physical states: solid catalyst + gaseous reactants = heterogeneous.

1b Why are catalytic converters less effective on short journeys?

2 marks
Model answerAt the start of the journey, the catalytic converter is too cold. Only a small proportion of reactant molecules have energy greater than or equal to the activation energy, so there are fewer successful collisions.
Why this gets the marks
  • 1 mark: temperature is too low at the start.
  • 1 mark: small proportion of molecules have enough energy to overcome activation energy.
Key explanationCatalytic converters work best once they have heated up. Low temperature reduces the proportion of successful collisions.
Common mistakesSaying the catalyst “has not started working”. The catalyst is present, but the reaction rate is low because the temperature is low.
Exam tipFor temperature and rate, link to activation energy and successful collisions.

Question 2 — Calcium Carbonate and Hydrochloric Acid Rate Graphs

Focus: interpreting final gas volume, initial rate, concentration, temperature and limiting reactants.

Excess calcium carbonate reacts with hydrochloric acid. The volume of gas produced is measured until the reaction stops.

2a(i) 50 cm³ of 0.2 mol dm⁻³ HCl at 25°C

1 mark
Model answerB. This reaction produces the same final volume of gas as curve C, but at a faster rate.
Why this gets the mark
Curve C uses 100 cm³ of 0.1 mol dm⁻³ HCl. The new reaction uses 50 cm³ of 0.2 mol dm⁻³ HCl. Both contain the same amount of acid overall, so the same volume of carbon dioxide forms. The higher concentration gives a faster initial rate.
Key explanationFinal gas volume depends on moles of acid. Initial gradient depends on rate. Higher concentration gives more frequent collisions.
Common mistakesChoosing a curve with a higher final gas volume just because the concentration is higher.
Exam tipFor gas-volume graphs: final volume = amount of limiting reactant; initial gradient = rate.

2a(ii) 100 cm³ of 0.1 mol dm⁻³ HCl at 10°C

1 mark
Model answerD. It produces the same final gas volume as curve C but more slowly.
Why this gets the mark
The volume and concentration of acid are the same as curve C, so the same total amount of gas is made. The lower temperature reduces the initial rate.
Key explanationAt lower temperature, particles have less kinetic energy. Fewer particles have energy greater than or equal to activation energy, so there are fewer successful collisions per second.
Common mistakesThinking a lower temperature means less gas is produced. It changes rate, not the amount of acid available.
Exam tipTemperature changes the speed of this reaction, not the final gas volume.

2a(iii) 100 cm³ of 0.2 mol dm⁻³ HCl at 25°C

1 mark
Model answerA. It produces more gas than curve C and reacts faster.
Why this gets the mark
The volume of acid is the same as curve C, but the concentration is doubled. There are twice as many moles of HCl, so more carbon dioxide is produced. The higher concentration also increases the rate.
Key explanationMore concentrated acid contains more acid particles per unit volume, giving more frequent collisions with calcium carbonate.
Common mistakesSaying it produces the same amount because the volume is the same. Concentration also matters.
Exam tipCompare both volume and concentration to decide the number of moles.

2b Which two curves have the same initial rate?

1 mark
Model answerA and B.
Why this gets the mark
The initial rate is shown by the initial gradient. A and B have the same initial steepness.
Key explanationA steeper initial gradient means a faster initial rate. Curves with the same initial gradient have the same initial rate.
Common mistakesChoosing curves with the same final gas volume. Same final volume does not necessarily mean same initial rate.
Exam tipInitial rate = gradient at the start of the curve.

2c Name the gas produced

1 mark
Model answerThe gas is carbon dioxide, CO₂.
CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂
Key explanationAcids react with carbonates to produce a salt, water and carbon dioxide.
Common mistakesSaying hydrogen. Hydrogen is produced when acids react with many metals, not carbonates.
Exam tipAcid + carbonate → salt + water + carbon dioxide.

Question 3 — Sodium Thiosulfate, Collision Theory and Boltzmann Distributions

Focus: measuring reaction rate, concentration effects, cloudiness and temperature distributions.

Sodium thiosulfate reacts with hydrochloric acid to form sulfur, sulfur dioxide, sodium chloride and water. The time taken for a cross to become obscured is measured.

3a Why does the solution become cloudy?

1 mark
Model answerThe solution becomes cloudy because solid sulfur is produced. The sulfur forms a precipitate in the solution.
Na₂S₂O₃(aq) + 2HCl(aq) → S(s) + SO₂(g) + 2NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)
Key explanationThe state symbol (s) shows sulfur is a solid. Small solid sulfur particles scatter light, making the solution cloudy.
Common mistakesSaying sulfur dioxide causes the cloudiness. Sulfur dioxide is a gas.
Exam tipUse state symbols in the equation. They often reveal the answer.

3b Suggest one other technique to measure rate

1 mark
Model answerUse colorimetry to measure how the cloudiness changes over time.
Other accepted methods
  • Measure the volume of gas produced over time.
  • Measure mass loss over time.
  • Use a pH meter.
Key explanationA colorimeter measures light passing through the solution. As more sulfur forms, the solution becomes cloudier and less light passes through.
Common mistakesSuggesting a thermometer. Temperature affects rate, but measuring temperature does not directly measure reaction progress here.
Exam tipChoose a variable that changes as the reaction progresses.

3c Predict the trend and explain using collision theory

3 marks
Model answerAs the concentration of sodium thiosulfate increases, the time taken for the cross to become obscured decreases. This is because there are more sodium thiosulfate particles per unit volume. The particles collide more frequently, so there are more successful collisions per second and the rate of reaction increases.
Why this gets the marks
  • 1 mark: time decreases as concentration increases.
  • 1 mark: more particles per unit volume.
  • 1 mark: increased collision frequency / more collisions per second.
Key explanationThe total volume of sodium thiosulfate plus water is kept constant at 50 cm³. Using more sodium thiosulfate solution and less water increases concentration, so the same visible endpoint is reached more quickly.
Common mistakesSaying “the rate decreases” instead of “the time decreases”. Also, “more collisions” alone is not precise enough; say more collisions per second.
Exam tipUse: “more particles per unit volume, so more frequent successful collisions.”

3d(i) Label the Boltzmann distribution axes

1 mark
Model answerHorizontal axis: kinetic energy. Vertical axis: number of molecules, fraction of molecules or percentage of molecules.
Key explanationA Boltzmann distribution shows the spread of kinetic energies in a sample of particles.
Common mistakesLabelling the x-axis as enthalpy or the y-axis as rate of reaction.
Exam tipBoltzmann graph: x-axis = energy; y-axis = number/fraction of molecules.

3d(ii) Draw the 40°C Boltzmann curve

2 marks
Model answerThe 40°C curve should start at the origin, peak higher than the 70°C curve, and peak further to the left. It should have a smaller high-energy tail and should not touch the horizontal axis.
Why this gets the marks
  • 1 mark: curve starts at origin and the peak is to the left of the 70°C peak.
  • 1 mark: peak is higher than the 70°C peak and the curve does not touch the x-axis.
Key explanationAt lower temperature, particles have a lower average kinetic energy. More molecules are grouped at lower energies, so the curve is taller and shifted left.
Common mistakesDrawing the lower-temperature curve lower and flatter. That represents a higher temperature.
Exam tipLower temperature = higher peak, further left, less spread out.

3d(iii) Explain temperature effect using Boltzmann distribution

2 marks
Model answerAt higher temperature, a greater proportion of molecules have energy greater than or equal to the activation energy. This means there are more successful collisions per second, so the rate of reaction increases.
Why this gets the marks
  • 1 mark: more molecules have energy ≥ activation energy.
  • 1 mark: more successful/effective collisions and faster rate.
Key explanationParticles also move faster at higher temperature, but the key A-level marking point is the larger proportion exceeding activation energy.
Common mistakesSaying activation energy decreases. Temperature does not lower activation energy; a catalyst does.
Exam tipAlways include: “greater proportion of particles have energy ≥ activation energy.”

Question 4 — Haber Process and Dynamic Equilibrium

Focus: dynamic equilibrium, Le Châtelier’s principle, temperature, pressure and catalysts.

N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g), ΔH = −92 kJ mol⁻¹.

4a Define dynamic equilibrium

2 marks
Model answerDynamic equilibrium is reached in a closed system when the rate of the forward reaction is equal to the rate of the reverse reaction. The concentrations of reactants and products remain constant.
Why this gets the marks
Any two key ideas are needed: closed system, equal forward and reverse rates, and constant concentrations.
Key explanationDynamic equilibrium does not mean the reaction has stopped. Both reactions continue, but there is no overall change in concentration.
Common mistakesSaying the amounts of reactants and products are equal. They are constant, not necessarily equal.
Exam tipUse the phrase: “closed system, equal rates, constant concentrations.”

4b(i) Effect of increasing temperature

2 marks
Model answerThe forward reaction is exothermic, so increasing temperature favours the reverse reaction. The equilibrium shifts left, towards nitrogen and hydrogen.
Why this gets the marks
  • 1 mark: forward reaction is exothermic.
  • 1 mark: equilibrium shifts left / towards reactants.
Key explanationIncreasing temperature favours the endothermic direction to oppose the change. Since the forward reaction is exothermic, the reverse reaction is endothermic.
Common mistakesSaying higher temperature makes more ammonia because rate increases. The question asks about equilibrium position, not rate.
Exam tipIncreasing temperature favours the endothermic direction.

4b(ii) Effect of increasing pressure

2 marks
Model answerIncreasing pressure shifts the equilibrium to the right, towards ammonia. There are 4 moles of gas on the left and 2 moles of gas on the right, so equilibrium shifts to the side with fewer gas molecules.
Why this gets the marks
  • 1 mark: identifies fewer gas molecules on the product side.
  • 1 mark: states the equilibrium shifts right / towards products.
Key explanationHigher pressure is opposed by shifting to the side with fewer gas particles: 1 N₂ + 3 H₂ = 4 moles; 2 NH₃ = 2 moles.
Common mistakesSaying pressure always favours products. It only favours the side with fewer gas molecules.
Exam tipCount gas moles only. Ignore solids and liquids for pressure shifts.

4b(iii) Effect of adding a catalyst

2 marks
Model answerA catalyst has no effect on the position of equilibrium. It increases the rate of the forward and reverse reactions by the same amount, so equilibrium is reached faster but the equilibrium mixture is unchanged.
Why this gets the marks
  • 1 mark: forward and reverse rates increase equally.
  • 1 mark: no effect on equilibrium position.
Key explanationA catalyst lowers activation energy for both directions of the reaction. It does not change the relative stability of reactants or products.
Common mistakesSaying the catalyst shifts equilibrium to the right or increases yield.
Exam tipCatalyst = faster equilibrium, same equilibrium position.

4c Why does using a catalyst reduce CO₂ emissions?

2 marks
Model answerA catalyst lowers the activation energy of the reaction. This means the Haber process can be carried out at a lower temperature or with less energy input. Less fossil fuel needs to be burned for heating, so less carbon dioxide is released.
Why this gets the marks
  • 1 mark: catalyst lowers activation energy / energy requirement.
  • 1 mark: less fuel is combusted, reducing CO₂ emissions.
Key explanationThe environmental benefit is indirect: the catalyst reduces the energy demand of the process, reducing combustion of fossil fuels.
Common mistakesSaying the catalyst absorbs carbon dioxide or changes the equilibrium yield.
Exam tipUse the chain: lower activation energy → lower temperature/less heating → less fuel burned → less CO₂.

4d Environmental disadvantage of catalysts

1 mark
Model answerSome catalysts are toxic, so disposal of used catalysts can cause environmental problems.
Key explanationIndustrial catalysts may contain transition metals or other substances that must be disposed of carefully.
Common mistakesGiving an economic disadvantage, such as “catalysts are expensive”. The question asks for an environmental disadvantage.
Exam tipEnvironmental answers should link to toxicity, pollution, waste, habitat damage or resource use.

Question 5 — N₂O₄/NO₂ Equilibrium, Kc and Pressure

Focus: dynamic equilibrium on graphs, Kc expressions, temperature effects and pressure colour changes.

N₂O₄(g) ⇌ 2NO₂(g). N₂O₄ is colourless and NO₂ is brown.

5a Mark the first time dynamic equilibrium is reached

1 mark
Model answerMark the first point where both concentration curves become horizontal.
Key explanationHorizontal lines mean the concentrations are no longer changing. The forward and reverse reactions are still happening, but at equal rates.
Common mistakesMarking the point where the curves cross. Equal concentrations do not mean equilibrium.
Exam tipOn concentration-time graphs, equilibrium starts when the curves become flat, not when they cross.

5b(i) Write the Kc expression

1 mark
Model answerKc = [NO₂]² / [N₂O₄]
Key explanationProducts go on the top, reactants on the bottom. The coefficient 2 in front of NO₂ becomes a power of 2.
Common mistakesForgetting to square [NO₂], putting the expression upside down, or missing square brackets.
Exam tipProducts over reactants; powers from the balanced equation.

5b(ii) Calculate Kc at 298 K

1 mark
Model answerKc = (0.0162)² / 0.0410 = 6.40 × 10⁻³
Why this gets the mark
The correct equilibrium concentrations are substituted into the correct Kc expression and calculated accurately.
Key explanation[NO₂] is squared because NO₂ has a coefficient of 2 in the balanced equation.
Common mistakesForgetting to square 0.0162, rounding too early, or using initial concentrations instead of equilibrium concentrations.
Exam tipWrite the expression first, then substitute the numbers.

5b(iii) Deduce whether the forward reaction is exothermic or endothermic

2 marks
Model answerAt the higher temperature, Kc increases. This means the equilibrium mixture contains a greater proportion of products, so equilibrium has shifted to the right. Since increasing temperature favours the forward reaction, the forward reaction must be endothermic.
Why this gets the marks
  • 1 mark: Kc increases, so more products are present at equilibrium.
  • 1 mark: if higher temperature shifts equilibrium right, the forward reaction is endothermic.
Key explanationA larger Kc means the numerator is larger relative to the denominator. For this expression, that means more NO₂ relative to N₂O₄.
Common mistakesSaying Kc increases because the rate increases. Kc is about equilibrium position, not rate.
Exam tipIf temperature increases and Kc increases, the forward reaction is endothermic. If temperature increases and Kc decreases, the forward reaction is exothermic.

5c Effect of increasing total pressure on colour

3 marks
Model answerIncreasing total pressure shifts the equilibrium to the left. This is because there are fewer moles of gas on the left-hand side: 1 mole of N₂O₄ compared with 2 moles of NO₂ on the right. More colourless N₂O₄ and less brown NO₂ are present, so the mixture becomes paler brown.
Why this gets the marks
  • 1 mark: equilibrium shifts left.
  • 1 mark: fewer gas molecules on the left-hand side.
  • 1 mark: mixture becomes paler brown or more colourless.
Key explanationLe Châtelier’s principle says the equilibrium shifts to oppose a change. Higher pressure is opposed by shifting to the side with fewer gas molecules.
Common mistakesSaying the mixture becomes darker brown. That would mean more NO₂ has formed.
Exam tipFor pressure and colour equilibria: count gas moles, decide the shift, then link to the colour of each species.

Summary — High-Value OCR Takeaways

Use these as final revision reminders after completing the model answers.

1. Catalysts
Catalysts lower activation energy but do not change ΔH or equilibrium position.
2. Temperature and rate
Higher temperature increases the proportion of particles with energy ≥ activation energy.
3. Concentration and rate
Higher concentration means more particles per unit volume and more frequent collisions.
4. Dynamic equilibrium
Closed system, equal forward and reverse rates, constant concentrations.
5. Pressure equilibria
Increasing pressure favours the side with fewer gas molecules.
6. Temperature equilibria
Increasing temperature favours the endothermic direction.
7. Kc
Products over reactants; powers come from the balanced equation.
8. Kc and temperature
Only temperature changes Kc. Catalysts and pressure do not change Kc.
9. Graphs
Initial rate is the initial gradient. Equilibrium starts when concentration curves become flat.
10. Exam wording
Use “successful collisions” and “greater proportion above activation energy” for full-rate explanations.
