Q1 — Chromium(III) sulfate (multiple choice)

1 mark TB1

Model answer

Option C — Cr₂(SO₄)₃

How to get the mark (quick method)
  • Write the ions: Cr³⁺ and SO₄²⁻.
  • Find the lowest common multiple of charges (LCM): 6.
  • 2 × Cr³⁺ gives +6 and 3 × SO₄²⁻ gives −6 → neutral compound.
  • Use brackets for sulfate because it stays as a group: Cr₂(SO₄)₃.
Common mistakes
  • Missing brackets: Cr₂SO₄₃ (ambiguous / incorrect).
  • Swapping numbers (e.g., Cr₃(SO₄)₂) without checking charge balance.
Exam tip
If it’s an ionic formula question, always do a charge check at the end: total positive charge + total negative charge must equal 0.

Q2 — Non-polar molecule (multiple choice)

1 mark TB5

Model answer

Option A — SF₆ (non-polar overall)

Why SF₆ is non-polar
  • Each S–F bond is polar, but SF₆ has a highly symmetrical arrangement.
  • The bond dipoles cancel because the molecule is symmetrical → no net dipole.
Common mistakes
  • Confusing “polar bonds” with “polar molecule”. A molecule can have polar bonds and still be non-polar if dipoles cancel.
  • Forgetting to consider shape (e.g. trigonal pyramidal shapes usually give a net dipole).
Exam tip
Two-step check: (1) Are the bonds polar? (2) Is the shape symmetrical enough for dipoles to cancel?

Q3 — Redox reaction (multiple choice)

1 mark TB6

Model answer

Option A — Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂

Why this is redox
  • Mg goes from oxidation state 0 → +2 (oxidised).
  • H⁺ goes from +1 → 0 in H₂ (reduced).
  • Both oxidation and reduction happen → redox.
Common mistakes
  • Thinking any reaction with oxygen is “redox” (not always in the equation shown).
  • Not checking oxidation states — always verify with numbers.
Exam tip
If an element changes oxidation number, it’s redox. Use OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons), Reduction Is Gain.

Q4 — Empirical formula (multiple choice)

1 mark TB7 MS

Model answer

Option C — empirical formula CH₄N

Method (empirical formula checklist)
  • Convert each element’s mass to moles: n = m/Ar.
  • Divide every mole value by the smallest mole value.
  • Round to whole numbers (or multiply to remove halves/thirds).
  • Write the empirical formula using the ratio.
Common mistakes
  • Stopping after finding moles (you must get a ratio).
  • Rounding too early (keep a few decimal places until the end).
Exam tip
If you get ratios like 1 : 1.5 : 3, multiply all by 2 to clear the 0.5.

Q5 — [Na⁺] after mixing solutions (multiple choice)

1 mark TB7 MS

Model answer

Option C — 0.300 mol dm⁻³

Method (step-by-step)
  1. Convert volumes: 100 cm³ = 0.100 dm³ (for each solution).
  2. Moles Na⁺ from NaCl: 0.200 × 0.100 = 0.0200 mol.
  3. Moles Na⁺ from Na₂CO₃: moles of Na₂CO₃ is 0.200 × 0.100 = 0.0200, but it gives 2 Na⁺ → 0.0400 mol Na⁺.
  4. Total Na⁺ moles = 0.0600 mol. Total volume = 0.200 dm³.
  5. [Na⁺] = 0.0600 / 0.200 = 0.300 mol dm⁻³.
Common mistakes
  • Forgetting Na₂CO₃ produces 2 Na⁺.
  • Using total volume = 0.100 dm³ (it doubles after mixing).
Exam tip
When a salt has a subscript (e.g. Na₂CO₃), always translate it into “how many ions per formula unit” before calculating ion moles.

Q6 — Exact neutralisation (multiple choice)

1 mark TB8 MS

Model answer

Option C — 0.100 mol Ba(OH)₂

How to do it (neutralisation using stoichiometry)
  • Moles of H₂SO₄: n = c×V. 100 cm³ = 0.100 dm³, so n = 1.00×0.100 = 0.100 mol.
  • H₂SO₄ provides 2 H⁺ per mole, so it needs 2 OH⁻ per mole to neutralise.
  • So OH⁻ needed = 2 × 0.100 = 0.200 mol OH⁻.
  • Ba(OH)₂ gives 2 OH⁻ per mole, so moles Ba(OH)₂ needed = 0.200 / 2 = 0.100 mol.
Common mistakes
  • Forgetting H₂SO₄ is diprotic (2 H⁺).
  • Forgetting Ba(OH)₂ contains 2 OH⁻.
Exam tip
For acids/bases, write “H⁺ equivalents” and “OH⁻ equivalents” to stop yourself missing the 2× factor.

Q7 — Titre larger than it should be (multiple choice)

1 mark TB9 WS

Model answer

Option D — Only 1

Why “1 only” is correct
  • Overfilling a volumetric flask makes the solution in the flask more dilute than intended.
  • So each cm³ from the burette contains fewer moles → you need a larger volume to neutralise → larger titre.
  • Rinsing the conical flask or pipette with water adds water but does not change the number of moles delivered (it only changes concentration in the flask, not moles).
Common mistakes
  • Thinking “adding water” always changes moles. It changes concentration, not moles, unless it is diluting the standard solution itself.
  • Mixing up what’s in the burette vs what’s in the conical flask.
Exam tip
Ask: does the error change the moles of analyte delivered? If it only adds water to the conical flask, the moles are unchanged.

Q8(c) — Explain bromine’s low melting point

3 marks TB5 AO2

Model answer

Bromine is a simple molecular substance (Br₂). Molecules are held together by weak London forces, so little energy is needed to overcome them when melting.

How to secure all 3 marks (checklist)
  • State it is simple molecular / made of Br₂ molecules.
  • Name the intermolecular forces: London (induced dipole–dipole).
  • Link force strength to melting point: forces are weak → low energy needed → low mp.
Common mistakes
  • Talking about breaking covalent bonds (melting breaks intermolecular forces, not covalent bonds).
  • Saying “hydrogen bonding” (Br₂ cannot hydrogen bond).

Q8(d) — Conductivity: HgBr₂ vs mercury (solid and molten)

5 marks TB4 AO2

Model answer (high-scoring)

Solid HgBr₂ does not conduct because ions are fixed. Molten HgBr₂ conducts because ions are mobile. Mercury conducts when solid and molten because delocalised electrons can move in both states.

Mark points (aim to mention all)
  • HgBr₂(s): no conduction because ions are fixed in lattice (not mobile).
  • HgBr₂(l): conducts because ions can move and carry charge.
  • Hg (metal, solid): conducts due to mobile delocalised electrons.
  • Hg (metal, molten): still conducts because electrons remain mobile.
Common mistakes
Saying “electrons move” in molten ionic compounds. In ionic melts, the charge carriers are ions.

Q9(b) — Dot-and-cross for CO (include a dative bond)

2 marks TB4 AO2

What your diagram must show

Three shared pairs between C and O (overall triple bond), with one pair shown as a dative bond. Include lone pairs (at least one on each atom).

How to draw it (mark-point checklist)
  • Show 3 bonding pairs between C and O.
  • Indicate one bonding pair as a dative (both electrons from the same atom using the same symbol/arrow).
  • Include lone pairs consistent with octets.
Tip: You don’t need a perfect artwork, but you do need the correct number of shared pairs and a clear dative indication.
Common mistakes
Only drawing a double bond (2 shared pairs). That usually drops you to 0–1 marks depending on the scheme.

Q9(c)(i) — Maximum CO volume at RTP

2 marks TB7 MS

Model answer

91 cm³ of CO

Method (limiting reagent → moles → gas volume)
  • Calculate moles of each reactant using n = m/Mr.
  • Identify the limiting reactant (the one that produces fewer moles of CO based on the equation ratio).
  • Use 1:1 ratio to find moles of CO.
  • Convert to volume at RTP using V = n×24 (dm³), then convert dm³ → cm³ (×1000).
Common mistakes
Mixing dm³ and cm³ (remember: 1 dm³ = 1000 cm³).

Q9(c)(ii) — Two improvements (practical)

2 marks TB9 WS

Model answers (any two)

Heat until no more gas is produced; allow the gas to cool to room temperature before reading volume.

Why these improvements earn marks
  • Heating longer ensures the reaction reaches completion (maximum gas produced).
  • Gas volume depends on temperature; cooling to RTP gives a fair/accurate reading.
Common mistakes
Vague statements like “be more careful” don’t earn marks. Name a specific change and link it to accuracy/reliability.

Q10(b) — Number of molecules in 1.3535 g of PBr₃

3 marks TB7 MS

Model answer

3.01 × 10²¹ molecules

Full method (mark-scheme style)
  • Calculate Mr: PBr₃ = 30.97 + (79.9×3) ≈ 270.7 g mol⁻¹
  • Moles: n = m/Mr = 1.3535 / 270.7 = 5.00×10⁻³ mol
  • Molecules: N = n×Nₐ = 5.00×10⁻³ × 6.02×10²³ = 3.01×10²¹
Common mistakes
  • Using Avogadro’s constant on the mass directly (you must convert to moles first).
  • Rounding Mr too aggressively early on.

Q10(c) — Shape of PBr₃ and explanation

3 marks TB5

Model answer

Trigonal pyramidal. There are 3 bonding pairs and 1 lone pair around P; electron pairs repel and the lone pair repels more strongly.

How to secure all marks
  • Name the molecular shape: trigonal pyramidal.
  • State electron pairs: 3 bonding pairs + 1 lone pair.
  • Link to repulsion: lone pair repels more → changes bond angles/shape.
Common mistakes
Saying “trigonal planar” (that would require no lone pair on the central atom).

Q11 — Percentage yield of urea

3 marks TB7 MS

Model answer

76.5%

Method (structured)
  1. Convert tonnes to grams (or kg consistently) and calculate moles of NH₃.
  2. Use equation ratio: 2 mol NH₃ → 1 mol urea (theoretical moles of urea).
  3. Convert actual mass of urea to moles.
  4. % yield = (actual / theoretical) × 100.
Check: yield must be ≤ 100%. If you get >100%, you’ve likely reversed the fraction or made a unit conversion error.
Common mistakes
  • Forgetting the 2:1 ratio (NH₃ to urea).
  • Swapping actual and theoretical in the percentage yield calculation.
Exam tip
Write “theoretical” and “actual” labels next to your numbers before calculating the percentage to avoid flipping them.

Q12(a) — Dot-and-cross for BaCl₂

2 marks TB4

What you must show

Ba²⁺ and two Cl⁻ ions with full outer shells, with correct charges shown.

Mark-point checklist
  • Ba becomes Ba²⁺ (loses 2 electrons).
  • Two Cl atoms each gain 1 electron → 2 × Cl⁻.

Q13(c)(i) — Moles of M₂CO₃ in 25.0 cm³ aliquot

2 marks TB8 MS

Model answer

0.00111 mol

Method (titration stoichiometry)
  • Moles HCl = c×V = 0.100 × (22.20/1000) = 0.00222 mol.
  • Equation ratio: M₂CO₃ : HCl = 1 : 2.
  • Moles carbonate = 0.00222 / 2 = 0.00111 mol.
Common mistakes
Forgetting to convert cm³ to dm³ (divide by 1000).

Q13(c)(ii) — Identify the carbonate

4 marks TB8 MS

Model answer

K₂CO₃

Method (full chain)
  • Total moles in 250.0 cm³: 25.0 cm³ is 1/10 of the flask, so multiply by 10: 0.00111 × 10 = 0.0111 mol.
  • Molar mass = mass / moles = 1.58 / 0.0111 = 142.3 g mol⁻¹.
  • For M₂CO₃: Mr = 2Ar(M) + 60 → 2Ar(M) = 142.3 − 60 = 82.3 → Ar(M) ≈ 41.2.
  • Closest Group 1 metal is K (39.1) → carbonate is K₂CO₃.
Common mistakes
Missing the ×10 scaling from 25.0 cm³ to 250.0 cm³, which makes Mr 10× too large.

Q14 — Two anomalous properties of ice due to hydrogen bonding

4 marks TB5 AO2

Model answer (two properties + explanation)

1) Ice is less dense than liquid water, so it floats: hydrogen bonds form an open lattice that holds molecules further apart.

2) Ice has a relatively high melting point: hydrogen bonds are strong intermolecular forces, so more energy is needed to overcome them.

How to get all 4 marks
You need two properties, and for each property you must link it to hydrogen bonding:
  • Property (e.g., lower density / floats) + why (open H-bonded lattice).
  • Property (e.g., high mp) + why (H bonds are strong; need energy to break).
Common mistakes
  • Stating properties without explanation (“ice floats”) — you need the hydrogen bonding link.
  • Confusing hydrogen bonding with covalent bonding (H-bonds are intermolecular).
Exam tip
Structure words that score: open lattice, hydrogen bonds, molecules further apart, more energy needed to overcome.
