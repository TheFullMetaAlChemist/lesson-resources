01.1 Agricultural Waste Removal

Model Answer: Organic matter and Harmful microbes.

Reasoning: Agricultural processes lead to animal feces and bacteria in water; these must be removed to prevent disease outbreaks and ecological damage (eutrophication) in natural waterways.

01.2 The Screening Stage

Model Answer: Removal of large solid objects (e.g., grit, rags, and plastics).

Reasoning: This is a physical separation technique using large metal grids. If large debris enters the treatment plant, it can clog or break the mechanical pumps.

01.3 Products of Sedimentation

Model Answer: Sewage sludge and Effluent.

Reasoning: In settlement tanks, gravity causes solid matter to sink to the bottom (forming sludge), while the remaining liquid (effluent) stays on top to be treated separately.

01.4 Anaerobic Digestion Conditions

Model Answer: Absence of oxygen (anaerobic) and the use of microorganisms (bacteria).

Reasoning: Anaerobic bacteria break down the sludge. This process is useful because it produces biogas, which can be burned for heat or electricity.

01.5 Aerobic Treatment Gas

Model Answer: \(O_2(g)\)

Reasoning: Aerobic bacteria require oxygen to survive and multiply. They use this oxygen to "digest" organic matter in the effluent. The subscript 2 and state symbol \((g)\) are required for full marks.

02.1 Equipment X

Model Answer: Watch glass (or evaporating basin).

Reasoning: A watch glass provides a large surface area for the solvent (water) to evaporate, leaving the solute (salts) behind.

02.2 Mean Calculation

1. Identify anomaly: 0.34 g is significantly different from 0.12 g and 0.14 g.

2. Calculation: \(\text{Mean} = \frac{0.12 + 0.14}{2}\)

3. Final Answer: 0.13 g

Reasoning: In scientific experiments, results that don't fit the pattern (anomalies) are excluded from the mean to make the final value more accurate and representative of the true measurement.

02.3 Pure Water Prediction

Model Answer: 0 g. Pure water contains only water molecules.

Reasoning: A "pure" chemical substance is defined as having only one type of particle. If salts were present, the sample would be a mixture, not pure water.

03.1 Purpose of LCA

Model Answer: To assess the environmental impact of a product across its entire life cycle: extracting raw materials, manufacturing, usage, and end-of-life disposal.

Reasoning: LCAs allow companies and consumers to make sustainable choices by revealing the "hidden" environmental costs of a product, such as the energy used during factory production.

03.2 Comparison of Shopping Bags (6 Marks)

Point 1: Raw Materials

Polymer bags use crude oil (finite/non-renewable), while paper bags use trees (renewable). Reasoning: Trees can be replanted, but crude oil takes millions of years to form and will run out.

Point 2: Production Energy & Water

Paper bags use 4x more energy (1.92 MJ vs 0.48 MJ) and more water (1.5 vs 1 gallons). Reasoning: Higher energy consumption usually means more fossil fuels burned in power stations, leading to more CO\(_2\) emissions.

Point 3: Pollution Output

Paper produces 5x more solid waste (35g vs 6g) and has a higher carbon footprint (5.52kg vs 1.58kg \(CO_2e\)). Reasoning: Paper manufacturing is chemically intensive and releases more waste into the environment.

Point 4: Product Lifespan

Plastic bags are reused 11 times on average; paper only 3 times. Reasoning: Plastic is more durable and water-resistant, making it more functional for multiple trips.

Final Evaluation/Conclusion:

"Although plastic bags use a finite resource, they are environmentally superior to paper bags in terms of energy efficiency, lower pollution, and significantly higher reuse rates. Therefore, plastic is the more sustainable option if reused."

04.1 Finite Resource Justification

Model Answer: Copper is obtained from minerals in the Earth. No new copper ores are being formed on a human timescale, so there is a limited, set amount available.

Reasoning: "Finite" means it is being used up faster than it can be replaced. Since the geological formation of ores takes millions of years, they are considered non-renewable.

04.2 Alternative Methods

Model Answer: Phytomining and Bioleaching.

Reasoning: Phytomining uses plants to absorb metal compounds; Bioleaching uses bacteria to produce leachate solutions containing metal ions. Both allow us to use "low-grade" ores (ores with very little metal).

04.3 Evaluation of Methods

Advantage: Avoids large-scale habitat destruction and the massive energy costs of digging and transporting tons of rock.

Disadvantage: Both methods are very slow (can take months or years) and do not yet meet the high global demand for copper.

Reasoning: Sustainable methods solve the "resource" problem but create a "time" problem for industry.

04.4 Extraction Reaction

Model Answer: Reduction (displacement and electrolysis are also accepted).

Reasoning: Metals in ores are usually bonded to oxygen or sulfate. We must "reduce" them to extract the pure metal. Reduction is the loss of oxygen or the gain of electrons.

04.5 The Half Equation

\(Cu^{2+} + 2e^- \rightarrow Cu\)

Reasoning: The copper ion (\(Cu^{2+}\)) has a positive charge because it is missing two electrons. To become a neutral copper atom (\(Cu\)), it must gain two negative electrons (\(2e^-\)). Remember the acronym OIL RIG: Reduction Is Gain.
