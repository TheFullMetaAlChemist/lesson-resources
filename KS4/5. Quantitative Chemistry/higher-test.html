01.1

Calculate the mass of oxygen required

Magnesium reacts with oxygen to form magnesium oxide.

1 mark

Question focus

4.8 g of magnesium makes 8.0 g of magnesium oxide. Calculate the mass of oxygen required.

Model answer

Mass of oxygen = 8.0 − 4.8 = 3.2 g

Show mark breakdown
  • 1Correctly subtracts the mass of magnesium from the mass of magnesium oxide to give 3.2 g.

Exam tip

Use conservation of mass: total mass of reactants = total mass of products.

01.2

Calculate the mean, excluding the anomaly

Mean calculation with experimental results.

3 marks

Data

Model answer

The anomalous result is 7.06 g.

Mean = (7.36 + 7.38 + 7.38) ÷ 3
Mean = 22.12 ÷ 3
Mean = 7.373... g
Mean = 7.37 g
Show mark breakdown
  • 1Identifies or excludes the anomalous result, 7.06 g.
  • 2Calculates the mean from the three remaining results.
  • 3Rounds correctly to two decimal places: 7.37 g.

Including all four results gives 7.30 g. This loses credit because the question says to leave out the anomalous result.

01.3

Suggest why less magnesium oxide was produced than expected

Evaluating an experiment.

2 marks

Model answer

Any two suitable reasons, for example:

  • Not all the magnesium reacted.
  • Some magnesium oxide escaped from the crucible.
  • There may have been weighing errors.
Show explanation

If not all the magnesium reacts, less product forms. If powder escapes when the lid is lifted, the measured mass of product will be lower than it should be.

02

Is the mixed solution more concentrated?

Comparing concentration using mass and volume.

3 marks

Question focus

Solution A contains 5 g in 50 cm³. Solution B contains 10 g in 100 cm³. Solution B is added to solution A.

Model answer

No, the student is not correct.

Both original solutions have the same concentration, so the new solution has the same concentration after mixing.

Working

SolutionMass of copper sulfateVolumeConcentration
A5 g50 cm³ = 0.050 dm³5 ÷ 0.050 = 100 g/dm³
B10 g100 cm³ = 0.100 dm³10 ÷ 0.100 = 100 g/dm³
Mixed15 g150 cm³ = 0.150 dm³15 ÷ 0.150 = 100 g/dm³
Show mark breakdown
  • 1States that the student is not correct / concentration does not increase.
  • 2Recognises that solutions A and B have the same concentration.
  • 3Explains that the ratio of copper sulfate to water stays the same.

Common mistake

Do not just look at the total mass of solute. Concentration depends on both the mass of solute and the volume of solution.

Particles and amount of substance.

1 mark

Model answer

The Avogadro constant is the number of atoms, molecules, ions or particles in one mole.

Common mistake

A mole is not a mass. It is an amount of substance containing a fixed number of particles.

04

Describe how to calculate relative formula mass

Using relative atomic masses and chemical formulae.

4 marks

Model answer

To calculate the relative formula mass of a compound, add together the relative atomic masses of all the atoms of each element in the formula.

Show mark breakdown
  • 1Use the relative atomic masses.
  • 2Include all atoms in the formula.
  • 3Multiply by any small numbers in the formula.
  • 4Add the values together.

Example

For CO₂: C = 12 and O = 16.

Mr of CO₂ = 12 + (16 × 2) = 44

Calculate moles of sodium hydroxide

Using mass and relative formula mass.

1 mark

Given

Mass of sodium hydroxide = 0.05 g. Relative formula mass = 40.

Model answer

Moles = mass ÷ Mr
Moles = 0.05 ÷ 40
Moles = 0.00125 mol
05.2

Calculate concentration in g/dm³

Converting cm³ to dm³ first.

1 mark

Given

0.05 g of sodium hydroxide in 25 cm³ of water.

Model answer

25 cm³ = 0.025 dm³
Concentration = mass ÷ volume
Concentration = 0.05 ÷ 0.025
Concentration = 2 g/dm³

Common mistake

Do not use 25 directly if the answer is required in g/dm³. Convert cm³ to dm³ first by dividing by 1000.

05.3

Effect of changing solute mass

How solute affects concentration.

1 mark

Model answer

As the mass of solute increases, the concentration increases.

Exam tip

For a fixed volume, concentration is directly proportional to the mass of solute.

05.4

Effect of changing solvent volume

How dilution affects concentration.

1 mark

Model answer

As the volume of solvent increases, the concentration decreases.

Exam tip

Adding more solvent spreads the same amount of solute through a larger volume, so the solution becomes more dilute.

05.5

Explain what is meant by a limiting reactant

Sodium hydroxide reacts with sulfuric acid.

3 marks

Model answer

The sulfuric acid is the limiting reactant because it is used up first. Sodium hydroxide is in excess. Once all the sulfuric acid has reacted, the reaction stops.

Show mark breakdown
  • 1States that sodium hydroxide is in excess.
  • 2States that all the sulfuric acid reacts / is used up.
  • 3Explains that the reaction stops when the limiting reactant has been used up.

Common mistake

Do not just write “there is less sulfuric acid”. The limiting reactant is the reactant that runs out first and limits the amount of product made.

06

Calculate the mass of carbon dioxide produced

Thermal decomposition of calcium carbonate.

4 marks

Question focus

Calcium carbonate decomposes when heated.

CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂

A student heats 25 g of calcium carbonate. Calculate the mass of carbon dioxide produced.

Model answer

11 g of CO₂

Tabular reacting-mass method

CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂
Mass (g) 25 ?
Mr 100 56 44
Moles 25 ÷ 100 = 0.25 0.25
Ratio 1 1 1
Mass of CO₂ = moles × Mr
Mass of CO₂ = 0.25 × 44
Mass of CO₂ = 11 g
Show step-by-step explanation
  1. Calculate Mr of CaCO₃: 40 + 12 + (16 × 3) = 100.
  2. Convert the known mass of CaCO₃ into moles: 25 ÷ 100 = 0.25 mol.
  3. Use the balanced equation ratio. CaCO₃ and CO₂ are in a 1:1 ratio.
  4. So 0.25 mol of CaCO₃ makes 0.25 mol of CO₂.
  5. Calculate mass of CO₂: 0.25 × 44 = 11 g.
Show mark breakdown
  • 1Calculates Mr of CaCO₃ as 100.
  • 2Calculates moles of CaCO₃ as 0.25 mol.
  • 3Uses the 1:1 ratio to identify moles of CO₂ as 0.25 mol.
  • 4Calculates mass of CO₂ as 11 g.

Common mistakes

  • Using mass ratios instead of mole ratios.
  • Forgetting to multiply oxygen by 3 in CaCO₃ or by 2 in CO₂.
  • Stopping at moles and forgetting to convert back to mass.
07

Calculate moles of sulfur dioxide

Large masses, unit conversion and standard form.

4 marks

Question focus

In 2010, 1.9 × 10⁷ kg of sulfur dioxide was emitted by vehicles. Calculate the number of moles of sulfur dioxide. Give the answer to two significant figures in standard form.

Model answer

Mr of SO₂ = 32 + (16 × 2) = 64

1.9 × 10⁷ kg = 1.9 × 10¹⁰ g

Moles = mass ÷ Mr
Moles = 1.9 × 10¹⁰ ÷ 64
Moles = 2.96875 × 10⁸
Moles = 3.0 × 10⁸ mol
Show mark breakdown
  • 1Calculates Mr of SO₂ as 64.
  • 2Converts kg to g correctly.
  • 3Uses moles = mass ÷ Mr.
  • 4Gives the answer to two significant figures in standard form: 3.0 × 10⁸ mol.

Common mistakes

  • Forgetting to convert kg into g before using Mr.
  • Using 48 for SO₂ instead of 64.
  • Writing 3 × 10⁸ instead of 3.0 × 10⁸ when the question asks for two significant figures.
