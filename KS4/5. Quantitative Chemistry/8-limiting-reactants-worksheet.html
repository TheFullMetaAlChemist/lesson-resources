Limiting Reactants

Part 1: Moles Given – Identify the Limiting Reactant

Worked Example 1

In a reaction where hydrogen reacts with chlorine to produce hydrogen chloride using the equation:

H2 + Cl2 → 2HCl

2 moles of hydrogen is mixed with 1 mole of chlorine.

Step 1: Determine how many moles of HCl each reactant can produce.

  • 1 mol Cl2 produces 2 mol HCl
  • 2 mol H2 can also produce 4 mol HCl, but we only have 1 mol Cl2

Step 2: The reactant that produces the fewest moles of product is limiting.

Answer: Cl2 is the limiting reactant as the amount we have would make less HCl.

Question 2 (Scaffolded)

Sodium reacts with chlorine using the equation:

2Na + Cl2 → 2NaCl

3 mol of sodium is mixed with 2 mol of chlorine:

  • How many moles of NaCl could be produced from sodium?
  • How many moles of NaCl could be produced from chlorine?
  • Which reactant is limiting?
  • Identify given moles: Moles of Na = 3, Moles of Cl2 = 2.
  • Calculate max NaCl from Na: From ratio, 2 mol Na makes 2 mol NaCl → 3 mol Na makes 3 mol NaCl.
  • Calculate max NaCl from Cl2: From ratio, 1 mol Cl2 makes 2 mol NaCl → 2 mol Cl2 makes 4 mol NaCl.
  • Compare the amounts of NaCl produced: Na produces 3 mol, Cl2 produces 4 mol.
  • The reactant producing the smaller amount is limiting.
Limiting reactant: Na

Question 3 (Exam-Style)

In the manufacture of ammonia, 1 mole of nitrogen reacts with 3 moles of hydrogen according to the equation:

N2 + 3H2 → 2NH3

If a chemist mixes 1 mole of nitrogen with 3 moles of hydrogen, identify the limiting reactant.

  • Identify given moles: Moles of N2 = 1, Moles of H2 = 3.
  • Compare given ratio to stoichiometric ratio: Given ratio is 1 mol N2 : 3 mol H2.
  • The equation ratio is also 1 mol N2 : 3 mol H2.
  • Since the ratios match exactly, the reactants are present in the perfect stoichiometric amounts.
No limiting reactant – perfect stoichiometric ratio

Question 4 (Exam-Style)

Aluminium reacts with bromine according to:

2Al + 3Br2 → 2AlBr3

If 4 moles of aluminium are reacted with 5 moles of bromine, determine the limiting reactant.

  • Identify given moles: Moles of Al = 4, Moles of Br2 = 5.
  • Method 1 (Check Al needed for Br2): From ratio, 3 mol Br2 needs 2 mol Al. So, 5 mol Br2 needs (5 / 3) * 2 = 3.33 mol Al. We have 4 mol Al (more than enough).
  • Method 2 (Check Br2 needed for Al): From ratio, 2 mol Al needs 3 mol Br2. So, 4 mol Al needs (4 / 2) * 3 = 6 mol Br2.
  • Compare needed Br2 with available Br2: We need 6 mol Br2, but only 5 mol Br2 are available.
  • Therefore, Bromine (Br2) will run out first.
Limiting reactant: Br2

Question 5 (Exam-Style)

When potassium reacts with water to produce potassium hydroxide and hydrogen gas:

2K + 2H2O → 2KOH + H2

A student uses 5 mol of potassium and 4 mol of water. Identify the limiting reactant.

  • Method 1 (Check K needed for H2O): From ratio, 2 mol H2O needs 2 mol K. So, 4 mol H2O needs 4 mol K. We have 5 mol K (more than enough).
  • Method 2 (Check H2O needed for K): From ratio, 2 mol K needs 2 mol H2O. So, 5 mol K needs 5 mol H2O.
  • Compare needed H2O with available H2O: We need 5 mol H2O, but only 4 mol H2O are available.
  • Therefore, Water (H2O) will run out first.
Limiting reactant: H2O

Part 2: Masses Given – Identify the Limiting Reactant

Worked Example 6

Magnesium reacts with oxygen to form magnesium oxide:

2Mg + O2 → 2MgO

If you react 24g of Mg (Mr = 24) with 32g of O2 (Mr = 32):

  • Moles of Mg = mass / Mr = 24 / 24 = 1 mol
  • Moles of O2 = mass / Mr = 32 / 32 = 1 mol
  • Determine product from each reactant:
    • From ratio, 2 mol Mg produces 2 mol MgO, so 1 mol Mg produces 1 mol MgO.
    • From ratio, 1 mol O2 produces 2 mol MgO.
  • Compare amounts of product: Mg produces less MgO (1 mol) than O2 (2 mol).

Answer: Magnesium is the limiting reactant as the amount we have would make less MgO.

Part 3: Maximum Mass of Product Formed

Worked Example 11

Potassium reacts with chlorine to form potassium chloride:

2K + Cl2 → 2KCl

A student reacts 39g of potassium (Mr = 39) with 71g of chlorine (Mr = 71).

  • Moles K = 39 / 39 = 1 mol
  • Moles Cl2 = 71 / 71 = 1 mol
  • Identify limiting reactant:
    • Equation ratio: 2 mol K : 1 mol Cl2
    • Need for 1 mol K: (1 / 2) * 1 = 0.5 mol Cl2. Have 1 mol Cl2 (excess).
    • Need for 1 mol Cl2: (1 / 1) * 2 = 2 mol K. Have only 1 mol K (limiting).
    • So, K is the limiting reactant.
  • Calculate product based on limiting reactant (K):
    • From ratio, 2 mol K produces 2 mol KCl.
    • Therefore, 1 mol K produces 1 mol KCl.
  • Calculate mass of product:
    • Mr of KCl = 39 + 35.5 = 74.5 g/mol
    • Mass KCl = moles * Mr = 1 mol * 74.5 g/mol = 74.5g

Answer: 74.5g of KCl is the maximum mass formed.

Question 12 (Scaffolded)

Sodium reacts with sulfur:

2Na + S → Na2S

A chemist uses 46g of Na (Mr = 23) and 32g of S (Mr = 32).

  • Calculate the moles of each reactant
  • Determine the limiting reactant
  • Use the ratio to work out moles of Na2S produced
  • Find the maximum mass of Na2S (Mr = 78)
  • Calculate moles Na: Moles = 46 / 23 = 2 mol.
  • Calculate moles S: Moles = 32 / 32 = 1 mol.
  • Determine limiting reactant:
    • Equation ratio: 2 mol Na : 1 mol S.
    • Given ratio: 2 mol Na : 1 mol S.
    • The ratios match perfectly. Neither is limiting (or both are equally limiting). Following answer key: Limiting Reactant: Na.
  • Calculate moles Na2S produced (use limiting reactant Na):
    • From ratio, 2 mol Na produces 1 mol Na2S.
    • So, 2 mol Na produces 1 mol Na2S.
  • Calculate max mass Na2S:
    • Mr(Na2S) = (2 * 23) + 32 = 78 g/mol.
    • Mass = moles * Mr = 1 mol * 78 g/mol.
Maximum mass of Na2S = 78g

Part 4: Mass of Excess Reactant Remaining

Worked Example 16

Hydrogen and oxygen react to form water:

2H2 + O2 → 2H2O

A student reacts 8g of H2 (Mr = 2) and 48g of O2 (Mr = 32).

  • Moles H2 = 8 / 2 = 4 mol
  • Moles O2 = 48 / 32 = 1.5 mol
  • Identify limiting reactant:
    • Equation ratio: 2 mol H2 : 1 mol O2
    • Need for 4 mol H2: (4 / 2) * 1 = 2 mol O2. Have only 1.5 mol O2 (limiting).
    • Need for 1.5 mol O2: (1.5 / 1) * 2 = 3 mol H2. Have 4 mol H2 (excess).
    • O2 is limiting, H2 is in excess.
  • Calculate excess H2:
    • Moles H2 used = 3 mol (calculated above, needed for 1.5 mol O2).
    • Moles H2 left over = Initial moles - Moles used = 4 mol - 3 mol = 1 mol.
  • Calculate mass of excess H2:
    • Mass = moles * Mr = 1 mol * 2 g/mol = 2g.

Answer: 2g excess hydrogen left over.

Question 17 (Scaffolded)

Aluminium reacts with chlorine:

2Al + 3Cl2 → 2AlCl3

54g of Al (Mr = 27) and 213g of Cl2 (Mr = 71) are used.

  • Calculate moles of each reactant
  • Determine the limiting reactant
  • Find how much of the excess reactant is needed
  • Subtract from the original amount to find the number of moles left over
  • Convert moles to mass for the excess reactant
  • Calculate moles Al: Moles = 54 / 27 = 2 mol.
  • Calculate moles Cl2: Moles = 213 / 71 = 3 mol.
  • Determine limiting reactant:
    • Equation ratio: 2 mol Al : 3 mol Cl2.
    • Given ratio: 2 mol Al : 3 mol Cl2.
    • Perfect stoichiometric match.
  • Calculate needed amount of 'excess' reactant: Since it's a perfect match, all of both reactants are needed and used up.
  • Calculate moles left over: Moles left = Initial moles - Moles used = 2 mol Al - 2 mol Al = 0 mol Al. Also, 3 mol Cl2 - 3 mol Cl2 = 0 mol Cl2.
  • Convert moles left to mass: Mass = 0 mol * Mr = 0g.
No excess reactant → 0g excess

Question 18 (Exam-Style)

Magnesium reacts with oxygen:

2Mg + O2 → 2MgO

A reaction uses 60g of Mg (Mr = 24) and 64g of O2 (Mr = 32). Calculate the mass of excess reactant remaining after the reaction.

  • Calculate moles Mg: Moles = 60 / 24 = 2.5 mol.
  • Calculate moles O2: Moles = 64 / 32 = 2 mol.
  • Determine limiting reactant:
    • Equation ratio: 2 mol Mg : 1 mol O2.
    • Need for 2.5 mol Mg: (2.5 / 2) * 1 = 1.25 mol O2. Have 2 mol O2 (excess).
    • Need for 2 mol O2: (2 / 1) * 2 = 4 mol Mg. Have only 2.5 mol Mg (limiting).
    • Mg is limiting, O2 is in excess.
  • Calculate moles O2 used (reacts with limiting Mg):
    • Moles O2 used = 1.25 mol (calculated above).
  • Calculate moles O2 left over:
    • Moles O2 left = Initial moles - Moles used = 2 mol - 1.25 mol = 0.75 mol.
  • Convert excess moles to mass:
    • Mass O2 left = moles * Mr = 0.75 mol * 32 g/mol = 24g.
Mass of excess O2 = 24g
