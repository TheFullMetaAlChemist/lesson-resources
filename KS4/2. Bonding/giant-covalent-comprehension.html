Section A – Giant Covalent Structures

1 What is a giant covalent structure? ▶
Answer: A giant covalent structure is a huge network of non-metal atoms joined together by many strong covalent bonds in a continuous lattice.
2 Fill in the blanks about giant covalent structures. ▶
Answer: Giant covalent structures have very high melting points and are usually insoluble in water.
3 Why is diamond so hard? ▶
Answer: In diamond each carbon atom forms four strong covalent bonds in a rigid three-dimensional network, so a lot of energy is needed to break the structure, making it extremely hard.
4 Describe one use of diamond from the text. ▶
Answer (any one):
  • Used in jewellery because it is hard, shiny and transparent.
  • Used on drill bits and cutting tools to drill through hard rock.
5 Give one similarity between silicon dioxide and diamond. ▶
Answer (any one):
  • Both have giant covalent structures.
  • Both have very high melting and boiling points.
  • Both are hard solids and do not conduct electricity.

Section B – Comparing Graphite and Diamond

6 Give two reasons why graphite is slippery. ▶
Answer:
  • In graphite, carbon atoms form layers of hexagons.
  • There are no covalent bonds between the layers – only weak forces – so the layers can slide over each other easily, making graphite soft and slippery.
7 Why can graphite conduct electricity but diamond cannot? ▶
Answer: In graphite each carbon atom has one spare outer-shell electron that is not used in bonding. These electrons are delocalised and can move along the layers, allowing graphite to conduct electricity. In diamond all four outer-shell electrons are used in covalent bonds, so there are no free electrons and diamond cannot conduct electricity.
8 Fill in the blanks about bonding in graphite. ▶
Answer: In graphite, each carbon atom forms three bonds, leaving one mobile (delocalised) electron.

Section C – Fullerenes and Graphene

9 What shape arrangement of carbon atoms makes up a fullerene like C₆₀? ▶
Answer: Fullerenes are made from carbon atoms arranged in hexagons and pentagons, forming hollow cages or tubes.
10 Name one possible use of fullerenes mentioned in the text. ▶
Answer (any one):
  • As tiny cages to carry drugs to specific sites in the body.
  • As catalysts.
  • As lubricants.
11 What is graphene? ▶
Answer: Graphene is a single sheet of carbon atoms joined together in a layer of hexagons – effectively one-atom-thick graphite.
12 Give two properties of graphene described on the page. ▶
Answer (any two):
  • Excellent conductor of electricity.
  • Excellent conductor of thermal energy (heat).
  • Very strong for its mass.
  • Very low density / extremely light and thin.

Section D – Short Written Answers

13 Why are fullerenes useful for drug delivery inside the body? ▶
Answer: Fullerene molecules form hollow cages that can trap drug molecules inside. The cage can then carry the drug to specific parts of the body, releasing it where it is needed.
14 Why is graphene thought to be important for future electronics? ▶
Answer: Graphene is an extremely good conductor of electricity, very strong, thin and light. This means it could be used to make faster, smaller and more flexible electronic circuits and displays.
15 Explain why graphite and graphene can conduct electricity but most other covalent substances cannot. ▶
Answer: In graphite and graphene some electrons are delocalised, so they are free to move through the structure and carry charge. In most other covalently bonded substances all the electrons are held tightly in bonds, so there are no mobile charge carriers and they cannot conduct electricity.
