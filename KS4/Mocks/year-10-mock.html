Question 8 – Acids, Alkalis, and Titration

8.1 pH Dependence on Acid Strength and Concentration

Model Answer: "pH depends on the concentration of H+ ions in the solution, which is affected by both the acid's strength and its concentration:

  • Strength: A strong acid (like HCl) ionises completely in water, releasing all its hydrogen atoms as H+ ions. A weak acid (like ethanoic acid) only partially ionises, releasing fewer H+ ions for the same concentration. Therefore, a strong acid produces a higher concentration of H+ ions and has a lower pH than a weak acid of the same concentration.
  • Concentration: A higher concentration of any acid (strong or weak) means there are more acid molecules per unit volume. This leads to a higher concentration of H+ ions (although the proportion ionised stays the same for a weak acid) and therefore a lower pH compared to a more dilute solution of the same acid."

Guidance: Keep your answer structured. Clearly explain the difference between strong and weak acids (degree of ionisation) and the effect of concentration (amount of substance per volume). Link both factors to the resulting H+ ion concentration and its effect on pH (lower pH = higher [H+]).

8.2 Using 23.50 cm³ in Titration Calculations

Model Answer: “The student likely performed multiple titrations and obtained several results (titres). Titres are considered concordant if they are close to each other (usually within 0.10 cm³ or 0.20 cm³). An outlier is a result that is significantly different from the others. The student should exclude any outliers and calculate the mean (average) of the concordant titres. Using the average of concordant results (e.g., 23.50 cm³) provides a more reliable and accurate value for the volume of solution used at the end-point for subsequent calculations.”

Guidance: Understand the importance of repeating measurements (titrations) to ensure reliability. Know what concordant results are and why outliers should be ignored when calculating a mean titre. Accuracy and reliability are key concepts.

8.4 Why Electrical Conductivity is Zero at the End-Point

Model Answer: "At the start of the titration (assuming HCl in flask, Ba(OH)2 added), the solution contains mobile ions (H+ and Cl-) and conducts electricity. As Ba(OH)2 is added, H+ ions react with OH- ions to form water (H2O), which is molecular and does not conduct well. Also, Ba2+ ions react with SO42- ions (if sulfuric acid was used) or form soluble BaCl2 (if HCl was used). In this specific case with Ba(OH)2 and HCl, the reaction is 2H+ + 2Cl- + Ba2+ + 2OH- → Ba2+ + 2Cl- + 2H2O. The highly mobile H+ and OH- ions are removed and replaced by less mobile Ba2+ and Cl- ions, causing conductivity to decrease. If BaSO4 precipitate formed (with H2SO4), conductivity would drop more sharply as ions form a solid. Conductivity reaches a *minimum* at the end-point when the concentration of free H+ and OH- ions is lowest. It doesn't usually drop to absolute zero because water itself slightly conducts, and the salt formed (BaCl2) is soluble and ionic, though perhaps less conductive than the initial acid."
(Correction to Model Answer based on typical conductivity titrations: It usually reaches a minimum, not zero, unless an insoluble precipitate removes *all* ions, which isn't the case here).

Guidance: Explain conductivity in terms of mobile ions. Describe how the ions present change during the neutralisation reaction. H+ and OH- ions are particularly good conductors. When they react to form water, conductivity decreases. The ions of the salt formed (Ba2+, Cl-) remain, so conductivity usually reaches a minimum, not absolute zero. Explain *why* the number of mobile ions is lowest at the equivalence point.

8.5 Increase in Conductivity After Adding Extra Base

Adding further barium hydroxide (Ba(OH)2) introduces excess mobile ions into the solution, specifically barium ions (Ba2+) and hydroxide ions (OH-). General Exam Tips for You

  • Clarity & Organisation: Write your answers in clear steps. Neat diagrams, clearly labelled graphs, and fully shown calculations help examiners follow your reasoning—even if a small mistake is made, you may still earn partial credit.
  • Use of Key Terms: Always include important vocabulary like "delocalised electrons,” “ionic,” “covalent,” "oxidised,” “reduced,” "activation energy,” "catalyst," "equilibrium," "rate of reaction," "exothermic," "endothermic," etc., where appropriate. These terms show your understanding of the concepts.
  • Showing Your Work: Especially in calculations, show all your steps logically. Even if you make a numerical error, detailed working can get you marks (Error Carried Forward, ECF). Make sure every calculation is step-by-step, including units.
  • Reviewing Your Marked Papers: Compare your answers carefully with these model answers and any specific mark scheme provided. Identify common errors you made (e.g., misinterpreting the question, calculation mistakes, missing key terms, incorrect balancing, forgetting units) and make corrections. Understanding *why* you lost marks is crucial for improvement.
  • Time Management: Practice answering questions under timed conditions. Allocate time based on the marks available for each part. Ensure you allocate enough time to both answer all parts of a question and check your work at the end.
  • Self-Assessment: Use these model answers to check if you've covered every aspect of the question. If any part of your original answer was missing or unclear, use the guidance here and your textbook/notes to improve it. Try re-attempting questions you struggled with after reviewing the feedback.
  • Read the Question Carefully: Make sure you understand exactly what is being asked before you start writing. Look for command words (Describe, Explain, Calculate, Compare, State, etc.) and keywords.
