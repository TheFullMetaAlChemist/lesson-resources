Question 1 — Dodecane & Viscosity

Higher-only viscosity and data interpretation questions

Q1.1 — Complete the formula of dodecane1 mark
WorkingGeneral formula of alkanes = CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ n = 12 H = 2(12) + 2 = 26
Model answer
C₁₂H₂₆
Marking point
C12H26
Explanation
Substitute n = 12 into the alkane formula. Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons and always follow this pattern.
Common misconceptions
  • Writing C₁₂H₂₄ by using the alkene formula.
  • Adding extra atoms such as oxygen, forgetting that alkanes contain only carbon and hydrogen.
Q1.2 — Why did the student use a water bath, not a Bunsen burner?1 mark
Model answer
  • A water bath allows better temperature control.
  • A water bath prevents the dodecane catching fire because dodecane is flammable.
Marking points
  • Controls the temperature
  • Will not set dodecane on fire
Explanation
Heating flammable organic liquids with a direct flame is dangerous. A water bath heats the sample more safely and keeps the temperature steadier.
Common misconceptions
  • It was used to heat it faster.
  • It was used to make the reaction quicker.
Q1.3 — Give one control variable used in the investigation1 mark
Model answer
Volume of dodecane
Marking point
volume of dodecane
Explanation
A control variable is kept constant so the effect of temperature can be isolated. If the volume changed, the flow time could change independently of viscosity.
Common misconceptions
  • Temperature — this is the independent variable.
  • Time taken — this is the dependent variable or measurement.
Q1.4 — Predict the time taken at 75 °C using the line of best fit2 marks
Model answer
A correctly read value from an extrapolated line of best fit at 75 °C.
Marking points
  • Extend the line to 75 °C
  • Read a value from the line with an allowed tolerance
Explanation
Students should extend the decreasing trend and read off the matching time. As temperature increases, viscosity decreases, so the time should continue to fall.
Common misconceptions
  • Drawing a curve upwards instead of following the existing trend.
  • Reading from the plotted points rather than the best-fit line.
  • Giving a value that increases instead of decreases.
Q1.5 — Explain how the trend shows the effect of temperature on viscosity2 marks
Model answer
As temperature increases, the time taken decreases. Therefore, the viscosity decreases.
Marking points
  • As temperature increases, time taken decreases
  • So the viscosity decreases
Explanation
Higher temperatures give molecules more kinetic energy and weaken intermolecular forces. The liquid flows more easily, so it has a lower viscosity and a shorter collection time.
Common misconceptions
  • Saying viscosity increases with temperature.
  • Confusing rate of flow with rate of reaction.
  • Thinking particles are reacting faster.

Question 2 — Atmospheric Gases, Climate & Sulfur

Extended response and environmental chemistry

Q2.1 — Describe the test for oxygen gas and give the result2 marks
Model answer
Insert a glowing splint into the gas. The splint relights or glows more brightly.
Marking points
  • Glowing splint
  • Relights or glows more brightly
Explanation
Oxygen supports combustion. The glowing splint relights because oxygen increases the rate of combustion enough to reignite it.
Common misconceptions
  • Using a burning splint instead of a glowing splint.
  • Saying it makes a popping sound, which is the hydrogen test.
  • Saying the splint burns faster instead of relighting.
Q2.2 — Explain how the percentages of CO₂, N₂ and O₂ changed from early Earth to today6 marks
Model answer
Carbon dioxide — decreased
  • Early Earth had about 95% CO₂, but today it is around 0.04%.
  • CO₂ dissolved in the oceans.
  • It formed sediments and sedimentary rocks such as carbonates.
  • Plants and algae removed CO₂ by photosynthesis.
  • Formation of fossil fuels also locked away carbon.
Nitrogen — increased
  • Early Earth had about 3% N₂, but today it is about 78%.
  • Volcanoes released nitrogen gas.
  • Denitrifying bacteria also released nitrogen into the atmosphere.
Oxygen — increased
  • Early Earth had less than 1% O₂, but today it is about 21%.
  • Algae and plants produced oxygen by photosynthesis.
Marking requirement
For Level 3, students must discuss all three gases, give correct reasons for each change and link ideas clearly.
Explanation
Earth’s atmosphere changed because of both geological processes, such as dissolving and rock formation, and biological processes, such as photosynthesis and bacterial activity.
Common misconceptions
  • Saying oxygen increased because of volcanoes.
  • Saying nitrogen decreased.
  • Confusing photosynthesis with respiration.
  • Thinking CO₂ fell because humans reduced pollution.
Q2.3 — Give two ways to reduce methane emissions2 marks
Model answer
  • Reduce livestock farming.
  • Reduce the number of rice or paddy fields.
  • Reduce rubbish going to landfill.
  • Use methane from landfill as an energy source.
  • Reduce use of fossil fuels.
  • Reduce destruction of peat bogs.
Marking point
Any two correct responses from the official list.
Explanation
Methane mainly comes from agriculture, landfill and fossil fuel extraction. Reducing these sources lowers emissions directly.
Common misconceptions
  • Using carbon dioxide strategies such as planting trees without linking them to methane.
  • Saying recycling reduces methane without linking it to reducing landfill.
  • Mentioning renewables without linking them to fossil-fuel methane emissions.
Q2.4 — Describe how sulfur dioxide emissions are produced2 marks
Model answer
Fossil fuels containing sulfur are burned, and the sulfur reacts with oxygen to form sulfur dioxide.
Marking points
  • Fuels containing sulfur are burned
  • Sulfur reacts with oxygen to produce SO₂
Explanation
Many fossil fuels contain sulfur impurities. When they burn, those impurities are oxidised to sulfur dioxide.
Common misconceptions
  • Saying sulfur dioxide comes from burning plastic.
  • Referring to volcanic eruptions instead of human-caused emissions.
Q2.5 — Give one harmful effect of sulfur dioxide1 mark
Model answer
  • Causes respiratory problems
  • Causes acid rain
Marking point
Either valid harm, or an immediate consequence of acid rain.
Explanation
Sulfur dioxide dissolves in rainwater to form acidic solutions and it also irritates the lungs.
Common misconceptions
  • Saying it destroys the ozone layer.
  • Saying it is a greenhouse gas.
Q2.6 — Why has the percentage of sulfur dioxide in the atmosphere decreased since 1980?1 mark
Model answer
  • Less use of sulfur-containing fossil fuels
  • More use of renewable energy or electric vehicles
  • Sulfur removed from fossil fuels before burning
  • Sulfur dioxide removed from waste gases after burning
  • New legislation or regulations
Marking point
Any one valid reason.
Explanation
Air quality laws and cleaner technologies have greatly reduced sulfur dioxide emissions over recent decades.
Common misconceptions
  • Because Earth is healing itself.
  • Because carbon dioxide increased instead.

Question 3 — Crude Oil & Cracking

Resources, hydrocarbons and petrochemicals

Q3.1 — What is meant by a finite resource?1 mark
Model answer
A finite resource is one that will run out.
Marking point
A resource that will run out, or non-renewable.
Explanation
Finite resources exist in limited amounts and cannot be replaced on a human timescale.
Common misconceptions
  • Finite means expensive.
  • Finite means rare.
  • Finite means difficult to extract.
Q3.2 — Define the term hydrocarbon1 mark
Model answer
A hydrocarbon is a compound made of carbon and hydrogen atoms only.
Marking point
Made up of carbon and hydrogen only.
Explanation
Hydrocarbons are central to petroleum chemistry. They must contain only carbon and hydrogen and no other elements.
Common misconceptions
  • Saying mainly carbon and hydrogen instead of only.
  • Including oxygen- or nitrogen-containing molecules such as ethanol.
Q3.3 — Calculate the volume of petrol in 90 barrels of crude oil4 marks
Working1 barrel = 159 dm³ Total volume of crude oil = 90 × 159 = 14,310 dm³ Petrol = 31.3% Volume of petrol = (31.3 / 100) × 14,310 = 4479.03 dm³ Rounded to 3 significant figures = 4480 dm³
Model answer
4480 dm³
Marking points
  • Use 90 × 159
  • Find the correct percentage of the total volume
  • Obtain the correct intermediate value
  • Round correctly to 4480 dm³
Explanation
This tests percentage calculation and significant-figure rounding. Students need to calculate the total crude oil volume first, then apply the petrol percentage.
Common misconceptions
  • Using 31.3 dm³ instead of 31.3%.
  • Dividing instead of multiplying.
  • Rounding incorrectly.
  • Ignoring the barrel-to-dm³ conversion.
Q3.4 — Useful material produced by the petrochemical industry1 mark
Model answer
Lubricants
Marking point
lubricants
Explanation
The petrochemical industry turns crude oil fractions into useful products. In this question, the accepted answer is lubricants.
Common misconceptions
  • Choosing alloys.
  • Choosing ceramics.
  • Choosing fertilisers.
Q3.5 — Name one other method of cracking hydrocarbons1 mark
Model answer
Steam cracking
Marking point
steam cracking
Explanation
Cracking can be catalytic or steam cracking. Steam cracking is often used to produce alkenes for making polymers.
Common misconceptions
  • Writing thermal decomposition without naming cracking.
  • Saying fractional distillation.
  • Saying polymerisation.
Q3.6 — Describe a test for hydrocarbon Z and give the result2 marks
Model answer
Add bromine water. The bromine water changes from orange to colourless.
Marking points
  • Add bromine water
  • Orange to colourless or decolourised
Explanation
Hydrocarbon Z is inferred to be an alkene. Alkenes contain a carbon-carbon double bond and decolourise bromine water in an addition reaction.
Common misconceptions
  • Saying the liquid becomes brown or yellow.
  • Using universal indicator instead of bromine water.
  • Saying clear without making it clear that it becomes colourless.

Question 4 — Equilibrium & Methanol

Formulations, equilibrium, pressure and catalysts

Q4.1 — Define the term formulation1 mark
Model answer
A formulation is a mixture that has been designed as a useful product.
Marking point
A mixture designed as a useful product.
Explanation
A formulation contains components in carefully measured amounts, each chosen for a specific purpose.
Common misconceptions
  • Calling a formulation a compound.
  • Saying any mixture is a formulation.
Q4.2 — Energy transferred during decomposition of methanol1 mark
Model answer
91 kJ/mol
Marking point
91 kJ/mol
Explanation
The reverse of an exothermic reaction is endothermic and requires the same quantity of energy, but in the opposite direction.
Common misconceptions
  • Choosing a value greater than 91 kJ/mol.
  • Thinking reverse reactions use a different energy magnitude.
Q4.3 — Name of the scientist who developed the equilibrium principle1 mark
Model answer
Le Chatelier
Marking point
Le Chatelier
Explanation
Le Chatelier’s Principle predicts how equilibrium shifts when conditions such as temperature, pressure or concentration change.
Common misconceptions
  • Confusing Le Chatelier with Haber or Arrhenius.
  • Using an unrelated scientist’s name.
Q4.4 — Effect of increasing temperature on the proportion of methanol1 mark
Model answer
The proportion of methanol decreases.
Marking point
The proportion of methanol decreases.
Explanation
The forward reaction is exothermic, so increasing temperature favours the endothermic reverse reaction and reduces the methanol proportion at equilibrium.
Common misconceptions
  • Saying temperature has no effect.
  • Thinking extra heat always favours the exothermic direction.
Q4.5 — Effect of using a catalyst on the proportion of methanol1 mark
Model answer
The proportion of methanol stays the same.
Marking point
stays the same
Explanation
A catalyst speeds up the forward and reverse reactions equally, so the equilibrium position is unchanged even though it is reached faster.
Common misconceptions
  • Saying a catalyst increases yield.
  • Saying a catalyst pushes equilibrium to the right.
Q4.6 — Explain the effect of increasing pressure on methanol proportion3 marks
Key ideaCO(g) + 2H₂(g) ⇌ CH₃OH(g) Left side = 3 moles of gas Right side = 1 mole of gas
Model answer
Increasing pressure increases the proportion of methanol because the reactant side has more gas molecules than the product side, so the equilibrium shifts to the side with fewer gas molecules.
Marking points
  • Increasing pressure increases the proportion of methanol
  • There are more moles on the reactant side
  • Equilibrium shifts to the side with fewer moles
Explanation
High pressure favours the side with fewer gas particles because that opposes the increase in pressure.
Common misconceptions
  • Thinking pressure affects solids and liquids in the same way here.
  • Talking about reaction rate instead of equilibrium yield.
Q4.7 — Explain the effect of a catalyst on the rate of methanol production3 marks
Model answer
A catalyst increases the rate of reaction by providing an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy.
Marking points
  • Increases the rate of reaction
  • Provides an alternative pathway
  • Lower activation energy
Explanation
Lower activation energy means more particles have enough energy to react, so successful collisions happen more often.
Common misconceptions
  • Saying a catalyst makes more product overall.
  • Saying a catalyst increases activation energy.
Q4.8 — One advantage of using a lower temperature2 marks
Model answer
  • A lower temperature reduces costs because less energy is used.
  • Less fuel is burned, so there is less contribution to global warming or less carbon dioxide produced.
Marking points
  • Reduces costs because less energy is used
  • Or less fuel burned so less global warming / less CO₂ produced
Explanation
Lower temperatures can reduce fuel costs and environmental impact. Students must not answer this by discussing equilibrium position for this question.
Common misconceptions
  • Saying it increases yield.
  • Only saying the reaction becomes slower without explaining the advantage.
Q4.9 — Balanced equation for producing methanol from carbon dioxide and hydrogen2 marks
EquationCO₂ + 3H₂ → CH₃OH + H₂O
Model answer
CO₂ + 3H₂ → CH₃OH + H₂O
Marking points
  • 2 marks for a fully correct balanced equation
  • 1 mark for correct substances but incorrect balancing
Explanation
Carbon, hydrogen and oxygen atoms must balance on both sides of the equation.
Common misconceptions
  • Using too little hydrogen.
  • Forgetting water as a product.

Question 5 — Rates of Reaction

Cross experiment, concentration and gradient questions

Q5.1 — What is the dependent variable in this investigation?1 mark
Model answer
The time taken for the cross to no longer be visible.
Marking point
time taken for the cross to be no longer visible
Explanation
This experiment measures the time needed for sulfur to make the solution cloudy enough to hide the cross. That measured time is the dependent variable.
Common misconceptions
  • Saying cloudiness is the dependent variable.
  • Saying the rate of reaction is measured directly.
Q5.2 — Why does the mixture become cloudy?2 marks
Model answer
Sulfur is produced, and sulfur is a solid or insoluble precipitate.
Marking points
  • Sulfur is produced
  • Sulfur is a solid, insoluble substance or precipitate
Explanation
Very small sulfur particles become suspended in the liquid and scatter light, causing the mixture to look cloudy.
Common misconceptions
  • Thinking sulfur dioxide gas causes the cloudiness.
  • Saying bubbles make it cloudy.
Q5.3 — Explain the effect of changing concentration on the rate3 marks
Model answer
Increasing the concentration increases the rate of reaction because there are more particles in the same volume, so collisions occur more frequently.
Marking points
  • Increasing concentration increases rate
  • More particles in the same volume or particles are closer together
  • More frequent collisions
Explanation
Collision theory says that with more particles per unit volume, successful collisions happen more often, so the rate increases.
Common misconceptions
  • Saying particles have more energy.
  • Saying collisions are more forceful.
Q5.4 — Why insert the stopper quickly?1 mark
Model answer
To reduce loss of gas or hydrogen.
Marking point
to reduce loss of gas or hydrogen
Explanation
Magnesium reacts straight away with hydrochloric acid. If the flask is not sealed quickly, some hydrogen escapes before it can be measured.
Common misconceptions
  • To stop the reaction starting early.
  • To make readings more accurate without mentioning gas loss.
Q5.5 — Test for the gas produced and the result2 marks
Model answer
Insert a burning splint into the gas. It produces a pop sound.
Marking points
  • Burning splint
  • Hydrogen burns with a pop
Explanation
Hydrogen reacts rapidly with oxygen and gives the characteristic squeaky pop test.
Common misconceptions
  • Using a glowing splint.
  • Saying the splint relights.
Q5.6 — Determine the rate at 75 seconds4 marks
Method1. Draw a tangent to the curve at 75 s. 2. Choose two points on the tangent. 3. Calculate the gradient using Δy / Δx. 4. Give the answer in cm³/s. Example: Point A = (60 s, 28 cm³) Point B = (90 s, 44 cm³) Rate = (44 - 28) / (90 - 60) = 16 / 30 ≈ 0.53 cm³/s
Model answer
A correct tangent method with a correctly calculated gradient in cm³/s. An acceptable answer is around 0.53 cm³/s if that tangent is used.
Marking points
  • Tangent drawn at 75 s
  • Correct x-step and y-step from the tangent
  • Rate calculated as y-step ÷ x-step
  • Final rate correctly calculated
Explanation
This is the instantaneous rate, so students must use a tangent rather than points directly on the curve.
Common misconceptions
  • Using two points on the curve rather than the tangent.
  • Using 75 as the x-step instead of a change in time.
  • Forgetting the units cm³/s.

Question 6 — Gold Extraction & Calculations

Moles, particles, bioleaching and phytomining

Q6.1 — Total mass of gold in 2.48 million smartphones5 marks
WorkingMoles of Au in one phone = 1.73 × 10⁻⁴ mol Ar of Au = 197 Mass in one phone = 1.73 × 10⁻⁴ × 197 = 0.034081 g Total mass = 0.034081 × 2.48 × 10⁶ = 84,520.88 g Convert to kilograms: 84,520.88 g = 84.5 kg
Model answer
84.5 kg
Marking points
  • Calculate the mass in one phone correctly
  • Multiply by 2.48 million correctly
  • Obtain the correct total in grams
  • Convert grams to kilograms correctly
  • Accept a correct answer to at least 2 significant figures
Explanation
This is a multistep calculation: moles to mass, then scale up to all phones, then convert from grams to kilograms.
Common misconceptions
  • Forgetting to scale up to 2.48 million smartphones.
  • Forgetting to convert from grams to kilograms.
  • Rounding too early.
  • Multiplying by 2.48 instead of 2.48 × 10⁶.
Q6.2 — Total number of gold atoms in one smartphone1 mark
WorkingNumber of atoms = moles × Avogadro constant = 1.73 × 10⁻⁴ × 6.02 × 10²³ = 1.04 × 10²⁰ atoms
Model answer
1.04 × 10²⁰ atoms
Marking point
1.04 × 10²⁰
Explanation
Number of particles equals the number of moles multiplied by Avogadro’s constant.
Common misconceptions
  • Dividing instead of multiplying.
  • Using powers of ten incorrectly.
  • Choosing a value far too large, such as 10²⁷.
Q6.3 — Describe how bioleaching is used to extract gold3 marks
Model answer
Bacteria are used. They produce a leachate or solution containing gold compounds. The gold is then extracted by displacement or electrolysis.
Marking points
  • Uses bacteria
  • Produces a leachate or solution containing gold compounds
  • Gold obtained by displacement or electrolysis
Explanation
Bioleaching uses microorganisms to help release soluble metal compounds. The metal is then extracted from the resulting solution.
Common misconceptions
  • Saying the bacteria eat the gold.
  • Saying the metal is melted.
  • Saying heat or electricity is used to dissolve the metal at the start.
Q6.4 — Why isn’t phytomining widely used for copper extraction?2 marks
Model answer
  • High-grade ores are still available.
  • There is not enough suitable land available.
  • Phytomining takes a long time.
  • It produces very small yields.
  • It requires new or expensive technology.
Marking point
Any two valid reasons.
Explanation
Phytomining is environmentally friendly, but it is slow, land-intensive and often low-yield, so it is not yet the main industrial method for copper extraction.
Common misconceptions
  • Saying it is avoided because it pollutes the environment.
  • Saying fertilisers are the main reason it is not used.
