Model answer
Crude oil is produced from plankton.
Marking point
plankton
Concept explanation

Hydrocarbons

hydrocarbons

Question 4 — Resources, Potable Water, Clothing, Cracking

14 marks

Q4.1 — What is meant by potable water?1 mark
Model answer
Potable water is water that is safe to drink.
Marking point
water that is safe to drink
Concept explanation
Potable water is not the same as pure water. It has low levels of dissolved salts and no harmful microbes, but it can still contain some impurities.
Common misconception
Many students write pure water. Potable water is treated and safe, but not necessarily pure.
Q4.2 — Why pass waste water through metal grids first?1 mark
Model answer
To remove large solid objects.
Marking point
to remove large solid objects
Explanation
Metal grids remove litter, leaves, plastics and debris before finer treatment stages.
Q4.3 — Number of hats from 4.5 kg wool3 marks
Working4.5 kg = 4500 g Each hat needs 50 g 4500 ÷ 50 = 90
Model answer
90 hats
Marking points
  • Convert 4.5 kg to 4500 g
  • Divide by 50
  • Final answer = 90
Concept explanation
This tests proportional reasoning and correct conversion between kilograms and grams.
Common error
Some students invert the calculation and do 50 ÷ 4500.
Q4.4 — Advantages of poly(propene) and wool4 marks
Model answer
Poly(propene):
  • It has high insulation, so it keeps the wearer warmer in cold conditions.
  • It has low cost, meaning clothing can be produced cheaply and made widely available.
  • It has high durability, so clothes last longer and need replacing less often.
Wool:
  • Wool comes from sheep, so it is a renewable resource, making it sustainable.
  • Wool provides medium insulation, helping maintain warmth naturally.
Marking criteria
  • At least one advantage of each material
  • Linked explanation for each point for Level 2
Concept explanation
Students need to compare the materials and link each property to a practical benefit.
Q4.5 — Clothes given to others / used for insulation blocks2 marks
Model answers
  • Given to others → reused
  • Used to make insulation blocks → recycled
Marking point
Must be in the correct order: reused then recycled.
Explanation
Reuse means using the product again for the same purpose. Recycle means processing the material to make something new.
Q4.6 — Two things that can also be used to crack alkanes2 marks
Correct answers
  • A catalyst
  • Steam
Marking point
Both must be selected for 2 marks.
Concept explanation
Cracking needs heat and either a catalyst or steam. Steam cracking is commonly used to produce alkenes such as ethene.
Q4.7 — Chemical used to test for alkenes1 mark
Model answer
Bromine water
Marking point
bromine water
Explanation
Alkenes decolourise orange bromine water to colourless because of an addition reaction across the double bond.

Q5.4 — Predicted time at 75 °C2 marks
Model answer
Extend the graph line to 75 °C, then read an approximate value from the graph.
Marking points
  • Extrapolate the line to 75 °C
  • Read a value from the graph
Concept explanation
Higher temperature lowers viscosity, so the liquid flows faster and the time becomes shorter.
Q5.5 — Explain the trend in Figure 52 marks
Model answer
As temperature increases, the time taken decreases, so dodecane flows faster. This shows that viscosity decreases as temperature increases.
Marking points
  • State the trend: time decreases
  • State the effect on viscosity: viscosity decreases
Explanation
Heating gives particles more energy, weakening intermolecular attractions so the liquid flows more easily.

Question 6 — Atmospheric Gases, Climate, Sulfur

14 marks

Q6.1 — Test for oxygen2 marks
Model answer
Insert a glowing splint into the gas. The splint relights or glows more brightly.
Marking points
  • Glowing splint
  • Relights
Explanation
Oxygen supports combustion but does not burn itself. A glowing splint is used because it is easier to reignite than a burning one.
Q6.2 — Explain how percentages of CO₂, N₂ and O₂ changed over time6 marks
Model answer
Carbon dioxide:
  • CO₂ decreased because it dissolved in the oceans.
  • It formed sediments and eventually sedimentary rocks.
  • Plants and algae removed CO₂ by photosynthesis.
Nitrogen:
  • N₂ increased because volcanic activity released nitrogen.
  • Denitrifying bacteria also released nitrogen.
Oxygen:
  • O₂ increased because plants and algae photosynthesised, releasing oxygen over millions of years.
Marking requirement
For the highest level, students must address all three gases with linked explanations.
Q6.3 — Two ways to reduce methane emissions2 marks
Any two of the following
  • Reduce livestock farming
  • Reduce the number of rice fields
  • Reduce rubbish going to landfill
  • Use methane from landfill as an energy source
  • Reduce fossil fuel use
  • Reduce destruction of peat bogs
Explanation
Methane is produced mainly by agriculture, landfill and fossil fuel extraction.
Q6.4 — How sulfur dioxide emissions are produced2 marks
Model answer
Fossil fuels containing sulfur are burned. The sulfur reacts with oxygen to form sulfur dioxide.
Marking points
  • Burning sulfur-containing fuels
  • Sulfur reacts with oxygen to form SO₂
Explanation
Coal and oil naturally contain sulfur impurities.

Q6.6 — Why sulfur dioxide percentages decreased since 19801 mark
