Question 1 — Dissolved solids in sea water

Required practical skills, evaporation, concentration and evaluating variation in samples.

01.1 Using a burette rather than a measuring cylinder 1 mark

Model answer

A burette is more accurate than a measuring cylinder.

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for stating that the burette is more accurate or has a higher resolution.

Explanation

A burette has smaller scale divisions, so it allows the student to measure 10.0 cm³ more accurately. This reduces uncertainty in the volume of sea water used.

Common mistake

Saying “more precise” on its own. The idea of accuracy or higher resolution needs to be clear.

Exam tip

For apparatus questions, compare the measuring ability of the equipment directly.

01.2 Dependent variable 1 mark

Model answer

The mass of dissolved solids.

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for identifying the mass of dissolved solids as the dependent variable.

Explanation

The dependent variable is what the student measures. Here, the student changes the sea water sample and measures how much dissolved solid remains after evaporation.

Common mistake

Writing “the sea water sample”. That is the independent variable, because it is what was changed.

Exam tip

Ask yourself: “What result was recorded?”

01.3 Making sure all water has evaporated 2 marks

Model answer

Reheat the evaporating basin and contents, then reweigh them. Repeat this until the mass stays constant.

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for reheating the evaporating basin and contents.
  • 1 mark for reweighing until constant mass.

Explanation

If water is still present, further heating will cause more water to evaporate and the mass will decrease. Once the mass no longer changes, all the water has evaporated and only the dissolved solids remain.

Common mistake

Saying “heat it for longer” without mentioning reweighing or constant mass.

Exam tip

“Heat to constant mass” is a key required practical phrase.

01.4 Calculating mass of dissolved solids 1 mark

Model answer

Subtract the mass of the empty evaporating basin from the mass of the evaporating basin and contents after heating.

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for subtracting the initial mass from the final mass.

Explanation

The final mass includes both the evaporating basin and the dissolved solids. Removing the mass of the empty basin leaves just the mass of the dissolved solids.

Common mistake

Subtracting in the wrong order.

Exam tip

Final mass − initial mass is a common calculation in practical chemistry.

01.5 Mean concentration calculation 5 marks

Model answer

Mean mass = (0.35 + 0.37 + 0.33 + 0.34) ÷ 4 = 1.39 ÷ 4 = 0.3475 g 10.0 cm³ = 0.010 dm³ Concentration = mass ÷ volume = 0.3475 ÷ 0.010 = 34.75 g/dm³

Final answer: 34.75 g/dm³

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for adding the four masses and dividing by 4.
  • 1 mark for calculating the mean mass as 0.3475 g.
  • 1 mark for converting 10.0 cm³ to 0.010 dm³.
  • 1 mark for using concentration = mass ÷ volume.
  • 1 mark for the final answer, 34.75 g/dm³.

Explanation

The question asks for mean concentration, not just mean mass. The volume must be in dm³ because the answer is required in g/dm³.

Common mistakes

  • Forgetting to convert cm³ into dm³.
  • Dividing by 10 instead of 0.010.
  • Giving 0.03475 instead of 34.75.
  • Calculating the total mass but not finding the mean concentration.

Exam tip

For concentration in g/dm³, always check whether the volume needs converting from cm³ to dm³.

01.6 Why samples contain different masses 1 mark

Model answer

The samples were taken from different places.

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for suggesting that the samples came from different locations.

Explanation

Different parts of the sea can contain different amounts of dissolved salts and minerals. This could be due to differences in evaporation, river input, pollution or local conditions.

Common mistake

Saying the student measured them wrongly. The question asks for a scientific reason why the samples might actually differ.

Exam tip

“Suggest” questions often want a plausible reason based on the context.

Question 2 — Hydrocarbon fuels

Fractional distillation, crude oil fractions, alkanes and complete combustion.

02.1 Naming the process 1 mark

Model answer

Fractional distillation.

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for naming fractional distillation.

Explanation

Crude oil is separated into fractions by heating it and allowing hydrocarbons with different boiling points to condense at different levels in the fractionating column.

Common mistake

Writing “cracking”. Cracking breaks large hydrocarbons into smaller ones; it does not separate crude oil into fractions.

Exam tip

A fractionating column with crude oil entering from a heater almost always means fractional distillation.

02.2 Changes of state 2 marks

Model answer

Heater: evaporation
Column: condensation

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for evaporation or boiling in the heater.
  • 1 mark for condensation in the column.

Explanation

In the heater, crude oil is heated so hydrocarbons change from liquid to gas. In the column, the vapours cool and change from gas back into liquid fractions.

Common mistake

Writing “melting” in the heater. Crude oil is already a liquid, so the relevant change is liquid to gas.

Exam tip

State changes are often worth easy marks: liquid → gas is evaporation; gas → liquid is condensation.

02.3 Fuel obtained at B 1 mark

Model answer

Petrol.

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for petrol.

Explanation

The column is cooler at the top and hotter at the bottom. Petroleum gases leave at the very top, and petrol condenses just below them because it has a low boiling point range of 40–110 °C.

Common mistake

Choosing kerosene because it is also relatively high in the column. Kerosene condenses lower than petrol because it has a higher boiling point range.

Exam tip

Lower boiling point = collected higher up the column.

02.4 Increasing flammability 1 mark

Model answer

Heavy fuel oil → diesel oil → kerosene

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for selecting this order.

Explanation

Larger molecules have higher boiling points and are less flammable. Heavy fuel oil has the highest boiling point range, so it is the least flammable. Kerosene has the lowest boiling point range of the three, so it is the most flammable.

Common mistake

Thinking heavier fuels burn more easily because they contain more carbon. In GCSE chemistry, smaller hydrocarbons are generally more flammable.

Exam tip

For crude oil fractions: shorter molecules are more volatile and more flammable.

02.5 Molecular formula from model 1 mark

Model answer

C₄H₁₀

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for C₄H₁₀.

Explanation

The model shows four carbon atoms joined in a chain. Counting the hydrogens gives ten hydrogen atoms.

Common mistake

Missing hidden hydrogens or counting bonds instead of atoms.

Exam tip

For alkanes, use the formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂. If n = 4, the formula is C₄H₁₀.

02.6 Naming the hydrocarbon 1 mark

Model answer

Butane.

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for butane.

Explanation

A hydrocarbon with four carbon atoms and only single bonds is an alkane. The alkane with four carbon atoms is butane.

Common mistake

Writing “butene”. Butene would have a carbon-carbon double bond and the formula C₄H₈.

Exam tip

Meth-, eth-, prop-, but- = 1, 2, 3, 4 carbons. -ane = single bonds only.

02.7 Complete combustion equation 2 marks

Model answer

C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for 3CO₂.
  • 1 mark for 4H₂O.

Explanation

There are 3 carbon atoms in C₃H₈, so 3CO₂ are needed. There are 8 hydrogen atoms, so 4H₂O are needed. This gives 10 oxygen atoms on the right, so 5O₂ are needed on the left, which was already shown in the question.

Common mistake

Balancing hydrogen first and forgetting to check carbon.

Exam tip

For hydrocarbon combustion, balance in this order: carbon, hydrogen, oxygen.

Question 3 — Aluminium and wood doors

Using resources, corrosion, formulations and evaluation.

03.1 Evaluating aluminium and wood for doors 6 marks

Model answer

Aluminium and wood both have advantages and disadvantages for making doors. Aluminium is stronger than wood, with a relative strength of 1.3 compared with 1.0, so it is less likely to be damaged. Aluminium also does not need protection to prevent corrosion, whereas wood needs painting to prevent rotting, so aluminium would require less maintenance.

However, aluminium is much more expensive, with a relative cost of 87 compared with 1 for wood. This means wood would be much cheaper for producing large numbers of doors. Wood also comes from trees, which are a renewable resource if they are replanted, whereas aluminium comes from metal ore in rock, which is finite and has to be mined. Mining can cause environmental damage such as noise, dust and habitat destruction.

Both materials can be disposed of usefully. Aluminium can be melted to make new products, conserving resources. Wood can be shredded into wood chips for fuel, although burning wood releases carbon dioxide.

Overall, wood is probably the better choice if cost and renewable raw materials are the main priorities. However, aluminium may be better where strength and low maintenance are more important.

How the marks are awarded

This is a level-of-response question.

  • Level 3, 5–6 marks: A clear judgement is given and strongly supported by a range of logically linked reasons.
  • Level 2, 3–4 marks: Some linked reasons are given, possibly with a simple judgement.
  • Level 1, 1–2 marks: Relevant points are made, but they are not well linked.

Explanation

“Evaluate” means students must do more than list facts. They need to compare aluminium and wood, explain the importance of the data, and reach a supported judgement.

Common mistakes

  • Only describing aluminium.
  • Only describing wood.
  • Listing the table without explaining why the points matter.
  • Giving no final judgement.
  • Saying aluminium rusts. Aluminium corrodes, but it has a protective oxide layer.

Exam tip

For 6-mark evaluation questions, use: advantage of A → disadvantage of A → advantage of B → disadvantage of B → justified conclusion.

03.2 How the oxide coating protects aluminium 2 marks

Model answer

The oxide coating acts as a barrier. It prevents oxygen and water from reaching the aluminium.

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for saying the oxide coating acts as a barrier.
  • 1 mark for saying it prevents air/oxygen/water reaching the aluminium.

Explanation

Corrosion needs substances such as oxygen and water to reach the metal surface. The aluminium oxide layer blocks these substances, so the aluminium underneath is protected.

Common mistake

Saying the oxide coating is “sacrificial protection”. That is not correct here.

Exam tip

Do not write “rusting” for aluminium. Rusting refers specifically to iron.

03.3 Composition of paint 1 mark

Model answer

A fixed mass of pigment and a fixed mass of liquid.

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for selecting this statement.

Explanation

A formulation is a mixture designed with specific amounts of each component so that it has useful properties. Paint must contain controlled amounts of pigment and liquid to give the correct colour, coverage and texture.

Common mistake

Thinking “formulation” means any variable mixture. In chemistry, formulations have carefully measured components.

Exam tip

A formulation is a useful mixture with components in fixed proportions.

Question 4 — The Earth’s atmosphere

Early atmosphere, carbon dioxide changes, graph skills and photosynthesis.

04.1 How nitrogen changed in the early atmosphere 2 marks

Model answer

The percentage of nitrogen increased because of intense volcanic activity.

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for stating that nitrogen increased.
  • 1 mark for linking this to volcanic activity.

Explanation

The Earth’s early atmosphere formed mainly from gases released by volcanoes. Nitrogen built up over time because it is relatively unreactive and accumulated in the atmosphere.

Common mistake

Saying oxygen increased. Oxygen was present only in very small amounts in the early atmosphere.

Exam tip

For atmosphere questions: early atmosphere = volcanic activity; later oxygen increase = photosynthesis.

04.2 Why carbon dioxide changed between 4.5 and 3.0 billion years ago 2 marks

Model answer

  1. Carbon dioxide dissolved in oceans/water.
  2. Carbonates formed sediments/precipitates.

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for carbon dioxide dissolving in oceans/water.
  • 1 mark for carbonate sediment formation.

Explanation

As the Earth cooled, oceans formed. Carbon dioxide dissolved in the oceans and reacted to form carbonate compounds. These eventually became sedimentary rocks such as limestone, removing carbon dioxide from the atmosphere.

Common mistakes

  • Writing “photosynthesis” — this was not credited for this time period in the mark scheme.
  • Saying “shells of sea creatures formed” instead of carbonate sediments.

Exam tip

Check the time period carefully. Early atmosphere questions often separate cooling/oceans/carbonates from later photosynthesis.

04.3 Plotting data and drawing a line of best fit 2 marks

Model answer guidance

The four points should be plotted accurately:

  • (4.5, 98)
  • (4.0, 54)
  • (3.5, 30)
  • (3.0, 21)

Then draw a smooth decreasing line of best fit through the points.

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for all four points plotted correctly.
  • 1 mark for an appropriate line of best fit.

Explanation

This graph shows the percentage of carbon dioxide decreasing over time in the Earth’s atmosphere.

Common mistakes

  • Joining points with a ruler instead of drawing a smooth trend line.
  • Plotting points backwards because the x-axis decreases from left to right.

Exam tip

Always check whether the x-axis increases or decreases before plotting.

04.4 How carbon dioxide and oxygen changed 3 marks

Model answer

Carbon dioxide decreased and oxygen increased because of photosynthesis by algae/plants.

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for stating carbon dioxide decreased and oxygen increased.
  • 1 mark for photosynthesis.
  • 1 mark for algae, plants or cyanobacteria.

Explanation

Photosynthetic organisms removed carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and released oxygen. Over millions of years, this caused oxygen levels to rise and carbon dioxide levels to fall.

Common mistakes

  • Referring to respiration instead of photosynthesis.
  • Only mentioning oxygen increasing without mentioning carbon dioxide decreasing.

Exam tip

Photosynthesis uses carbon dioxide and releases oxygen. This is a very common GCSE atmosphere question.

Question 5 — Unsaturated hydrocarbons

Alkenes, trends in boiling point, chlorine tests, addition reactions and gas volume calculations.

05.1 Estimating boiling point X 1 mark

Model answer

Approximately −6 °C.

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for a value between +4 °C and −16 °C.

Explanation

As the size of the hydrocarbon molecules increases, boiling point increases steadily. C₄H₈ should therefore have a boiling point between C₃H₆ at −47 °C and C₅H₁₀ at 30 °C.

Common mistake

Giving a value outside the trend.

Exam tip

Use the pattern in the table, not exact arithmetic.

05.2 Product when ethene reacts with steam 1 mark

Model answer

Ethanol.

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for ethanol.

Explanation

Ethene reacts with steam in a hydration reaction to produce ethanol.

C₂H₄ + H₂O → C₂H₅OH

Common mistake

Writing “ethane”. Adding water does not produce an alkane.

Exam tip

Alkene + steam → alcohol.

05.3 Test for chlorine 2 marks

Model answer

Test: Use damp litmus paper.
Result: The litmus paper is bleached/turns white.

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for damp litmus paper.
  • 1 mark for bleaching/turning white.

Explanation

Chlorine first turns damp blue litmus red because it is acidic, then bleaches it white. The key observation is bleaching.

Common mistake

Only writing “turns red”. The key identifying feature is bleaching.

Exam tip

Chlorine = bleaches damp litmus paper.

05.4 Displayed formula after reaction with chlorine 1 mark

Model answer

The correct structure is the one where the double bond has opened and one chlorine atom has added to each of the two carbon atoms that were double-bonded.

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for selecting the correct displayed formula.

Explanation

Alkenes undergo addition reactions. The C=C double bond breaks and chlorine atoms add across the bond.

Common mistakes

  • Choosing substitution products.
  • Keeping the double bond in the product.

Exam tip

Addition reactions: the double bond disappears and atoms add across the bond.

05.5 Naming C₃H₆ 1 mark

Model answer

Propene.

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for propene.

Explanation

C₃H₆ fits the alkene formula CₙH₂ₙ. Three carbons gives “prop-”, and a double bond gives “-ene”.

Common mistake

Writing propane. Propane is C₃H₈.

Exam tip

-ane = alkane. -ene = alkene.

05.6 Smoky flame 1 mark

Model answer

Because of incomplete combustion.

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for incomplete combustion.

Explanation

Unsaturated hydrocarbons often burn with smoky flames because incomplete combustion produces tiny carbon particles, called soot.

Common mistake

Writing “not enough oxygen” alone. The mark scheme specifically credits incomplete combustion.

Exam tip

Smoky flame = soot = incomplete combustion.

05.7 Gas volume calculation for C₃H₆ 5 marks

Model answer

Mr of C₃H₆ = (3 × 12) + (6 × 1) = 36 + 6 = 42 Moles = mass ÷ Mr = 2.1 ÷ 42 = 0.05 mol Volume of 1 mole = 24 dm³ Volume = 24 × 0.05 = 1.2 dm³ 1.2 dm³ × 1000 = 1200 cm³

Final answer: 1200 cm³

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for converting 24 dm³ to 24 000 cm³, or converting the final answer later.
  • 1 mark for Mr = 42.
  • 1 mark for moles = 0.05.
  • 1 mark for multiplying by gas volume.
  • 1 mark for correct final answer.

Explanation

At room temperature and pressure, 1 mole of any gas occupies 24 dm³. First find the number of moles, then use the molar gas volume and convert the units into cm³.

Common mistakes

  • Forgetting to convert dm³ to cm³.
  • Using 24 cm³ instead of 24 dm³.
  • Calculating Mr incorrectly.

Exam tip

For gas calculations: find Mr, find moles, use molar gas volume, then check units carefully.

Question 6 — Analysis of ions

Metal ion tests, flame tests, halide tests, carbonate tests and weak acids.

06.1 Another ion giving a white precipitate with sodium hydroxide 1 mark

Model answer

Calcium ions, magnesium ions or zinc ions.

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for any correct example: Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺ or Zn²⁺.

Explanation

Several metal ions produce white precipitates with sodium hydroxide solution.

Common mistake

Giving aluminium ions again. The question asks for another ion.

Exam tip

Learn the common precipitate colours: Cu²⁺ = blue; Fe²⁺ = green; Fe³⁺ = brown; Al³⁺/Zn²⁺/Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ = white.

06.2 Confirming aluminium ions 2 marks

Model answer

Test: Add excess sodium hydroxide solution.
Result: The white precipitate dissolves to form a colourless solution.

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for adding excess sodium hydroxide.
  • 1 mark for the precipitate dissolving.

Explanation

Aluminium hydroxide dissolves in excess sodium hydroxide, unlike some other white precipitates.

Common mistake

Only saying “a white precipitate forms”. That does not confirm aluminium because other ions also produce white precipitates.

Exam tip

“Excess sodium hydroxide” is the key phrase for aluminium ion confirmation.

06.3 Orange-red flame test ion 1 mark

Model answer

Ca²⁺

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for Ca²⁺.

Explanation

Calcium ions produce an orange-red or brick-red flame in flame tests.

Common mistake

Writing “calcium” instead of the ion symbol.

Exam tip

Memorise flame test colours: Li⁺ = crimson, Na⁺ = yellow, K⁺ = lilac, Ca²⁺ = orange-red, Cu²⁺ = green.

06.4 White precipitate with silver nitrate 1 mark

Model answer

Cl⁻

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for Cl⁻.

Explanation

Silver nitrate tests for halide ions: chloride gives a white precipitate, bromide gives a cream precipitate, and iodide gives a yellow precipitate.

Common mistake

Writing “chlorine”. The ion is chloride, Cl⁻.

Exam tip

Silver nitrate = halide test.

06.5 Testing gas from carbonate ions 2 marks

Model answer

Test: Bubble the gas through limewater.
Result: The limewater turns milky/cloudy.

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for the limewater test.
  • 1 mark for limewater turning milky/cloudy.

Explanation

Carbonates react with acids to produce carbon dioxide gas. Carbon dioxide turns limewater cloudy because calcium carbonate forms.

Common mistake

Saying “the gas relights a glowing splint”. That tests for oxygen, not carbon dioxide.

Exam tip

Carbonate + acid → carbon dioxide → limewater cloudy.

06.6 Why ethanoic acid produced bubbles more slowly 4 marks

Model answer

The rate of reaction is lower because ethanoic acid is a weak acid. Weak acids are only partially ionised, so the concentration of hydrogen ions is lower. This means the frequency of collisions is lower, so bubbles are produced more slowly.

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for the lower reaction rate.
  • 1 mark for ethanoic acid being weaker.
  • 1 mark for lower hydrogen ion concentration or partial ionisation.
  • 1 mark for lower frequency of collisions.

Explanation

Nitric acid is a strong acid and fully ionises, producing many hydrogen ions. Ethanoic acid only partially ionises, so fewer hydrogen ions are available to react with carbonate ions.

Common mistakes

  • Saying weak acids are dilute. Weak and dilute are different ideas.
  • Saying the particles have less energy.

Exam tip

Weak acids are partially ionised, have a lower H⁺ concentration and often react more slowly than strong acids of the same concentration.

Question 7 — Polymers

Condensation polymers, amino acids, addition polymerisation and thermosetting polymers.

07.1 Naming compound A and compound B 2 marks

Model answer

A: ethanediol
B: hexanedioic acid

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for ethanediol.
  • 1 mark for hexanedioic acid.

Explanation

Compound A contains two alcohol groups, so it is a diol. It has two carbon atoms, giving the name ethanediol. Compound B contains two carboxylic acid groups, so it is a dicarboxylic acid. It has six carbon atoms in total, giving the name hexanedioic acid.

Common mistakes

  • Writing ethanol instead of ethanediol.
  • Forgetting “dioic acid” for the second compound.

Exam tip

Count carbon atoms carefully and identify functional groups first.

07.2 Type of condensation polymer produced 1 mark

Model answer

Polyester.

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for polyester.

Explanation

A polyester forms when a diol reacts with a dicarboxylic acid. The monomers join together using ester links.

Common mistake

Writing “addition polymer”. This is condensation polymerisation because a small molecule is removed.

Exam tip

Alcohol + carboxylic acid → ester linkage → polyester.

07.3 Small molecule lost 1 mark

Model answer

Water.

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for water or H₂O.

Explanation

Condensation reactions remove a small molecule when bonds form between monomers. In polyester formation, this small molecule is water.

Common mistake

Writing hydrogen.

Exam tip

Condensation polymerisation almost always removes a small molecule such as water.

07.4 Polymer produced when glycine polymerises 1 mark

Model answer

Polypeptide.

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for polypeptide or polyamide.

Explanation

Glycine is an amino acid. Amino acids join together through peptide bonds to form polypeptides.

Common mistake

Writing “protein”. Proteins are made from amino acids, but the polymer type credited here is polypeptide or polyamide.

Exam tip

Amino acids form peptide bonds and polypeptides.

07.5 Displayed structural formula of glycine 1 mark

Model answer

The completed structure should show an amino group and a carboxylic acid group attached to the same carbon chain.

NH₂CH₂COOH

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for the correct displayed structure of glycine.

Explanation

Glycine is the simplest amino acid. It contains an amino group, −NH₂, and a carboxylic acid group, −COOH.

Common mistakes

  • Missing the OH in the carboxylic acid group.
  • Drawing incorrect bonding around carbon.

Exam tip

Carbon forms four bonds. Always check bonding carefully in displayed formula questions.

07.6 Polymer made from different amino acids 1 mark

Model answer

Protein.

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for protein.

Explanation

Proteins are polymers made from many amino acids linked together by peptide bonds.

Common mistake

Choosing DNA, starch or cellulose.

Exam tip

Proteins are natural condensation polymers made from amino acids.

07.7 Poly(tetrafluoroethene) repeating unit 3 marks

Model answer guidance

The repeating unit must show four C–F bonds, one C–C single bond, continuation bonds through the brackets and an n outside the brackets.

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for four C–F bonds and one C–C bond.
  • 1 mark for two single continuation bonds extending through the brackets.
  • 1 mark for n after the polymer repeating unit.

Explanation

In addition polymerisation, the double bond opens, monomers join together and no atoms are lost. The polymer repeating unit for PTFE contains only single bonds.

Common mistakes

  • Leaving the double bond in the polymer.
  • Forgetting the continuation bonds.
  • Forgetting the n.

Exam tip

For addition polymers: open the double bond, draw brackets, add continuation bonds, then put n outside.

07.8 Structure of a thermosetting polymer 2 marks

Model answer

Thermosetting polymers have long chain molecules with covalent bonds or cross-links between the chains.

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for long chain molecules.
  • 1 mark for covalent bonds or cross-links between the chains.

Explanation

The strong covalent cross-links prevent the chains from sliding past each other when heated, so thermosetting polymers do not soften easily.

Common mistake

Referring only to intermolecular forces.

Exam tip

Thermosetting polymers have lots of cross-links, are rigid and do not melt easily.

Question 8 — Rates of reaction

Hydrogen peroxide decomposition, catalysts, reaction profiles, graph gradients and temperature.

08.1 Why the flask and contents lost mass 2 marks

Model answer

A gas, oxygen, was produced and escaped from the flask.

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for gas or oxygen being produced.
  • 1 mark for the gas escaping from the flask.

Explanation

Hydrogen peroxide decomposes to produce water and oxygen. As oxygen leaves the flask, the mass decreases.

2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂

Common mistake

Saying the liquid evaporated significantly. The mass loss is due to oxygen gas escaping.

Exam tip

If mass decreases during a reaction, look for a gas escaping.

08.2 Why catalysts A and B give different rates 3 marks

Model answer

Catalysts provide a different pathway for the reaction. Catalyst B gives a lower activation energy, so the rate of reaction is higher using catalyst B.

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for catalysts providing a different pathway.
  • 1 mark for the activation energy using B being lower.
  • 1 mark for the reaction rate being higher using B.

Explanation

A lower activation energy means more particles have enough energy for successful collisions, so the reaction happens faster.

Common mistakes

  • Saying catalysts increase the energy of particles.
  • Saying catalysts are used up.

Exam tip

Catalysts lower activation energy, increase rate and are not used up.

08.3 Determining rate at 60 seconds 5 marks

Model answer guidance

  1. Draw a tangent to the curve at 60 seconds.
  2. Choose two well-spaced points on the tangent.
  3. Calculate the gradient using change in y divided by change in x.
Rate = Δy ÷ Δx A suitable answer is approximately: 7 × 10⁻³ g/s

Acceptable answers vary depending on the tangent drawn.

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for drawing a tangent at 60 seconds.
  • 1 mark for selecting x and y values from the tangent.
  • 1 mark for using gradient = y step ÷ x step.
  • 1 mark for a correct calculation of rate.
  • 1 mark for giving the answer in standard form.

Explanation

The gradient of a tangent gives the instantaneous rate of reaction at one specific time. Because the question asks for the rate at 60 seconds, the tangent must be drawn at 60 seconds rather than using the whole curve.

Common mistakes

  • Using the whole curve instead of a tangent.
  • Using points from the curve instead of from the tangent.
  • Forgetting to give the answer in standard form.

Exam tip

For graph rate questions: tangent = instantaneous rate; gradient = rate.

08.4 How temperature affects rate of decomposition 3 marks

Model answer

The rate of decomposition increases at higher temperatures because particles have more energy and collide more frequently. A greater proportion of collisions have enough energy to exceed the activation energy.

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for the rate increasing at higher temperatures.
  • 1 mark for particles having more energy or moving faster.
  • 1 mark for more frequent collisions or a greater proportion of collisions exceeding activation energy.

Explanation

Increasing temperature increases kinetic energy. Particles move faster, collide more often and more collisions are successful.

Common mistakes

  • Saying temperature lowers activation energy.
  • Only mentioning faster movement without linking to collisions.

Exam tip

Higher temperature means more energy, more frequent collisions and more successful collisions.

Question 9 — Ammonia and the Haber process

Equilibrium, pressure, catalysts, separating ammonia and sustainability.

09.1 Why a high temperature is used 3 marks

Model answer

The forward reaction is endothermic, so a high temperature shifts the equilibrium to the right and increases the yield of products. A high temperature also increases the rate of reaction.

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for the forward reaction being endothermic.
  • 1 mark for the equilibrium position shifting to the right.
  • 1 mark for increased yield of products, or increased rate of reaction.

Explanation

An endothermic reaction absorbs heat, so increasing temperature favours the forward reaction according to Le Chatelier’s principle.

Common mistake

Saying high temperature always increases yield without linking this to the reaction being endothermic.

Exam tip

Temperature: endothermic forward reaction → favours products; exothermic forward reaction → favours reactants.

09.2 Why low pressure is used 2 marks

Model answer

Low pressure shifts the equilibrium to the right because there are more moles of gas on the right-hand side.

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for low pressure shifting the equilibrium position to the right.
  • 1 mark for there being more moles or molecules on the right.

Explanation

CH₄ + H₂O ⇌ CO + 3H₂ Left side = 2 moles of gas Right side = 4 moles of gas

Lower pressure favours the side with more gas molecules, so the equilibrium shifts to the right.

Common mistake

Mixing this reaction up with the Haber process itself. This question is about producing hydrogen from methane and steam.

Exam tip

Pressure favours the side with fewer gas moles at high pressure and more gas moles at low pressure.

09.3 Effect of nickel catalyst on equilibrium 1 mark

Model answer

No effect on equilibrium position.

How the mark is awarded

1 mark for no effect on equilibrium position.

Explanation

Catalysts increase the rate of both the forward and reverse reactions equally. They help equilibrium to be reached faster, but they do not change the position of equilibrium.

Common mistake

Saying the equilibrium shifts to the right.

Exam tip

Catalysts only affect how quickly equilibrium is reached.

09.4 Volume of air needed to obtain nitrogen 2 marks

Air is approximately 80% nitrogen. Volume of air = (50 × 100) ÷ 80 = 62.5 dm³

Final answer: 62.5 dm³

  • 1 mark for the correct calculation setup.
  • 1 mark for the final answer, 62.5 dm³.

If air is approximately 80% nitrogen, more than 50 dm³ of air is needed to obtain 50 dm³ of nitrogen.

Multiplying by 0.8 instead of dividing by 0.8.

Exam tip

If only part of a mixture is useful, divide by the percentage as a decimal or fraction.

09.5 Separating ammonia at X 3 marks

Model answer

The reaction mixture is cooled so that ammonia condenses or liquefies, but nitrogen and hydrogen remain as gases.

How the marks are awarded

  • 1 mark for cooling the reaction mixture.
  • 1 mark for ammonia condensing or liquefying.
  • 1 mark for nitrogen and hydrogen remaining as gases.

Explanation

Ammonia has a higher boiling point than nitrogen and hydrogen, so it liquefies first when the mixture is cooled. The unreacted nitrogen and hydrogen can be recycled.

Common mistake

Saying filtration is used. Filtration separates an insoluble solid from a liquid, not a mixture of gases.

Exam tip

Think about boiling points when separating gases.

09.6 Why nitrogen use is more sustainable than hydrogen use 3 marks

Model answer

  1. Nitrogen is abundant in the air.
  2. Hydrogen production from methane releases carbon dioxide, which contributes to climate change.
  3. Natural gas is a finite resource.

How the marks are awarded

1 mark for each correct reason, up to 3 marks.

Other acceptable points include that using nitrogen releases fewer greenhouse gases, or that nitrogen released by bacteria replaces nitrogen used to make ammonia.

Explanation

Nitrogen is available from the atmosphere and is part of a cycle. Hydrogen production often depends on methane from natural gas, which is finite, and one reaction in hydrogen production releases carbon dioxide.

Common mistakes

  • Saying nitrogen is completely renewable without explanation.
  • Talking generally about pollution without linking it to hydrogen or methane production.

Exam tip

For sustainability questions, think about renewable versus finite resources, greenhouse gases, environmental impacts and resource availability.
