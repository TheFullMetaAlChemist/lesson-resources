Q1.7

Specification 4.2.1.1/4.2.1.2 - Ionic bonding

Magnesium oxide is a compound formed from the metal magnesium and the non-metal oxygen.

Model Answer:

The magnesium atom loses its two outer shell electrons to form a Mg²⁺ ion. The oxygen atom gains two electrons to form an O²⁻ ion. The oppositely charged ions are strongly attracted to each other, forming magnesium oxide with ionic bonding. Both ions now have complete outer shells of electrons.

Tip

  3. Identify the resulting ion charges
  4. Mention that ions have complete outer shells of electrons
  5. Explain that oppositely charged ions are attracted to each other

  • Include key equipment (thermometer, balance, measuring cylinder)

  • Describe how to take measurements accurately
  • Include steps for ensuring reliability (repeat readings, calculate averages)
  • Mention safety precautions if relevant

Question 4: Zinc and Compounds of Zinc

Q4.1

Specification 4.4.2.2/4.4.2.3, RPA 1 - Making salts

Write a method for how you could obtain pure dry crystals of zinc chloride from zinc oxide and hydrochloric acid. [6 marks]

Model Answer:

  1. Add zinc oxide powder to dilute hydrochloric acid in a beaker, stirring continuously with a glass rod.
  2. Continue adding small amounts of zinc oxide until some remains undissolved after stirring (ensuring that all the acid has reacted and the solution is not acidic).
  3. Filter the mixture using filter paper and a funnel to remove the excess zinc oxide, collecting the zinc chloride solution in a clean beaker.
  4. Heat the filtrate (zinc chloride solution) gently using a Bunsen burner and heat-resistant mat, until the solution becomes more concentrated (do not boil dry).
  5. Allow the concentrated solution to cool slowly to room temperature, which will allow crystals of zinc chloride to form.
  6. Filter the crystals from the remaining solution, then pat them dry between sheets of filter paper.
  7. Place the crystals in a warm oven or leave them to dry in a warm, dry place to evaporate any remaining water.

Explanation:

The method involves two key chemical processes: first, a neutralization reaction between an acid (HCl) and a metal oxide (ZnO) to form a salt (ZnCl₂), then the crystallization of the salt by evaporation and cooling.

ZnO(s) + 2HCl(aq) → ZnCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l)

Tip for extended response questions

This is a 6-mark, level-of-response question, which will be marked according to the detail and scientific accuracy of your answer.

Level 3 (5-6 marks): A comprehensive method covering all steps accurately, in logical order, with correct scientific terminology.

Level 2 (3-4 marks): A method covering most key steps but may lack some detail or contain minor errors.

Level 1 (1-2 marks): A basic method covering some key steps but lacking detail and organization.

To maximize marks:

  1. Include all key steps in a logical sequence
  2. Use correct scientific terminology (e.g., filtration, crystallization, filtrate)
  3. Include the purpose of each step (why are you doing it?)
  4. Mention safety precautions where relevant
  5. Include specific details like "stirring continuously" or "heat gently"
  6. Demonstrate understanding of the chemistry involved (neutralization, crystallization)

Common errors to avoid:

  • Not adding excess zinc oxide to ensure complete neutralization
  • Not filtering off the excess zinc oxide
  • Heating the solution to dryness (this can decompose the salt)
  • Not including a step to dry the crystals

Q5.2

Specification 4.1.2.5/4.4.1.2 - Group 1

Explain why rubidium is more reactive than potassium.

Model Answer:

Rubidium is more reactive than potassium because its outer electron is further from the nucleus due to having more electron shells. This means there is less attraction between the nucleus and the outer electron in rubidium, making the electron easier to lose during reactions. The outer electron in rubidium also experiences more shielding from inner electrons, further reducing the nuclear attraction.

Tip

When explaining trends in reactivity of Group 1 elements:

  1. Refer to the distance between the outer electron and the nucleus
  2. Mention the effect of increased electron shells
  3. Explain how this affects the attraction between the nucleus and the outer electron
  4. Link this to how easily the atom loses its outer electron during reactions

Q6.1

Specification 4.4.3.2/4.4.3.5 - Electrolysis of molten compounds

Na⁺ + e⁻ → Na

Tip

For half equations:

  • Check what's happening - is the substance gaining or losing electrons?

Q6.6

Specification 4.4.3.4 - Electrolysis of aqueous solutions

Explain how the alkaline solution is produced.

You should refer to the processes at the electrodes.

Model Answer:

During electrolysis, water molecules are preferentially discharged at the cathode rather than sodium ions. Hydrogen ions from water gain electrons to form hydrogen gas (2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂ or 2H₂O + 2e⁻ → H₂ + 2OH⁻). This leaves hydroxide ions (OH⁻) in the solution. At the anode, chloride ions lose electrons to form chlorine gas (2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻). The remaining sodium ions (Na⁺) and hydroxide ions (OH⁻) in solution form sodium hydroxide, making the solution alkaline.

Tip

To explain the formation of alkaline solutions in electrolysis:

  1. Identify which ions are discharged at each electrode (consider reactivity rules for aqueous solutions)
  2. Write the relevant half-equations
  3. Explain what happens to the remaining ions in solution
  4. Mention how these remaining ions lead to the solution becoming alkaline (presence of excess OH⁻ ions)

Q7.4

Specification 4.3.1.2/4.3.2.1/4.3.2.2 - Chemical calculations

Calculate the minimum mass in grams of magnesium needed to completely reduce 1.2 kg of silicon dioxide.

Equation: 2Mg + SiO₂ → Si + 2MgO

Relative atomic masses (Ar): O = 16 Mg = 24 Si = 28

Model Answer:

Step 1: Calculate the Mr of SiO₂

Mr(SiO₂) = Ar(Si) + 2 × Ar(O) = 28 + (2 × 16) = 28 + 32 = 60 g/mol

Step 2: Convert mass of SiO₂ to grams

Mass of SiO₂ = 1.2 kg × 1000 g/kg = 1200 g

Step 3: Calculate moles of SiO₂

Moles = Mass ÷ Mr

Moles of SiO₂ = 1200 g ÷ 60 g/mol = 20 mol

Step 4: Use mole ratio from the balanced equation to find moles of Mg

From the equation: 2 mol of Mg reacts with 1 mol of SiO₂

So, moles of Mg needed = Moles of SiO₂ × (2/1) = 20 mol × 2 = 40 mol

Step 5: Calculate mass of Mg

Mass = Moles × Ar

Mass of Mg = 40 mol × 24 g/mol = 960 g

Tip

For calculations involving reactant masses:

  1. Write a balanced equation (given: 2Mg + SiO₂ → Si + 2MgO)
  2. Calculate the relative formula mass (Mr) or relative atomic mass (Ar) of relevant substances
  3. Convert given mass to moles (Mass ÷ Mr)
  4. Use mole ratios from the balanced equation to find moles of the required substance
  5. Convert moles back to mass (Moles × Mr or Moles × Ar) as needed
  6. Check units (e.g., convert kg to g)

Q7.5

Specification 4.2.1.4 - Covalent bonding

Complete Figure 9 to show the outer shell electrons in a molecule of Si₂H₆

(Assume Figure 9 shows the arrangement Si-Si with 3 H atoms attached to each Si)

Model Answer:

The diagram should show electron pairs (represented by dots, crosses, or lines) forming single covalent bonds:

  • One bond between the two Silicon (Si) atoms.
  • Three bonds between each Silicon atom and three separate Hydrogen (H) atoms.
  • Each Si atom shares one electron in the Si-Si bond and one electron in each of its three Si-H bonds.
  • Each H atom shares one electron in its Si-H bond.
  • Around each Si atom, there should be a total of 8 outer shell electrons (4 pairs).
  • Around each H atom, there should be a total of 2 outer shell electrons (1 pair).

Tip

When drawing covalent bonding diagrams:

  1. Determine the number of outer electrons for each atom (Si is in Group 14 = 4 outer electrons, H is in Group 1 = 1 outer electron).
  2. Draw the atoms in their correct structural arrangement (like ethane: H₃Si-SiH₃).
  3. Show shared electron pairs (bonds) between atoms using dots, crosses, or lines. Use different symbols for electrons from different types of atoms if required.
  4. Check that each Si atom has a share in 8 electrons (fulfills the octet rule).
  5. Check that each H atom has a share in 2 electrons (completes its first shell).

Q7.6

Specification 4.3.2.4/4.3.5 - Volumes of gases

Calculate the total volume of gases present after the reaction between 30 cm³ Si₂H₆ and 150 cm³ O₂.

Equation: 2Si₂H₆(g) + 7O₂(g) → 4SiO₂(s) + 6H₂O(g)

All volumes of gases are measured at the same temperature and pressure.

Model Answer:

Step 1: Use the equation to determine volume ratios (Avogadro's Law)

2 volumes Si₂H₆ reacts with 7 volumes O₂ to produce 6 volumes H₂O gas (SiO₂ is solid).

Ratio: Si₂H₆ : O₂ : H₂O(g) = 2 : 7 : 6

Step 2: Calculate volume of O₂ needed to react completely with 30 cm³ Si₂H₆

Volume of O₂ needed = (Volume of Si₂H₆) × (O₂ ratio / Si₂H₆ ratio)

Volume of O₂ needed = 30 cm³ × (7 / 2) = 30 × 3.5 = 105 cm³

Step 3: Determine the limiting reactant and calculate excess O₂ remaining

We have 150 cm³ O₂ available, but only 105 cm³ is needed. Therefore, Si₂H₆ is the limiting reactant, and O₂ is in excess.

Excess O₂ = Initial O₂ - O₂ used = 150 cm³ - 105 cm³ = 45 cm³

Step 4: Calculate volume of H₂O gas produced from 30 cm³ Si₂H₆

Volume of H₂O produced = (Volume of Si₂H₆) × (H₂O ratio / Si₂H₆ ratio)

Volume of H₂O produced = 30 cm³ × (6 / 2) = 30 × 3 = 90 cm³

Step 5: Calculate total gas volume after reaction

The gases present after reaction are the excess O₂ and the produced H₂O(g). SiO₂(s) is solid and does not contribute to gas volume.

Total gas volume = Volume of Excess O₂ + Volume of H₂O produced

Total gas volume = 45 cm³ + 90 cm³ = 135 cm³

Tip

For gas volume calculations (at constant T and P):

  1. Use the balanced equation to find the mole ratios of gaseous reactants and products. These ratios are equal to the volume ratios (Avogadro's Law).
  2. Identify the limiting reactant if initial volumes of more than one reactant are given. Calculate how much of the other reactant(s) is used and how much remains in excess.
  3. Calculate the volume(s) of gaseous product(s) formed based on the limiting reactant.
  4. Sum the volumes of all gaseous substances present after the reaction (excess reactants + gaseous products).
  5. Remember that solids and liquids do not contribute to the gas volume.

Question 8: Acids and Alkalis

Q8.1

Specification 4.3.2.5/4.4.2.4/4.4.2.6 - pH scale

Explain why the pH of an acid depends on:

• the strength of the acid

• the concentration of the acid.

Model Answer:

The pH of a solution is a measure of its hydrogen ion (H⁺) concentration. Lower pH values indicate higher H⁺ concentrations.

Strength: Acid strength refers to the degree of ionization (or dissociation) of the acid in water. Strong acids (like HCl) fully ionize, releasing all their possible H⁺ ions into the solution. Weak acids (like CH₃COOH) only partially ionize, meaning only a fraction of their molecules release H⁺ ions at any given time. Therefore, for the same concentration, a strong acid produces a higher H⁺ concentration than a weak acid, resulting in a lower pH.

Concentration: Concentration refers to the amount of acid substance dissolved per unit volume of solution (e.g., in mol/dm³). A higher concentration means more acid molecules are present overall. For any given acid (strong or weak), increasing its concentration will lead to a higher concentration of H⁺ ions in the solution (more molecules available to ionize, even if partially), thus lowering the pH.

Tip

Always remember that pH is fundamentally linked to H⁺ concentration: pH ≈ -log₁₀[H⁺]. When explaining effects on pH:

  • Strength relates to the *proportion* of acid molecules that ionize. (Strong = high proportion, Weak = low proportion)
  • Concentration relates to the *total amount* of acid molecules present per volume. (Concentrated = high amount, Dilute = low amount)
  • Both factors influence the final H⁺ concentration, and therefore the pH.
  • Lower pH = higher H⁺ concentration.

Q8.2

Specification 4.4.2.5, RPA 2 - Titration

A student titrated 25.00 cm³ of hydrochloric acid with 0.100 mol/dm³ barium hydroxide solution.

Table 2 shows the results. (Assume Table 2 shows titres like: Rough=23.90, 1=23.55, 2=23.50, 3=23.45, 4=23.50)

The student calculated the volume of barium hydroxide solution to be used in the titration calculation as 23.50 cm³.

Explain why the student used a volume of 23.50 cm³ of barium hydroxide solution in the titration calculation.

Model Answer:

The student used 23.50 cm³ because it is the mean (average) of the concordant results. Concordant results are titres that are close to each other, typically within 0.10 cm³. In this case, titres 2 (23.50 cm³), 3 (23.45 cm³), and 4 (23.50 cm³) are concordant. (Some might also include titre 1=23.55 as concordant with 23.50). The rough titration (23.90 cm³) is ignored as it is only an estimate. Calculating the mean of 23.50, 23.45, and 23.50 gives (23.50 + 23.45 + 23.50) / 3 = 70.45 / 3 = 23.483... cm³, which rounds to 23.50 cm³ (to 2 decimal places, consistent with burette readings). If 23.55 was included: (23.55+23.50+23.45+23.50)/4 = 94.00 / 4 = 23.50 cm³ exactly.

Therefore, 23.50 cm³ represents the reliable average volume required for neutralization.

Tip

In titration calculations:

  1. Identify concordant results (titres that agree closely, usually within 0.10 cm³ or 0.20 cm³ depending on precision required).
  2. Do *not* include the rough/initial titration in the calculation of the mean.
  3. Exclude any clearly anomalous results (values that are very different from the others).
  4. Calculate the mean (average) of the concordant results only.
  5. Give the mean titre to the appropriate number of decimal places (usually 2, matching burette readings).

Q8.3

Specification 4.3.4/4.4.2.5, RPA 2 - Titration

25.00 cm³ of the hydrochloric acid reacted with 23.50 cm³ of the 0.100 mol/dm³ barium hydroxide solution.

The equation for the reaction is:

2HCl(aq) + Ba(OH)₂(aq) → BaCl₂(aq) + 2H₂O(l)

Calculate the concentration of the hydrochloric acid in mol/dm³.

Model Answer:

Step 1: Calculate moles of Ba(OH)₂ used

Volume of Ba(OH)₂ = 23.50 cm³ = 23.50 / 1000 = 0.02350 dm³

Concentration of Ba(OH)₂ = 0.100 mol/dm³

Moles = Concentration × Volume

Moles of Ba(OH)₂ = 0.100 mol/dm³ × 0.02350 dm³ = 0.002350 mol

Step 2: Use the balanced equation to find moles of HCl

From the equation, the mole ratio is 2 mol HCl : 1 mol Ba(OH)₂

Moles of HCl = Moles of Ba(OH)₂ × (2 / 1)

Moles of HCl = 0.002350 mol × 2 = 0.004700 mol

Step 3: Calculate the concentration of HCl

Volume of HCl = 25.00 cm³ = 25.00 / 1000 = 0.02500 dm³

Concentration = Moles ÷ Volume

Concentration of HCl = 0.004700 mol ÷ 0.02500 dm³ = 0.188 mol/dm³

Tip

For titration calculations (like Concentration = Moles / Volume):

  1. Convert all volumes to dm³ (divide cm³ by 1000).
  2. Calculate moles of the solution with known concentration and volume (the 'known' solution).
  3. Use the mole ratio from the balanced chemical equation to find the moles of the other reactant (the 'unknown' solution).
  4. Calculate the concentration of the unknown solution using its moles (from step 3) and its volume (given in the question, converted to dm³).
  5. Pay attention to significant figures and units.

Q8.4

Specification 4.2.2.3/4.4.2.2/4.4.2.4/4.4.2.5 - Ionic compounds, Neutralisation

Explain why the electrical conductivity of the mixture was zero when the sulfuric acid had just been neutralised by barium hydroxide solution.

Use the equation for the reaction: H₂SO₄(aq) + Ba(OH)₂(aq) → BaSO₄(s) + 2H₂O(l)

Refer to ions in your answer.

Model Answer:

Electrical conductivity in solution requires the presence of mobile ions.

Initially, sulfuric acid contains mobile H⁺(aq) and SO₄²⁻(aq) ions, and barium hydroxide contains mobile Ba²⁺(aq) and OH⁻(aq) ions, so both solutions conduct electricity.

According to the equation H₂SO₄(aq) + Ba(OH)₂(aq) → BaSO₄(s) + 2H₂O(l), when the acid is exactly neutralised:

  • The H⁺ ions from the acid react with the OH⁻ ions from the alkali to form water (H₂O(l)). Water is a covalent molecule and ionises very slightly, so it is a very poor conductor of electricity.
  • The Ba²⁺ ions from the alkali react with the SO₄²⁻ ions from the acid to form barium sulfate (BaSO₄(s)). Barium sulfate is an insoluble solid (precipitate). Its ions are locked in a lattice structure and are not mobile in the solution.

At the exact point of neutralisation, there are virtually no mobile ions remaining in the mixture (only the very few from the slight ionisation of water). Therefore, the electrical conductivity drops to (near) zero.

Tip

When explaining electrical conductivity changes during reactions, especially neutralisation involving precipitates:

  1. State the requirement for conductivity (mobile ions).
  2. Identify the mobile ions present initially in the reactants.
  3. Use the chemical equation to identify the products.
  4. Explain what happens to the initial ions – do they form molecules (like water), precipitates (like BaSO₄), or remain as spectator ions?
  5. Relate the presence or absence of mobile ions in the final mixture to the observed conductivity.

Q8.5

Specification 4.2.2.3/4.4.2.2/4.4.2.4/4.4.2.5 - Ionic compounds

The student then added a further 10 cm³ of barium hydroxide solution after neutralisation.

The electrical conductivity of the mixture increased.

Give one reason why.

Model Answer:

The electrical conductivity increased because the excess barium hydroxide solution added contains mobile ions. Specifically, it introduces additional barium ions (Ba²⁺) and hydroxide ions (OH⁻) into the mixture. Since these ions are mobile charge carriers, their presence increases the solution's ability to conduct electricity.

Tip

After the neutralisation point (or equivalence point):

  • Adding more of the reagent (in this case, Ba(OH)₂) introduces an excess of its constituent ions.
  • These excess ions are mobile in the solution.
  • Mobile ions allow the solution to conduct electricity.
  • The more excess ions added, the higher the conductivity will become (up to a point).

Exam Technique and General Tips

Understanding Command Words

Command Words

Describe - Give an account of something, state characteristics and features. What does it look like? What happens?

  • Example: "Describe the structure and bonding of diamond"
  • Approach: State key facts about the topic (e.g., giant covalent, C atoms, 4 bonds each, tetrahedral) without necessarily explaining the reasons behind them.

Explain - Set out reasons for why/how something occurs. Use scientific principles.

  • Example: "Explain why diamond has a very high melting point"
  • Approach: Give reasons and link cause and effect (e.g., strong covalent bonds require lots of energy to break).

Calculate - Work out a numerical answer.

  • Approach: Show all steps of your working clearly and give your final answer with the correct units and appropriate significant figures.

Suggest - Apply your knowledge and understanding to a new or unfamiliar situation.

  • Example: "Suggest one reason why the C₇₀ molecule is suitable for this use"
  • Approach: Use scientific knowledge (e.g., fullerene structure) to make a plausible, logical suggestion even if you haven't learned that specific application directly.

Predict - Give a likely outcome based on patterns, trends, or scientific principles.

  • Example: "Predict the observation when rubidium reacts with water compared to potassium."
  • Approach: Apply known trends (e.g., reactivity in Group 1) to determine what is likely to happen.

Compare - Identify similarities and differences between two or more things.

  • Example: "Compare the properties of Group 1 metals and transition metals."
  • Approach: Point out similarities (e.g., both are metals) and differences (e.g., melting point, density, ion formation).

Tackling Calculations

Calculation Tips (The 'IDEA' method)

  1. Identify
    • What information is given in the question (values, units)?
    • What are you being asked to calculate?
  2. Decide
    • Which formula or equation(s) relate the given information to what you need to find? (e.g., moles = mass/Mr, concentration = moles/volume).
    • Do you need data from a periodic table (Ar values)?
  3. Execute
    • Substitute the values into the equation(s).
    • Show ALL steps of your working clearly.
    • Convert units if necessary (e.g., cm³ to dm³, kg to g) *before* calculating or as appropriate.
  4. Answer & Assess
    • Write down the final numerical answer.
    • Include the correct units (e.g., g, mol/dm³, cm³).
    • Check if the answer needs to be given to a specific number of significant figures.
    • Does the answer seem reasonable in the context of the question? (Sanity check).

Drawing and Interpreting Graphs

Graph Skills

  1. Plotting points: Use small, neat crosses (x) or circled dots (⊙) placed accurately.
  2. Line of best fit: Draw a single, thin line or smooth curve that represents the general trend of the points. Aim for roughly equal numbers of points above and below the line. Do *not* force it through the origin unless theoretically expected. Use a ruler for straight lines.
  3. Reading values: Read values accurately from the axes. Use a ruler to help if needed.
  4. Extrapolating lines: Extend the line of best fit beyond the plotted points if required (e.g., to find an intercept). Use a ruler and draw a dashed or distinct line for the extrapolated part.
  5. Calculating gradients (for straight lines):
    • Choose two points that are *on the line of best fit* and reasonably far apart. Do *not* use plotted data points unless they happen to fall exactly on your line.
    • Draw a large right-angled triangle using these points.
    • Calculate the change in the y-axis value (rise) and the change in the x-axis value (run).
    • Gradient = Rise / Run = (y₂ - y₁) / (x₂ - x₁).
    • Include units for the gradient (units of y-axis divided by units of x-axis, e.g., °C/g).
  6. Interpreting shape: Relate the shape of the graph (e.g., linear, curved, plateau) to the underlying scientific principles.

For Extended Response Questions (e.g., 6 marks)

Extended Response Tips

  1. Understand the question: Read it carefully. Identify the command word(s) and exactly what needs to be included in the answer. Note the number of marks available.
  2. Plan before writing: Briefly jot down the key scientific points, keywords, or steps you need to cover. Organize them logically. For method questions, think sequentially. For explanations, think cause-and-effect.
  3. Use precise scientific vocabulary: Use terms like "atom", "ion", "molecule", "electron", "covalent bond", "ionic bond", "delocalised", "rate", "concentration", "catalyst", "equilibrium", "oxidation", "reduction" correctly.
  4. Structure your answer: Write in clear sentences and paragraphs. Use linking words and phrases (e.g., "therefore", "because", "as a result", "this leads to") to show connections between ideas.
  5. Be specific and detailed: Provide enough detail to demonstrate your understanding. Refer to specific examples, data, or diagrams if relevant and requested. Address all parts of the question.
  6. Check your answer: Reread your answer to ensure it makes sense, directly answers the question, and includes the key points from your plan. Check for clarity and accuracy.

Exam Time Management

Time Management Tips

  1. Allocate time wisely: Check the total time and total marks for the paper. Aim for roughly 1 minute per mark as a guideline. Adjust slightly based on question type (calculations might take longer per mark, multiple choice less).
  2. Read questions carefully: Don't rush reading. Misinterpreting a question wastes time and loses marks. Underline key information and command words.
  3. Look for clues: Information in diagrams, tables, introductions, or previous parts of the question is often needed for later parts.
  4. Don't get stuck: If you're struggling with a question, leave it for now and come back later if you have time. Put a mark next to it so you remember. It's better to attempt all questions than spend too long on one.
  5. Attempt every question: Even if unsure, write down what you know. You might pick up partial credit, especially for method marks in calculations or relevant points in explanations.
  6. Check your work: If you finish early, use the time to review your answers, check calculations, ensure units are included, and make sure you haven't missed any questions.
