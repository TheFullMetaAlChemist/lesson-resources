Question 01.1 – What type of substance is copper oxide?

1 mark
Question

Copper oxide is insoluble. What type of substance is copper oxide?

Model answer

Base

Explanation

Copper oxide is a metal oxide, and metal oxides are bases. It is not an alkali because it is insoluble in water.

Common mistakes
  • Choosing alkali instead of base.
  • Thinking all metal oxides are salts.

Question 01.2 – Type of reaction

1 mark
Question

Copper oxide reacts with dilute hydrochloric acid to produce copper chloride and water. Tick the type of reaction taking place.

Model answer

Neutralisation

Explanation

This is an acid reacting with a base. Acid + base produces salt + water, so this is a neutralisation reaction.

Common mistakes
  • Choosing combustion because oxygen appears in copper oxide.
  • Choosing decomposition because several substances are involved.

Question 01.3 – Balanced symbol equation

2 marks
Question

Write a balanced symbol equation for the reaction between CuO and HCl.

Model answer

CuO + 2HCl → CuCl2 + H2O

Explanation

First write the correct formulas. Then balance the equation by placing a 2 in front of HCl so the number of hydrogen and chlorine atoms is the same on both sides.

Common mistakes
  • Writing CuCl instead of CuCl2.
  • Forgetting the 2 in front of HCl.
  • Changing the small formula numbers instead of balancing numbers.

Question 01.4 – Making pure dry copper chloride crystals

6 marks
Question

Describe how you could produce pure dry crystals of copper chloride from copper oxide and dilute hydrochloric acid.

Model answer

Add dilute hydrochloric acid to a beaker and gently warm it. Add copper oxide and stir. Continue adding copper oxide until no more reacts, so all of the acid is used up. Filter the mixture to remove the excess copper oxide. Pour the filtrate into an evaporating basin and heat gently until some of the water evaporates and the solution becomes more concentrated. Pour the solution into a crystallising dish and leave it to cool so crystals form. Remove the crystals and dry them.

Explanation

Excess copper oxide is added so that all of the acid reacts.

Filtering removes the unreacted solid.

Evaporation concentrates the solution.

Cooling allows crystals to form from the concentrated solution.

This is the standard method for preparing a soluble salt from an insoluble base.

Common mistakes
  • Missing out the filtering step.
  • Not using excess copper oxide.
  • Evaporating all the water away instead of allowing crystals to form.
  • Forgetting to dry the crystals.

Question 02.1 – Control variable

1 mark
Question

Give one variable the student should control.

Model answer

Any sensible control variable, for example mass of metal, surface area of the metal, volume of copper sulfate solution, concentration of copper sulfate solution, or initial temperature of the solution.

Explanation

A control variable is something kept the same each time so the test is fair. Good examples are quantities or conditions that might affect the temperature rise.

Common mistakes
  • Naming a piece of equipment instead of a variable.
  • Giving the metal type, which is the independent variable.

Question 02.2 – Independent variable

1 mark
Question

Name the independent variable in the student’s investigation.

Model answer

Type of metal

Explanation

The independent variable is the one changed on purpose. In this investigation, the student changes which metal is added to the copper sulfate solution.

Common mistakes
  • Choosing temperature rise, which is the dependent variable.
  • Writing copper sulfate solution, which is not what is changed.

Question 02.3 – Complete the equation with state symbols

3 marks
Question

Tyler added zinc powder to copper sulfate solution. The solution was decolourised and a red-brown powder formed. Complete the equation, including state symbols, for the reaction.

Model answer

Zn(s) + CuSO4(aq) → Cu(s) + ZnSO4(aq)

Explanation

Zinc and copper are solids, so they use (s). Copper sulfate and zinc sulfate are solutions, so they use (aq). The equation is already balanced in a 1:1:1:1 ratio.

Common mistakes
  • Using (l) instead of (aq).
  • Forgetting that copper is a solid product.
  • Writing the correct substances but in an unbalanced or muddled equation.

Question 02.4 – Estimated order of reactivity

2 marks
Question

Use the results to place the metals in an estimated order of reactivity.

Model answer

magnesium > zinc > tin > copper / tungsten / silver

Explanation

A higher temperature rise suggests a more reactive metal. Magnesium gave the highest temperature rise, then zinc, then tin. Copper, tungsten and silver all gave 0.0, so the results do not separate them.

Common mistakes
  • Trying to force copper, tungsten and silver into a definite order.
  • Putting zinc above magnesium.

Question 02.5 – Why the order cannot be certain

2 marks
Question

Explain why it is not possible to be certain about the order of reactivity of the metals in the table.

Model answer

It is not possible to be certain because tungsten, copper and silver all gave a temperature rise of 0.0, so from these results alone you cannot tell which of them is more reactive.

Explanation

The experiment distinguishes magnesium, zinc and tin, but it does not provide enough information to rank tungsten, copper and silver because they all gave the same result.

Common mistakes
  • Saying the whole set of results is useless.
  • Claiming the experiment was wrong rather than limited.

Question 02.6 – How to gain more information

2 marks
Question

Suggest how you could gain more information to improve your order of reactivity.

Model answer

You could make the metals react with air, water or oxygen and compare how vigorous the reactions are.

Another acceptable idea is to measure the voltage between pairs of metals dipped in an electrolyte.

Explanation

A different test could provide extra evidence to separate the metals that all gave 0.0 in the copper sulfate test. More information would allow a better estimated order.

Common mistakes
  • Saying only “repeat the experiment” without explaining how that would give new information.
  • Giving a vague answer with no comparison method.

Question 02.7 – Why copper does not make copper chloride with hydrochloric acid

1 mark
Question

Copper chloride cannot be produced by reacting copper with hydrochloric acid. Give a reason why.

Model answer

Copper does not react with acids.

Explanation

Copper is below hydrogen in the reactivity series, so it does not react with dilute acids such as hydrochloric acid.

Common mistakes
  • Saying copper is completely unreactive in all situations.
  • Blaming the acid instead of the metal’s low reactivity.

Question 02.8 – Why potassium is unsafe

1 mark
Question

It is not safe to produce potassium chloride by reacting potassium with hydrochloric acid. Give a reason why.

Model answer

Potassium is very reactive, so it is too dangerous.

Explanation

Potassium reacts extremely violently, so using it with hydrochloric acid would be unsafe.

Common mistakes
  • Saying potassium would not react.
  • Giving a vague answer like “it is harmful” with no link to reactivity.

Question 02.9 – Oxidation and reduction in terms of electrons

2 marks
Question

The ionic equation for the reaction between magnesium and dilute hydrochloric acid is:

Mg + 2H+ → Mg2+ + H2

Explain, in terms of electrons, which species is oxidised and which is reduced in the reaction.

Model answer

Magnesium is oxidised because it loses electrons.

Hydrogen ions are reduced because they gain electrons.

Explanation

Oxidation means loss of electrons, and reduction means gain of electrons. Magnesium atoms lose electrons to form magnesium ions, while hydrogen ions gain electrons to form hydrogen gas.

Common mistakes
  • Mixing up oxidation and reduction.
  • Saying magnesium gains electrons.
  • Referring only to hydrogen, instead of hydrogen ions.

Question 03.1 – What is an alkali?

1 mark
Question

What is an alkali?

Model answer

Soluble base

Explanation

An alkali is a base that dissolves in water. Insoluble bases are not alkalis.

Common mistakes
  • Choosing insoluble base.
  • Thinking any dissolved substance counts as an alkali.

Question 03.2 – Estimating hydrogen ion concentration

1 mark
Question

The pH of hydrochloric acid was 1.0 and the pH of ethanoic acid was 2.9. The hydrogen ion concentration of hydrochloric acid was 0.1 mol/dm3. Which estimate is approximately correct for the hydrogen ion concentration of ethanoic acid?

Model answer

0.001 mol/dm3

Explanation

Each increase of 1 in pH means the hydrogen ion concentration is ten times smaller. A pH of 2.9 is close to pH 3, so the concentration is about 100 times lower than 0.1 mol/dm3, giving an estimate of 0.001 mol/dm3.

Common mistakes
  • Choosing 0.1 mol/dm3 because both acids started at the same concentration.
  • Forgetting that the pH scale is logarithmic.

Question 03.3 – Why the acids have different pH values

3 marks
Question

Explain why the solution of hydrochloric acid has a different pH to the solution of ethanoic acid.

Model answer

Hydrochloric acid is a stronger acid because it completely ionises in water. Ethanoic acid is a weak acid because it only partly ionises. Therefore hydrochloric acid produces a higher concentration of hydrogen ions and has a lower pH.

Explanation

Even when two acids have the same concentration, their pH can be different if one is strong and one is weak. A strong acid releases nearly all of its hydrogen ions, but a weak acid releases only some of them.

Common mistakes
  • Saying hydrochloric acid is more concentrated.
  • Not mentioning ionisation at all.
  • Confusing strong and concentrated.
