Question 01.1 – Add the state symbols

2 marks
Question

Complete the equation by adding state symbols.

zinc ___ + copper sulfate ___ → zinc sulfate ___ + copper ___
Model answer

zinc (s) + copper sulfate (aq) → zinc sulfate (aq) + copper (s)

Explanation

Zinc and copper are metals, so they are solids and use (s). Copper sulfate and zinc sulfate are dissolved in water, so they are aqueous and use (aq).

Common mistakes
  • Using (l) instead of (aq) for solutions.
  • Forgetting that the metal products are solids.
  • Missing one or more state symbols.

Question 01.2 – Visible changes in the reaction

2 marks
Question

Give two changes that Tyler would see during this reaction.

Model answer
  • The solution changes from blue to colourless.
  • A red-brown copper solid forms.
Explanation

Copper sulfate solution is blue. When zinc reacts with it, zinc sulfate is formed, which is colourless, and copper metal is displaced. Copper is seen as a red-brown solid.

Common mistakes
  • Saying only that it “changes colour” without stating the colours.
  • Naming the new solid incorrectly.
  • Describing temperature change instead of visible change.

Question 01.3 – Type of variable

1 mark
Question

Tyler used the same volume of copper sulfate solution in each reaction. What type of variable is the volume of copper sulfate solution?

Model answer

Control variable

Explanation

A control variable is something that is kept the same in every test so that the comparison is fair.

Common mistakes
  • Choosing independent variable because it sounds scientific.
  • Choosing dependent variable because it was measured.

Question 01.4 – Measuring mass

1 mark
Question

Which measuring instrument is used to measure the mass?

Model answer

Balance

Explanation

Mass is measured using a balance, usually in grams. A pipette measures volume, a ruler measures length, and a stopwatch measures time.

Common mistakes
  • Confusing mass with volume.
  • Choosing a measuring instrument linked to a different quantity.

Question 01.5 – Most reactive metal

2 marks
Question

Identify the most reactive metal and give a reason for your answer.

Model answer

Magnesium, because it gave the highest temperature rise.

Explanation

A bigger temperature rise shows that more energy was released. This means the reaction was more vigorous, so magnesium was the most reactive metal tested.

Common mistakes
  • Giving only the metal name without the reason.
  • Choosing zinc because it also reacted strongly.
  • Talking about colour change instead of temperature rise.

Question 01.6 – No temperature rise with silver

1 mark
Question

Why was there no temperature rise when silver was added?

Model answer

No reaction occurred.

Explanation

Silver is less reactive than copper, so it cannot displace copper from copper sulfate solution. Because no reaction takes place, no temperature rise is seen.

Common mistakes
  • Saying silver is unreactive without linking this to no reaction.
  • Saying the thermometer was wrong.

Question 02.1 – Meaning of alkali

1 mark
Question

Choose what alkali means.

Model answer

Soluble base

Explanation

An alkali is a base that dissolves in water. Insoluble bases are still bases, but they are not alkalis.

Common mistakes
  • Choosing insoluble base.
  • Thinking any salt in solution is an alkali.

Question 02.2 – Ion in an alkali

1 mark
Question

Choose which ion causes sodium hydroxide to be alkaline.

Model answer

OH−

Explanation

Alkaline solutions contain hydroxide ions. These are the ions responsible for the solution being alkaline.

Common mistakes
  • Choosing Na+ because sodium hydroxide contains sodium.
  • Confusing H+ with acids and OH− with alkalis.

Question 02.3 – pH of sodium hydroxide

1 mark
Question

Suggest the pH of sodium hydroxide solution.

Model answer

13

Explanation

Sodium hydroxide is a strong alkali, so it has a high pH. A value of 13 is suitable for a strongly alkaline solution.

Common mistakes
  • Choosing 7 because it is neutral.
  • Choosing a low number linked to acids.

Question 02.4 – Name of the salt formed

1 mark
Question

Sodium hydroxide reacts with hydrochloric acid to form a salt. Name the salt formed.

Model answer

Sodium chloride

Explanation

The sodium comes from sodium hydroxide and the chloride comes from hydrochloric acid, so the salt formed is sodium chloride.

Common mistakes
  • Writing sodium hydroxide instead of the salt.
  • Writing hydrochloride instead of chloride.

Question 02.5 – Litmus and why it is used

2 marks
Question

A chemical called litmus is sometimes added to the reactants. Name this type of chemical and explain why it is used.

Model answer

Litmus is a pH indicator. It is used because it changes colour when neutralisation is complete.

Explanation

Indicators help you tell when the acid and alkali have fully reacted. The colour change shows that the solution has reached the correct point.

Common mistakes
  • Writing only “indicator” without explaining why it is used.
  • Saying “it changes colour” without linking this to the end point of the reaction.

Question 03.1 – Complete the word equation

2 marks
Question

Complete the word equation for the reaction.

carbon + ______ → ______ + ______
Model answer

carbon + iron oxide → iron + carbon dioxide

Explanation

Carbon removes oxygen from iron oxide. The iron oxide loses oxygen to become iron, and the carbon gains oxygen to become carbon dioxide.

Common mistakes
  • Writing iron ore instead of iron oxide.
  • Writing carbon monoxide instead of carbon dioxide.
  • Putting the products on the wrong side.

Question 03.2 – Gain of oxygen

1 mark
Question

In this reaction carbon gains oxygen. Name the type of reaction that occurs when a substance gains oxygen.

Model answer

Oxidation

Explanation

At GCSE, oxidation can be defined as the gain of oxygen.

Common mistakes
  • Writing reduction.
  • Confusing oxidation with burning only.

Question 03.3 – Why carbon extracts iron

2 marks
Question

Explain why carbon is used to extract iron. Include details about reactivity and displacement in your answer.

Model answer

Carbon is more reactive than iron, so it can displace iron from iron oxide.

Explanation

A more reactive element can remove oxygen from the oxide of a less reactive element. Because carbon is above iron in the reactivity series, it can extract iron from iron oxide.

Common mistakes
  • Mentioning only that carbon is reactive, without comparing it to iron.
  • Not using the idea of displacement.
  • Talking about melting instead of extraction by reaction.

Question 04.1 – Type of substance

1 mark
Question

Copper oxide is insoluble. What type of substance is copper oxide?

Model answer

Base

Explanation

Metal oxides such as copper oxide are bases. Because it is insoluble, it is not an alkali.

Common mistakes
  • Writing alkali instead of base.
  • Thinking all oxides are acids.

Question 04.2 – Type of reaction

1 mark
Question

Copper oxide reacts with dilute hydrochloric acid to produce copper chloride and water. Tick the type of reaction taking place.

Model answer

Neutralisation

Explanation

This is an acid reacting with a base. Acid + base produces salt + water, which is a neutralisation reaction.

Common mistakes
  • Choosing decomposition because there are several substances involved.
  • Choosing combustion because oxygen is present in copper oxide.

Question 04.3 – Balanced symbol equation

2 marks
Question

Write a balanced symbol equation for the reaction between CuO and HCl.

Model answer

CuO + 2HCl → CuCl2 + H2O

Explanation

First write the correct formulas for each substance. Then balance the equation by placing a 2 in front of HCl so the number of hydrogen and chlorine atoms is the same on both sides.

Common mistakes
  • Writing CuCl instead of CuCl2.
  • Forgetting the 2 in front of HCl.
  • Changing small formula numbers instead of using balancing numbers.

Question 04.4 – Making pure dry crystals of copper chloride

6 marks
Question

Describe how you could produce pure dry crystals of copper chloride from copper oxide and dilute hydrochloric acid.

Model answer

Add dilute hydrochloric acid to a beaker and gently warm it. Add copper oxide and stir. Keep adding copper oxide until no more reacts. Filter the mixture to remove the excess copper oxide. Pour the filtrate into an evaporating basin and heat gently to evaporate some of the water. Then leave the solution to cool so crystals form. Finally, remove the crystals and allow them to dry.

Explanation

Why excess copper oxide is added: this makes sure all of the acid reacts.

Why filtering is needed: it removes the unreacted solid copper oxide.

Why the solution is heated: to evaporate some water and make the solution more concentrated.

Why it is cooled: crystals form from the concentrated solution as it cools.

This is the standard method for preparing a soluble salt from an insoluble base.

Common mistakes
  • Not adding excess copper oxide.
  • Missing out the filtering step.
  • Evaporating all the water away instead of allowing crystals to form.
  • Not mentioning drying the crystals at the end.
