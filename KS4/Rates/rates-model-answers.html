Question 1 5 marks Collision theory basics

Sulfur dioxide and oxygen

This question checks key foundation ideas: word equations, compounds, collisions, activation energy and ways to change the rate.

1a(i) Complete the word equation.

Model answer

oxygen, sulfur trioxide

What the examiner wanted

Both missing words had to be correct to get the mark. One correct word on its own would not be enough here.

1a(ii) Is sulfur dioxide an element, a mixture or a compound?

Model answer

Compound

Why?

SO2 contains sulfur and oxygen chemically joined together, so it is a compound.

1b Complete the sentence about pressure and collisions.

Model answer

The frequency of the collisions increases.

Common mistake

Answers such as “there are more particles” were too vague on their own. The mark was for the idea that collisions happen more often.

1c What is the minimum energy needed for particles to react called?

Model answer

Activation energy

1d Give one way to increase the rate other than changing pressure.

Model answer

Add a catalyst.

Another acceptable answer was to increase the temperature.

Question 2 6 marks Sodium thiosulfate practical

Concentration and rate

This question focused on graph skills, practical errors and explaining why a higher concentration gives a faster reaction.

2a(i) Draw a line of best fit.

Model answer

A continuous straight line should be drawn through the trend of the data, ignoring the anomalous point.

Key idea

The mark was for showing the overall trend, not forcing the line through every point.

2a(ii) Suggest two reasons why the points do not all lie on the line of best fit.

Model answer

  1. There may have been timing errors, such as not starting or stopping the stopwatch at exactly the right moment.
  2. There may have been measurement errors, such as judging the point when the cross disappeared differently each time.

Other acceptable ideas included temperature fluctuations, an anomalous result, plotting errors, recording errors or calculation errors.

Where students lose marks

“Human error” can be accepted, but only if it is clear enough. Better answers give a specific example of the error.

2b(i) How does the graph support the idea that rate is directly proportional to concentration?

Model answer

The graph shows a straight-line relationship, so as concentration increases, the rate increases in direct proportion.

Simple version that would score

“As concentration increases, the rate goes up” would get the mark.

2b(ii) Explain, in terms of particles, why the rate increases when concentration increases.

Model answer

When the concentration is higher, there are more reacting particles in the same volume of solution. This means the particles are closer together and collide more often, so there are more successful collisions each second and the reaction is faster.

What the mark scheme was looking for

More particles in the same volume.
Particles are closer together.
More collisions or more frequent collisions.

Common mistake

Talking about particles having more energy is not the main point here. This question was about concentration, not temperature.

Question 3 5 marks Rate calculations

Hydrogen from magnesium and acid

This question tested graph/table reading, average rate calculations and explaining why rate changes during a reaction.

3a(i) Calculate the average rate between 30 s and 50 s.

Model answer

At 30 s the volume is 130 cm3. At 50 s the volume is 175 cm3.

Change in volume = 175 − 130 = 45 cm3

Time = 20 s

Average rate = 45 ÷ 20 = 2.25 cm3/s

Rate = 2.25 cm3/s

How the marks were awarded

  • 1 mark for using the correct readings: 130 and 175
  • 1 mark for dividing by 20
  • 1 mark for the correct answer of 2.25

3a(ii) Explain why this rate is different from the rate in the first 10 seconds.

Model answer

The rate is slower between 30 and 50 seconds because the concentration of the reactants is lower than it was at the start. As the reaction continues, reactant particles are used up, so there are fewer collisions each second.

To get both marks

Say that the concentration of the reactant or reactants is lower.
Link this to fewer collisions per second.
Question 4 13 marks Required practical ideas

Factors affecting rate

This longer question tested planning a method, explaining the effect of temperature, defining a catalyst and naming biological catalysts.

4a Describe a method to investigate the effect of particle size on the rate of reaction of calcium carbonate with hydrochloric acid.

Model answer

  1. Measure a fixed volume of hydrochloric acid using a measuring cylinder.
  2. Put the acid into a conical flask connected to a gas syringe with a stopper and delivery tube.
  3. Measure a fixed mass of calcium carbonate using a balance.
  4. Add the calcium carbonate to the flask, quickly fit the stopper and start the timer.
  5. Record the volume of gas collected at regular time intervals, or measure how long it takes to collect a fixed volume of gas.
  6. Repeat the experiment using different sized pieces of calcium carbonate.
  7. Keep the mass of calcium carbonate the same each time.
  8. Keep the volume, concentration and temperature of the hydrochloric acid the same each time.
  9. Repeat each test and calculate a mean to improve reliability.

What a top-level answer needed

A strong answer had to be logical, detailed and valid. It needed both the practical method and the control variables.

How marks were often lost

  • Not saying how the rate would actually be measured.
  • Forgetting the gas syringe, stopper or delivery tube.
  • Not controlling mass, acid volume, acid concentration or temperature.
  • Mentioning different particle sizes but not explaining how the experiment would be repeated fairly.

4b Explain the effect of increasing temperature on the rate of reaction.

Model answer

Increasing the temperature increases the rate of reaction because the particles have more energy and move faster. This means collisions happen more often, and a greater proportion of collisions have enough energy to overcome the activation energy, so more collisions are successful.

Key marking points

The rate increases.
Particles have more energy or move faster.
Collision frequency increases and/or more collisions are successful.

4c What is meant by a catalyst?

Model answer

A catalyst is a substance that increases the rate of a reaction and is not used up during the reaction.

Common mistake

Only saying “it speeds up a reaction” is not enough for full marks. You also needed the idea that it is not used up.

4d Explain how a catalyst works.

Model answer

A catalyst works by providing an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy. This means more collisions are successful each second, so the reaction happens faster.

Important note

The mark scheme extract mainly showed the one-mark answer for biological catalysts in part (d), but this explanation is the standard GCSE explanation students should use when asked how a catalyst works.

4e What are catalysts in biological systems called?

Model answer

Enzymes
