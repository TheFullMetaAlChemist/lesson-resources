Rates of Reaction – Model Answers & Follow-up

Jump to a question:

Q1(a)(i) • Complete the word equation

Spec: 4.1.1.1 AO1 1 mark
Model answer
sulfur dioxide + oxygen → sulfur trioxide
Explanation / working
This is the word equation for oxidation of sulfur dioxide (SO₂) to sulfur trioxide (SO₃): list both reactants and the product in words.
Common errors & misconceptions
• Writing a symbol equation when the question asks for words.
• Writing “sulfur oxide” instead of sulfur trioxide.
• Missing oxygen as a reactant.

Q1(a)(ii) • Classification of SO₂

Spec: 4.1.1.1 AO1 1 mark
Model answer
compound
Explanation / working
SO₂ contains the elements sulfur and oxygen chemically bonded in fixed proportions, so it is a compound.
Common errors & misconceptions
Choosing “element” because the name sounds singular; choosing “mixture” (mixtures aren’t chemically bonded).

Q1(b) • Effect of increasing pressure (gases)

Spec: 4.6.1.3 AO1 1 mark
Model answer
It increases the frequency of collisions.
Explanation / working
Higher pressure pushes gas particles closer together so collisions happen more often.
Common errors & misconceptions
Saying pressure makes particles “more energetic” (it mainly changes collision frequency, not particle energy).

Q1(c) • Minimum energy to react

Spec: 4.6.1.3 AO1 1 mark
Model answer
activation energy
Explanation / working
Activation energy (Eₐ) is the minimum energy colliding particles must have for a reaction to occur.
Common errors & misconceptions
Writing “catalyst” or “rate” instead of the energy term.

Q1(d) • One way to increase the rate (not pressure)

Spec: 4.6.1.2 AO1 1 mark
Model answer
Use a catalyst or increase the temperature.
Explanation / working
Catalyst lowers Eₐ (more successful collisions). Higher temperature increases particle kinetic energy and collision frequency.
Common errors & misconceptions
“Add more reactant” (must be linked to concentration); saying “increase pressure” when reactants aren’t gases.

Q2(a)(i) • Line of best fit

Spec: 4.6.1.1 AO3 1 mark
Model answer
Draw one straight line that follows the trend and misses the anomalous point.
Explanation / working
For a linear relationship, a straight best-fit summarises the trend; clear outliers shouldn’t control the line.
Common errors & misconceptions
Dot-to-dot; forcing the line through an outlier; drawing a curve when the data trend is linear.

Q2(a)(ii) • Why don’t all points lie on the line?

Spec: 4.6.1.1 AO3 2 marks
Model answer
Any two sensible reasons: timing judgement; deciding when the cross disappears; reading/measurement error; temperature fluctuations; recording/plotting mistakes; apparatus limitations; an anomalous result.
Explanation / working
Rate experiments are affected by random and measurement errors; small differences lead to scatter around the trend.
Common errors & misconceptions
Vague answers like “it went wrong” without naming a specific error source.

Q2(b)(i) • Evidence for direct proportionality

Spec: 4.6.1.1 AO3 1 mark
Model answer
A straight line (approximately) that passes through the origin shows rate ∝ concentration.
Explanation / working
Doubling concentration doubles rate if the relationship is proportional; the graph test is a straight line through (0,0).
Common errors & misconceptions
Saying only “it increases” without mentioning straight line and origin.

Q2(b)(ii) • Why concentration increases rate

Spec: 4.6.1.3 AO2 2 marks
Model answer
More particles per unit volume ⇒ more frequent collisions and therefore more successful collisions each second.
Explanation / working
Collision theory: increasing concentration raises collision frequency; the number of effective collisions per second increases.
Common errors & misconceptions
Claiming particles are more energetic (concentration doesn’t change energy); confusing with pressure.

Q3(a)(i) • Calculate mean rate (30–50 s)

Spec: 4.6.1.1 AO2 3 marks
Model answer
2.25 cm³ s⁻¹
Explanation / working
Read volumes at 30 s and 50 s (175 and 130 cm³). ΔV = 45 cm³ over 20 s ⇒ 45 ÷ 20 = 2.25 cm³ s⁻¹.
Common errors & misconceptions
Wrong times; dividing by 50 instead of 20; missing units.

Q3(a)(ii) • Why is the later rate lower?

Spec: 4.6.1.3 AO2 2 marks
Model answer
Reactant concentration falls as it’s used up ⇒ fewer collisions per second ⇒ lower mean rate.
Explanation / working
Fewer particles in a given volume means reduced collision frequency and fewer effective collisions.
Common errors & misconceptions
Non-scientific statements (e.g., “reaction gets tired”).

Q3(b) • Why continuous processing can be more efficient

Spec: 4.6.1.4 AO2 1 mark
Model answer
Lower labour/operating costs (e.g., lower enzyme costs) and avoids stop–start.
Explanation / working
Continuous plants run steadily, improving resource use and output per worker.
Common errors & misconceptions
Answering only “it’s faster” without any cost/efficiency idea.

Q4(a) • Method: particle size vs rate (CaCO₃ + HCl)

Spec: 4.6.1.2 AO3 6 marks
Model answer
  • Independent: size of CaCO₃ particles (large chips → small chips → powder). Keep the mass the same each time (e.g. 5.00 g).
  • Dependent: rate of gas produced (cm³ s⁻¹)
  • Control: volume and concentration of acid (e.g. 50 cm³ of 1.0 mol dm⁻³), temperature (keep ~25 °C or use a water bath), same mass of solid, same conical flask/apparatus, quick and airtight bung, same measuring times.

Method — short, clear steps

  1. Measure 50 cm³ of 1.0 mol dm⁻³ HCl into a conical flask using a measuring cylinder.
  2. Weigh 5.00 g of CaCO₃ (large chips). Prepare the same mass for small chips and for powder.
  3. Add the CaCO₃ to the acid, immediately put in the bung, and start the stopwatch.
  4. Record the gas volume every 10–15 s for about 2 minutes (or until readings stop changing).
  5. Rinse the flask; use fresh acid each time. Repeat steps 1–5 for small chips and then powder.
  6. Repeat each size (at least twice) and work out the mean values.
Explanation / working
Only the independent variable (particle size) changes; all other factors are controlled. Repeats and means improve reliability. The initial gradient of volume–time graphs compares rates fairly because it is taken before reactant concentration changes much.
Common errors & misconceptions
• Changing both mass and size between trials.
• Leaks (loose bung), not replacing bung quickly → low volumes.
• Reusing the same acid (concentration falls).
• Not controlling temperature / total volume.
• Writing “collect gas” without how (apparatus + timings).
• Comparing final volumes only instead of rate/gradient.

Q4(b) • Explain the effect of temperature on rate

Spec: 4.6.1.3 AO2 3 marks
Model answer
Higher temperature → particles move faster and a bigger fraction has E ≥ Eₐ → more frequent and more energetic effective collisions → faster rate.
Explanation / working
Temperature raises mean kinetic energy; both collision frequency and the fraction of successful collisions increase.
Common errors & misconceptions
Saying “collisions are stronger” without linking to activation energy; confusing temperature with concentration.

Q4(c) • What is a catalyst?

Spec: 4.6.1.4 AO1 2 marks
Model answer
A substance that increases the rate of reaction and is not used up.
Explanation / working
Definition requires two parts: effect on rate + not consumed overall.
Common errors & misconceptions
Missing one part; saying a catalyst adds energy to particles.

Q4(d) • How a catalyst works

Spec: 4.6.1.4 AO2 2 marks
Model answer
Provides an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy, so a greater fraction of collisions is successful at the same temperature.
Explanation / working
On a reaction profile, the peak is lower with a catalyst; therefore the rate increases.
Common errors & misconceptions
Claiming a catalyst increases the number of collisions (its main role is reducing Eₐ).

Q4(e) • Biological catalysts

Spec: 4.6.1.4 AO1 1 mark
Model answer
enzymes
Explanation / working
Enzymes are catalysts in biological systems.
Common errors & misconceptions
Answering “proteins” or “hormones” without linking to catalysis.

Follow-up • Spec 4.1.1.1

How do you know that SO₂ is a compound rather than an element or mixture — and what word equation shows how SO₃ is made?

Things to Think About
• Compounds are two or more elements chemically bonded in fixed ratios.
• Mixtures are not chemically bonded; composition can vary.
• Word-equation format: reactant + reactant → product.
Model Answer
SO₂ is a compound because it contains sulfur and oxygen chemically bonded. Word equation: sulfur dioxide + oxygen → sulfur trioxide.
Explanation
A formula with more than one element symbol indicates a compound. Oxidising sulfur dioxide by adding oxygen forms sulfur trioxide.

Follow-up • Spec 4.6.1.1

How would you show that the rate is directly proportional to concentration and make your data more reliable?

Things to Think About
• What shows proportionality on a graph?
• Which calculations (mean rate, gradient) help?
• Repeats, anomalies, and control variables.
Model Answer
Plot rate vs concentration: a straight line through (0,0) shows proportionality. Improve reliability by repeating runs, averaging, identifying anomalies, and keeping temperature/total volume constant.
Explanation
A constant gradient and line through the origin indicate direct proportionality. Reliability comes from repeatability and controlled variables.

Follow-up • Spec 4.6.1.2

If a reaction is too slow, which single factor would you change first and which two controls would you keep the same to make a fair test?

Things to Think About
• Choose one factor (concentration, temperature, surface area, or catalyst) and justify with particle ideas.
• Name two control variables to hold constant.
Model Answer
Increase the temperature first (often most effective) because it increases particle kinetic energy and the frequency of effective collisions. Keep, for example, concentration/volume of acid and the mass of solid the same.
Explanation
Only one factor should change so any rate difference can be attributed to that factor. Controls prevent confounding.

Follow-up • Spec 4.6.1.3

As a reaction proceeds, why does the mean rate usually decrease? Explain using collision theory.

Things to Think About
• What happens to reactant concentration with time?
• How do collision frequency and the fraction with E ≥ Eₐ change?
Model Answer
As reactants are used up, their concentration falls, so collisions are less frequent and fewer are successful each second — the rate falls.
Explanation
Rate depends on the number of effective collisions per second. Lower concentration → fewer meetings → lower rate.

Follow-up • Spec 4.6.1.4

How does a catalyst change the reaction profile, and why does that make the reaction faster?

Things to Think About
• What happens to the activation energy on the profile?
• Does the catalyst get used up?
• Link Eₐ to the number of successful collisions.
Model Answer
A catalyst provides an alternative pathway with a lower Eₐ (lower peak). At the same temperature a larger fraction of particles has enough energy, so the reaction is faster. The catalyst is not used up.
Explanation
Lowering Eₐ increases the proportion of collisions that are successful per second (Maxwell–Boltzmann idea).
