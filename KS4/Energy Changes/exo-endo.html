Exothermic & Endothermic Reactions

A low-stakes digital version of your worksheet. Have a go here first, get instant feedback, then complete your paper copy with more confidence.

Exothermic

Energy is released

Surroundings get warmer 🔥

An exothermic reaction transfers energy to the surroundings. This usually makes the temperature increase.

Examples: combustion, neutralisation, hand warmers, self-heating cans.

Endothermic

Energy is taken in

Surroundings get colder ❄️

An endothermic reaction takes energy in from the surroundings. This usually makes the temperature decrease.

Examples: thermal decomposition, sports injury cold packs, citric acid and sodium bicarbonate.

Key idea

Energy cannot be created or destroyed

In chemical reactions, energy is transferred. It either moves from the reaction to the surroundings or from the surroundings into the reaction.

Memory trick: Exo sounds like exit. In an exothermic reaction, energy exits the reaction and goes into the surroundings.

Activation energy

The activation energy is the minimum energy particles need before they can react. Think of it like the first push needed to get a reaction started.

Imagine pushing a bike up a small hill before it can roll down the other side. The energy needed to get up the hill is like activation energy.

Worksheet Task 4

Draw It Yourself: Reaction Profiles

Use these models to help you draw your own profiles on the worksheet. Remember to label reactants, products, activation energy and energy released or absorbed.

Exothermic reaction profile

In an exothermic profile, the reactants are higher than the products because energy is released.

  1. Draw the reactants higher than the products.
  2. Draw a curved hump between them.
  3. Label the hump as activation energy.
  4. Add an arrow downwards for energy released.

Endothermic reaction profile

In an endothermic profile, the products are higher than the reactants because energy is absorbed.

  1. Draw the reactants lower than the products.
  2. Draw a curved hump between them.
  3. Label the hump as activation energy.
  4. Add an arrow upwards for energy absorbed.
