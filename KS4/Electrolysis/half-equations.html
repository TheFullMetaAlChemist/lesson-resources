Introduction to Electrolysis

Electrolysis is the process of splitting ionic compounds into their elements using electricity. During this process:

  • Oxidation (loss of electrons) occurs at the anode (+)
  • Reduction (gain of electrons) occurs at the cathode (–)

Half-equations show these processes clearly, allowing us to see exactly what happens at each electrode.

Key Points to Remember

  • The cathode (–) is negatively charged and attracts positive ions (cations)
  • The anode (+) is positively charged and attracts negative ions (anions)
  • At the cathode (–), ions gain electrons (reduction)
  • At the anode (+), ions lose electrons (oxidation)

OIL RIG

Remember the mnemonic: OIL RIG

Oxidation Is Loss of electrons | Reduction Is Gain of electrons

Why Write Half-Equations?

Half-equations are important because they:

  • Show exactly what is happening at each electrode
  • Indicate which elements are being produced
  • Demonstrate the flow of electrons
  • Help balance the overall equation for the electrolysis process

Writing Half-Equations for Molten Compounds

In molten compounds, the ions are free to move but there's no water present. This means:

At the cathode (–):

Positive ions (cations) gain electrons and are reduced to form their elements.

General equation: Xn+ + ne− → X

At the anode (+):

Negative ions (anions) lose electrons and are oxidized to form their elements.

General equation: nY− → (n/2)Y2 + ne−

Worked Example: Molten Sodium Chloride (NaCl)

In molten NaCl, we have Na+ and Cl− ions.

At the cathode (–):

Sodium ions gain electrons: Na+ + e− → Na

At the anode (+):

Chloride ions lose electrons: 2Cl− → Cl2 + 2e−

Practice Questions: Molten Compounds

At the cathode (–):

1. Write the half-equation for the cathode reaction in molten potassium chloride (KCl).

K+ + e- → K
Think about which ion is positive in KCl. At the cathode, positive ions gain electrons.

2. Write the half-equation for the cathode reaction in molten calcium oxide (CaO).

Ca2+ + e- → Ca
In CaO, calcium forms Ca²⁺ ions. Remember that the charge determines how many electrons are needed.

3. Write the half-equation for the cathode reaction in molten aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃).

Al3+ + e- → Al
Aluminium forms Al³⁺ ions. The number of electrons needed equals the charge on the ion.

4. Write the half-equation for the cathode reaction in molten lithium fluoride (LiF).

Li+ + e- → Li
Lithium forms Li⁺ ions. At the cathode, these positive ions gain electrons.

5. Write the half-equation for the cathode reaction in molten magnesium bromide (MgBr₂).

Mg2+ + e- → Mg
Magnesium forms Mg²⁺ ions. The charge tells you how many electrons are needed.

At the anode (+):

1. Write the half-equation for the anode reaction in molten potassium iodide (KI).

I- → I2 + e-
Iodide ions (I⁻) are negative and lose electrons at the anode. Remember that halide ions (like I⁻) form diatomic molecules (I₂).

2. Write the half-equation for the anode reaction in molten sodium bromide (NaBr).

Br- → Br2 + e-
Bromide ions (Br⁻) lose electrons at the anode. Remember that halides form diatomic molecules (Br₂).

3. Write the half-equation for the anode reaction in molten zinc chloride (ZnCl₂).

Cl- → Cl2 + e-
Chloride ions (Cl⁻) lose electrons at the anode to form chlorine gas (Cl₂).

4. Write the half-equation for the anode reaction in molten copper(II) oxide (CuO).

O2- → O2 + e-
Oxide ions (O²⁻) lose electrons at the anode to form oxygen gas (O₂).

5. Write the half-equation for the anode reaction in molten iron(III) fluoride (FeF₃).

F- → F2 + e-
Fluoride ions (F⁻) lose electrons at the anode. Remember that halogens form diatomic molecules (F₂).

Writing Half-Equations for Aqueous Solutions

In aqueous solutions, water (H₂O) introduces additional ions and complications. The reactivity of ions determines which will discharge at each electrode.

At the cathode (–):

There is competition between metal ions and hydrogen ions (from water):

  • If metal ions are less reactive than hydrogen (e.g., Cu, Ag), they will discharge: Cu2+ + 2e− → Cu
  • If metal ions are more reactive than hydrogen (e.g., Na, K, Ca), hydrogen gas will form instead: 2H+ + 2e− → H2

At the anode (+):

There is competition between other negative ions and hydroxide ions (from water):

  • If halide ions (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) are present, they will discharge: 2Cl− → Cl2 + 2e−
  • If no halide ions are present, hydroxide ions discharge: 4OH− → O2 + 2H2O + 4e−

Reactivity Series for Metals

This simplified reactivity series will help you determine if a metal will discharge at the cathode:

Worked Example: Aqueous Sodium Chloride (NaCl)

In aqueous NaCl, we have Na+, Cl−, H+, and OH− ions.

At the cathode (–):

Sodium is more reactive than hydrogen, so hydrogen ions are discharged instead of sodium:

2H+ + 2e− → H2

At the anode (+):

Chloride ions are present (a halide), so they discharge:

2Cl− → Cl2 + 2e−

Summary: Writing Half-Equations for Electrolysis

Key Points to Remember

1. Electrodes

  • Cathode (–): Negative electrode where reduction occurs (gain of electrons)
  • Anode (+): Positive electrode where oxidation occurs (loss of electrons)

2. Molten Ionic Compounds

At the cathode: Metal ions gain electrons

General equation: Mn+ + ne− → M

At the anode: Non-metal ions lose electrons

General equation: 2X− → X2 + 2e− (for halides)

General equation: 2O2− → O2 + 4e− (for oxides)

3. Aqueous Solutions

At the cathode: Competition between metal ions and H+ from water

  • If metal is less reactive than hydrogen: Mn+ + ne− → M
  • If metal is more reactive than hydrogen: 2H+ + 2e− → H2

At the anode: Competition between anions and OH− from water

  • If halide ions are present: 2X− → X2 + 2e−
  • If no halide ions are present: 4OH− → O2 + 2H2O + 4e−

4. Steps for Writing Half-Equations

  1. Identify if the compound is molten or in aqueous solution
  2. Determine which electrode you're writing for (cathode or anode)
  3. For molten compounds: Identify the ions present and write equations accordingly
  4. For aqueous solutions: Consider reactivity and competition from water
  5. Balance the half-equation by ensuring charge and atoms are balanced on both sides

5. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Forgetting to include electrons in the half-equation
  • Putting electrons on the wrong side (they're gained at the cathode, lost at the anode)
  • Incorrect number of electrons in the equation
  • Not accounting for the state of the compound (molten vs. aqueous)
  • Overlooking the reactivity series when dealing with aqueous solutions
